## Abstract

SUMMARYDespite promising results with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy, outcomes of patients with brain metastasis (BrM) remain poor. Identifying determinant of resistance is crucial but has been hindered by limited access to patient samples and the scarcity of relevant preclinical models. Here, we developed two mouse melanoma BrM models that mirror the disparate response to ICB seen in patients. By characterizing their mutational landscape and performing single-cell phenotypic and transcriptomic analysis, we demonstrated that these models recapitulate both the cellular and molecular features of human disease. Through our comparative analysis, we identified key factors contributing to ICB response. In the responsive model, we found that tumor cells expressed inflammatory programs that polarized microglia toward reactive states, expressing high levels of MHC-I and -II and immunostimulatory molecules, eliciting robust T cell recruitment and activation, which favored anti-tumor immunity. Conversely, the resistant melanoma cells were characterized by neurological molecular signatures and expression of ligands that maintain microglia homeostatic states, resulting in poor T cell infiltration and therapy resistance. We validated our findings in BrM patients, showing that BR1 and BR3 signatures correlated with T cell infiltration and were associated with better and worse patient outcomes, respectively. Our study fills a critical gap by providing clinically relevant models and revealing mechanistic insights into BrM ICB responses, identifying potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets.

## INTRODUCTION

Cancer treatment has seen substantial improvement in the last decade. However, therapeutic options for patients with brain metastasis (BrM) are still limited and frequently ineffective, leading to a dismal prognosis1,2. Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) has shown promising results in clinical trials, reaching up to 55% response rates in asymptomatic melanoma BrM but 6-22% in symptomatic patients or with leptomeningeal disease3–7. While ICB combination or in association with radiotherapy, in particular stereotactic radiation therapy (SRT), can enhance efficacy, the use of steroids – often prescribed to BrM patients to reduced inflammation – has proven to be detrimental for ICB response6. Therefore, there is an urgent need to identify optimal combinations and regimens to apply in each clinical scenario. Moreover, we still lack a clear understanding of the specific tumor microenvironment (TME) responses in BrMs that could lead to the identification of biomarkers strongly correlating with the clinical benefit of ICB.

The brain tumor microenvironment (BrTME) is highly specialized, containing unique resident cell types including microglia that constitute the majority of its immune components, and non-immune cells, such as neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes8–10. These are combined with specific extracellular matrix (ECM) networks and neurotransmitters8–10, all of which contribute to brain homeostasis and distinguish the BrTME from non-brain TME. The BrTME is also controlled in part by the blood-brain barrier (BBB), which regulates the access and distribution of infiltrating immune cells, tumor cells, and drugs, to the brain8–12. Previous reports have shown clear differences in immune composition and tumor molecular profile between BrMs, extracranial metastases, and primary tumors in patients13–16. This raises the question of whether non-brain tumor determinants and mechanisms of ICB responses apply to BrM. Recent studies have expanded our knowledge regarding the diversity of the BrTME, demonstrating that heterogeneity in immune composition within the BrTME among patients is mainly driven by tumor types (e.g., primary brain tumor versus BrM) and subtypes (e.g., BrM tumor of origin such as melanoma, breast carcinoma, etc)17–22 rather than the uniqueness of the brain environment. These studies showed that the largest discrepancies among BrM subtypes were in the T cell and myeloid compartments17–22. Nevertheless, how tumor cells regulate the BrTME and its impact on ICB responses remain to be elucidated.

Important limitations of these retrospective studies include the relatively small cohorts, the diverse treatments patients received, and the rare availability of biopsies from ICB responder patients. Additionally, the constraints of sample collection result in heterogeneous representation of the stroma in BrM biopsies, including an under-representation of microglia. Modeling BrM in mice is extremely challenging and the majority of models show low penetrance and/or long latencies23,24. Few BrM models are currently available, including an autochthonous model23–26. Many require intracranial injection of melanoma cells27,28, which bypasses extravasation and artificially disrupts the BBB, thereby inducing inflammation. Altogether, these limitations preclude the comparative studies between responders and non-responders, thus preventing the identification of predictive biomarkers of ICB response and the discovery of new therapeutic targets.

The aim of this study was to address this unmet need by developing two syngeneic brain-metastatic models M4-BR1 (hereafter BR1) and M4-BR3 (BR3) derived from our previously established M4- B2905 (M4) melanoma mouse cell line29. We demonstrate that these models represent the molecular and cellular complexity and diversity of ICB responses observed in patients. Through comparative studies, we identify specific tumor molecular signatures associated with immune BrTME composition, ICB response or resistance, and patient outcomes. Therefore, this study provides a robust foundation for the necessary mechanistic studies to optimize BrM therapy.

## RESULTS

### Development of metastatic melanoma models with high brain penetrance

To study BrM therapeutic response to ICB, we generated a panel of syngeneic immunocompetent preclinical models preserving BBB integrity by intracardiac (i.c.) injection of the M4 melanoma mouse cell line29 into syngeneic C57BL/6 mice. We have previously demonstrated that this cell line, generated by neonatal UV-irradiation of hepatocyte growth factor transgenic mice (HgfTg), faithfully recapitulates the etiology, histopathology, and molecular features of human cutaneous melanoma29. After recovering metastatic cells from the brain, we cycled them in vivo to enhance their brain- metastatic potential (see Methods) (Figure S1A). We obtained two polyclonal sublines, BR1 and BR3, that exhibit high penetrance to the brain and form numerous pigmented macroscopic metastases in up to 100% of injected animals on average per experiment (Figure 1A-B). We confirmed the presence of melanoma cells in the brains of mice from both models by dopachrome tautomerase (DCT)30,31 immunostaining at day 18-20 post-implantation (model endpoint) (Figure S1B). To assess metastatic burden in an unbiased manner, we developed a machine learning approach that semi-automatically detects and counts metastatic foci on whole-brain stereomicroscope images32. Following optimization (see Methods), we achieved a comparable number of BrM per mouse between BR1 and BR3 (Figure 1C). However, BR3 formed more extracranial metastases and displayed a lower BrM/extracranial metastases ratio compared to BR1, indicating different metastatic tropism between the two models (Figures S1C-D, 1C). Nevertheless, in both BR1 and BR3, the highest incidence of metastases was observed in the brain (Figure S1D). Thus, we successfully developed two immunocompetent BrM models with high brain penetrance while preserving a physiological BBB, providing a valuable platform for the comparative and mechanistic studies of BrM therapeutic responses.

### BR1 and BR3 models exhibit mutational landscapes representative of human BrM melanomas

To confirm that our models are translationally relevant, we interrogated their molecular resemblance with human disease. We first characterized the mutational landscape of cultured BR1 and BR3 cell lines in comparison to the M4 parental line using whole exome sequencing analysis. We found an elevated tumor mutation burden in both brain-metastatic lines compared with the parental M4 cells (432, 403 and 327 genes in BR1, BR3 and M4 parental, respectively; Figure S1E). This is in agreement with observations from clinical data showing higher mutational burden in BrM compared to primary tumors14,33,34. Not surprisingly, 90% and 86% of the genes mutated in the M4 parental line were also detected in BR1 and BR3, respectively. However, each of the brain-metastatic models presented approximately 30% of unique mutated genes (Figure S1E), which may partly explain their distinct metastatic behavior in vivo. Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA) of the mutated genes in each model revealed significant enrichment of the PTEN and PI3K/AKT pathways, although the specific mutated genes varied between the models (Figure S1F and Table S1). We next explored the mutational landscape in metastatic patients using the publicly available MSK-MET-SKCM cohort35 and the cBioportal platform36–38. We found that most of the mutated genes identified in our models that are related with these pathways, were also frequently mutated in BrMs from melanoma patients (Figure S1G). In line with our observation, while no specific mutations unique to melanoma BrM have been identified so far, AKT1E17K increases BrM incidence in mice, and patients with mutations in PI3K/AKT pathway (e.g., PTEN) have a higher risk of developing central nervous system (CNS) metastases26,33,34. Overall, these results show that our models recapitulate, at least in part, the molecular complexity of human melanomas.

### Single-cell transcriptomic profiles of BR1 and BR3 BrMs show strong correlation with patient data sets

To further corroborate the molecular similarities between BrMs from our two preclinical models and melanoma patients, we performed single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNAseq) of whole brains from BR1- and BR3-bearing animals (Figure 1D, see Methods). After annotating CNS stromal, tumor, and immune cells (Figure 1D, see Methods), we focused on melanoma cells for downstream analysis. We then applied a Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)39 approach to identify the key molecular programs (referred to as metaprograms [MPs]) that characterize our models in vivo (see Fig S1H and Methods). NMF-based methods are able to identify gene expression patterns that highlight differences within tumors, as well as variations between different tumors40–42. A similar approach has been successfully used in BrM patients to discover tumor MPs13. Importantly, NMF allowed us to compare the features of our models with available clinical data. We identified seven different MPs (MP1-MP7) and characterized them using Metascape pathway analysis43 (Figure 1E and Table S2, see Methods). MP1 and MP4 were enriched in pathways and genes previously found to be expressed in pan-cancer and melanoma BrM patients, such as cell cycle (MP1), and immune response and antigen presentation (MP4)13,20 (Figure 1E and Table S2, see methods). Other MPs showed enrichment for melanin biosynthesis (MP2, MP7), translation and mitochondrial metabolism (MP5), and neuro-functions such as axon guidance and neurodevelopment (MP3, MP6), which have been recently shown to be increased in BrM compared to primary tumors or extra-BrM metastases in pan-cancer and melanoma patients13–15,20 (Figure 1E and Table S2, see Methods). To validate that our mouse BrM MPs are representative of human disease, we computed correlations between these mouse-MP and human-MP from three patient cohorts Biermann et. al13, Smalley et al.44, and Alvarez-Breckenridge et al.45 (Figure 1F-G, see Methods). Each mouse-MP identified in our study correlated with one or more patient-MP from at least one cohort (Figure 1F). For instance, mouse MP4 enriched for antigen presentation pathways mapped to the human MPs 1 and 2 characterized by similar pathways13. In all three patient cohorts, human-MP1 containing translation control pathways (e.g., MYC targets and mTORC1 signaling13), strongly correlated with mouse-MP5 (Figure 1F). Similarly, human-MP7, enriched in melanocytic genes and neuronal development and function positively correlated with mouse MPs involved in melanin production (MP2 and MP7) and neurodevelopment (MP6 and MP3) pathways (Figure 1F). Importantly, we verified that the correlations found between pairs of human- and mouse-MPs were consistent across all melanoma BrM human datasets13,44,45 (Figure 1G). Overall, these results underscore the robustness, reliability, and clinical relevance of our murine brain-metastatic models, demonstrating their translational potential.

### BR1 and BR3 BrM recapitulate the diversity of responses to ICB observed in the clinic

To evaluate the ICB therapeutic response of the models, we treated syngeneic mice that were i.c.- injected with either BR1 or BR3 using anti-CTLA-4 or anti-PD-L1 monotherapies, or combination therapy (combo), dual isotype or vehicle controls (Figure 2A). We started dosing at day 3 or 4 with each antibody, after verifying metastatic brain seeding on day 4 through microscopy, DCT immunostaining, and ex vivo culture of cells from the implanted brains (Figure S2 A-D). When control groups reached the endpoint, we assessed BrM burden using our machine-learning algorithm32. We observed striking response differences between the two models (Figure 2B-E). We found a significant reduction in total metastatic foci count and area in BR1 following treatment with both mono- and combo-therapies. Combo therapy was the most effective regimen, also reducing the size of individual metastases (Figure 2D). In addition, we observed significantly fewer extracranial metastases in this model, which disappeared in half of the monotherapy-treated and more than 90% of the combo-treated animals (Figure S2E). In marked contrast, BR3 BrM were resistant to ICB, showing no changes in the total metastatic area, BrM foci count, or individual lesion size in any therapeutic group (Figure 2E). Notably, we observed a modest decrease in total extracranial metastasis counts in BR3-bearing animals treated with ICB monotherapies, and a significant response with combo therapy (Figure S2F). These results align with clinical studies indicating that extracranial and BrM responses to ICB do not always correlate with each other3.

### ICB-sensitive and resistant models display distinct BrTME and remodeling upon treatment

Because we saw striking differences in the response to ICB between the two models, we interrogated the BrTME to identify potential immune correlates of BrM ICB response. We performed multiparametric spectral flow cytometry on brain leukocytes from both combo-treated, which yielded the strongest response, and untreated BR1 and BR3 models. Consistent with their ICB responsiveness, we observed a significantly higher number of total leukocytes in BR1 compared to BR3, independent of their treatment status (Figure 3A). To gain more granularity, we performed unsupervised clustering of immune cells followed by supervised cell annotation (Figure 3B, S3A-B; Table S3). Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) visualization and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) using the frequency of the identified leukocyte populations revealed different BrTME compositions between the two models in both treated and untreated groups (Figure 3C-D).

To determine the specific immune populations driving the differences between untreated BR1 and BR3, we analyzed the PC1 loadings, which segregate these two groups (Figure 3D). These loadings revealed neutrophils, microglia, and Ly6Clo monocytes as enriched in BR3 samples, which indeed had a higher proportion of these cells (Figure 3D-E and S3C-E). Both the proportion of microglia (> 90% of total leukocytes) and the infiltrating cell populations in the BR3 ICB-resistant BrTME resembled that of non-tumor-bearing brains (PBS) (Figure 3E and S3F). In contrast to BR3, BR1 showed a higher frequency and number of various infiltrating cells, including natural killer (NK) cells, DCs, and T cells, which are known to favor ICB responses46,47 (Figure 3E-H and S3G-H). This indicates a stronger immune cell recruitment in untreated BR1 BrM compared to untreated BR3.

We observed a therapy-induced increase of total leukocytes along with an enrichment of T-cells in both models (Figure 3A, 3E-H, S3G-H, 3J). This aligns with recently published clinical studies showing ICB-driven increase T cell infiltration21,44,48. Nevertheless, TME dissimilarities between BR1 and BR3 were still observed after combo therapy, as evidenced by their segregation along PC2 (Figure 3D, S3D, S3I). BR1 continued to display higher frequency and number of infiltrating cells compared to BR3, which after combo therapy was specifically due to a surge in CD8+ T cells (Figure 3C-H, S3D- E, S3D, S3G-J). Additionally, a higher number of microglia was observed in treated BR1 compared to treated BR3 (Figure 3F). Altogether, these observations indicate BrTME immune compositional differences between ICB-sensitive and -resistant BrMs, with some differences, such as those in T cells and microglia, persisting after therapy.

### A distinct T cell landscape is associated with ICB response

The quantity and characteristics of infiltrating T cells play an important role in the response to ICB49–52. Therefore, we studied the specific T-cell characteristics associated with ICB-responses in our BrM models using our whole-brain scRNA-seq data from PBS, BR1-, and BR3-untreated samples (Figures S1D, S3K-L, 4A-E, and S4A-D). We performed differential gene expression analysis of total CD8 T cells between untreated ICB-sensitive BR1 and the resistant BR3. This analysis revealed a higher expression of activation/effector function-associated genes (Nkg7, Pdcd1, Ifng, Gzmb) in BR1, whereas the BR3 CD8 T cell pool displayed a naïve signature (Sell, Lef1, Tcf7, Foxp1) (Figure 4A). UMAP projection and unsupervised clustering of the T cells revealed six CD8 T cell clusters (CD8.sc1- 6) and six CD4 T cell clusters (CD4.sc1-6) with different abundance between the two models (Figure 4B-C, S4A). Compared to BR1, BR3 was enriched in T cell subsets defined by a naïve gene signature (CD8.sc1, CD4.sc1) (Figures 4B-D, S4A-C, Tables S4-5). Projection of our T cell clusters onto a single-cell reference atlas for T cell states49 confirmed that CD8.sc1 and CD4.sc1 correspond to the naïve compartments (Figures 4E, S4D). These clusters account for over 60% of the total CD8+ and CD4+ T cell populations in BR3 and PBS control, whereas they make up only 5-20% of T cells in BR1 (Figures 4C, S4A). BR3 also presented a higher proportion of cluster CD8.sc3, which mapped mostly onto CD8+ effector memory T cells on the reference atlas (Figures 4B-E, S4B, Table S4).

In contrast to BR3, BR1 was characterized by a higher diversity of T cells, including various CD8+ and CD4+ T cell subsets expressing different levels of genes encoding for T cell degranulation/lysosomal activity (Lamp1, Ctsl, Cd63; CD8.sc2), activation/dysfunction (Cd81, Ckb, Nr3c1; CD8.sc2)53,54, T cell regulatory function (Foxp3, Il2ra, Ikzf2; CD4.sc5-6), and immune checkpoint receptors previously described as activation and/or exhaustion markers (Lag3, Pdcd1, Havcr2; CD8.sc4-6 and CD4.sc3-4)49,52,53 (Figures 4B-E, S4A-C, Tables S4-5). Among the latter, CD8.sc5, the most enriched cluster in BR1, displayed the highest level of genes encoding for cytotoxic molecules (e.g., Ifng, Prf1, Gzmb) (Figures 4B-D, S4B, Table S4). Similarly, the BR1-enriched CD4- sc3 exhibited the highest expression of Ifng and Tbx21 (T-bet), consistent with a Th1-like signature (Figures S4A, S4C-D, 4E, Table S4). Notably, CD8.sc4, exclusively found in the BR1 BrTME, expressed the transcription factor TCF-1 (Tcf-7), the nuclear protein TOX (Tox), and low level of genes encoding for cytotoxic cytokines and immune checkpoint receptors (except Havcr2) (Figures 4B-E, S4A-C, Tables S4-5). This expression profile resembles that of the so-called “exhausted stem cell-like CD8 T cells”, previously identified as essential for ICB response and associated with better clinical outcomes in melanoma patients50,52 (Figures 4D-E, Table S4). Finally, the BR1 enriched clusters CD8.sc6 and CD4.sc6 displayed a proliferative profile (Mki67, Cdk1, Ccnb2), suggesting that local expansion contributes to the higher T cell number observed in this model (Figures 4B-D, S4A- C, Tables S4-5). We confirmed these T cell compositional differences between untreated BR1 and BR3 BrTME by spectral flow cytometry and showed higher number of activated T cells in BR1 (Figures 4F-J, S4E-G).

To determine if the T cell compositional differences between the two models are sustained after treatment, we analyzed the BrTMEof combo-treated BR1 and BR3 (Figures 4F-H, S4E-F). While all CD8+ and CD4+ T cell subsets slightly increased in BR3 after therapy, BR1 showed a specific 20-fold increase in CD8.F5 and a 10-fold increase in CD8.F4, both subsets marked by activation. Interestingly, CD8.F5 resembles tissue-resident memory T cells (TRM), which have been associated with better ICB efficacy and improved patient outcomes for non-brain tumors55–57 (Figure 4H). In addition, combo therapy in BR1 led to a significant increase in the CD4+ T cell subset CD4.F5 displaying activation/exhaustion markers (Figures 4H, 4J, S4F, S4G). Together, these results demonstrate that the quantity and the quality of the T cells differ between the ICB-responder and resistant BrM models, highlighting the importance of the intrinsic BrTME T cell landscape in ICB response and aligning with immune correlates of response described for non-brain tumors50–52.

### Unique reactive microglia signatures are found in ICB-responder BrM

Microglia, the predominant immune cell type in the CNS, are crucial in regulating brain homeostasis as well as response to tissue injury and pathogens58–60. While different studies have suggested their dual role in BrM development10,61,62, their contribution to ICB response is unclear. As expected, microglia represented 80-95% of the immune BrTME in both of our models. We then inquired whether different microglia transcriptional states might be associated with BrM response to ICB. To do so, we turned to our whole-brain scRNA-seq dataset from untreated samples and extracted microglia for further analysis (Figure S3K-L). Differential gene expression and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) between BR1 and BR3 showed enrichment of homeostatic microglia genes and pathways such as neuronal development and synaptic remodeling58–60 in BR3 (Figures 5A-B, S5A). In contrast, BR1 microglia upregulated genes and pathways associated with MHC class I and class II antigen presentation (MHC-I and MHC-II) (H2-Aa, Cd74, B2m), response to type I and type II interferons (IFN-I and IFN-II) (Ifitm3, Ifi27l2a, Cxcl10), and inflammatory proteins including T-cell attracting chemokines (Cxcl10, Cxcl16, Cxcl9) (Figures 5A-B), indicating a reactive microglia transcriptional signature in the responder model. We found similar pathways were enriched in BR1 relative to PBS, while, as expected, PBS samples were enriched in pathways associated with microglia homeostatic function (Figure S5B). Our analysis only revealed TNFα signaling via NF-κB enriched in BR3 microglia compared to PBS (Figure S5B).

Microglia are heterogeneous, particularly in disease conditions58–60. To investigate microglia heterogeneity in our BrM models, we performed unsupervised clustering and identified thirteen different microglia clusters (Figure 5D). Clusters Mg1-4 and Mg8, characterized by the highest level of homeostatic microglia genes and pathways (e.g., phagocytosis, regulation of cellular response to TGFβ, and neuronal function), represented the predominant microglia states in BR3 and PBS (Figure 5C-E, S5C). Clusters Mg5-6, also highly abundant in these groups, were enriched in pathways related to ribosomal and mitochondrial function, indicating a transcriptionally active state (Figure 5E). On the other hand, the overall reactive signature of BR1 microglia was driven by the combination of five different clusters, Mg9-Mg13, defined by reduced expression of homeostatic genes and pathways (Mg9-11 and Mg13), upregulation of IFN response genes (Mg12-13), antigen presentation (Mg9, Mg12-13), and pathways involved in T cell recruitment and regulation of adaptive immune responses (Mg12-13) (Figure 5C-E, S5C). These clusters constituted 70-80% of the total BR1 microglia, while representing less than 15% of BR3 and minimally present in PBS (Figure 5D). Clusters Mg11 and Mg13, almost absent in BR3 and PBS, expressed mitotic and proliferative genes (Figures 5E, S5C), explaining the higher number of microglia found in BR1 (Figure 3F). Using high-parametric spectral flow cytometry, we confirmed the higher proportion and total number of microglia with various reactive states in BR1 (Mg.D-E) as compared to BR3 (Figure 5F-J, S5D). Taken together, our data suggest that reactive microglia states, characterized by high expression of MHC-I/II and T cell- attracting chemokines, are associated with response to ICB, while resistant BrMs are enriched in microglia that maintain homeostatic function.

### Systemic ICB alters microglia functional state

It is unclear whether systemic ICB treatment can impact microglia states. To address this question, we assessed the combo-treated BR1 and BR3 microglia phenotype using spectral flow cytometry. Surprisingly, therapy led to the disappearance of almost all homeostatic microglia states in both models (Mg.A-Mg.C; Figures 5F-I, S5D). Instead, they were replaced by various non-homeostatic states characterized by the absence of P2RY12 expression, a receptor essential for microglia homeostasis. The loss of this receptor has been previously linked with neuroinflammation59,63 (Mg.F-Mg.J; Figures 5F-I, S5D). While the decrease of P2RY12 was a common therapy-induced feature, some phenotypic differences were still observed between BR1 and BR3 (Figure 5H-I, S5D). Only BR1 microglia maintained the expression of MHC-II, while reducing markers associated with macrophage pro- tumorigenic function such as ARG1, SIRPα64–67 and the T cell inhibitory receptors TIM3 and LAG368–70 (clusters Mg.F-G; Figure 6F-J, S6D). Of note, we observed a reduced level of PD-L1, resulting from either phenotypic changes or the blockade of PD-L1 with anti-PD-L1 antibody included in the combo treatment, thus preventing its detection by flow cytometry. In contrast, the microglia state increased in BR3, but not in BR1, after therapy (Mg.J), expressed the highest levels of TIM3, LAG3, and SIRPα (Figure 5G, 5I-J, S5D). These results indicate that systemic ICB therapy alters microglia functional states. The key differences between microglia in ICB-responder and non-responder BrMs, regardless of treatment status, lie in the expression of genes, pathways, and proteins that can modulate T-cell activities.

### BR1 tumor cell-intrinsic programs favor immune response while those in BR3 support brain homeostasis

We then sought to identify tumor cell-intrinsic molecular features that may be influencing the BrTME dissimilarities observed in our models. To do so, we compared BR1 and BR3 melanoma cells from in vivo established BrM in our whole brain scRNA-seq data set. Model-based analysis of single-cell transcriptomics (MAST)71,72 followed by GSEA revealed that BR3 cells, compared to BR1, expressed higher levels of genes and pathways related to melanin biosynthesis (e.g., Tyr, Dct, Slc45a2), neuronal function (e.g., Sema3a, Unc5c, Slc6a11, Pde1c/3a/4b, Cacna1d, Selenop), and metabolic pathways such as glutathione transferase activity (e.g., Gsta4, Gstm1), oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS, e.g., Atp6v0d2, Cox7a1), and lipid metabolism (e.g., Apoe) (Figures 6A, S6A). On the other hand, BR1 melanoma cells showed enrichment in MHC-I and MHC-II antigen presentation (e.g., H2-K1/D1, B2m, Tap1, H2-Aa/Ab1/Eb1, Cd74,), immune response and IFN signaling (e.g., Ifng, Irf1, Stat1, Ifi203, Cxcl9), cell cycle, DNA replication and repair (e.g., Mki67, Aurka/b, Plk1, Top2a, Pdgfa, Chek1) (Figures 6A, S6A). Notably, these differences were also observed when we analyzed the MP distributions in the two models, showing higher activity of MP2 (melanin biosynthesis), MP6 (neurofunction), and MP7 (melanin biosynthesis and fatty acid metabolism) in BR3, while BR1 tumor cells were enriched in MP1 (cell cycle) and MP4 (immune) (Figures 6B).

Given that the BR1 and BR3 cell lines are polyclonal, we investigated if the transcriptomic signatures distinguishing these two models were broadly expressed across cells within each model or specific to BrM cell sub-populations. To be able to align our findings with clinical data, we performed unsupervised clustering of tumor cells based on their expression of MPs (Figure 6C-D). We identified 5 clusters, T1-T5. BR3 samples were enriched in clusters T3-T5, with T4 being the predominant cluster in this model (Figure 6C, 6E and S6B). These clusters uniquely showed high activity of the melanin- MPs (MP2 and MP7), with T3 also expressing the highest level of neuro-MP activity (MP3 and MP6) (Figure 6D). In contrast, clusters T1 and T2, unique subpopulations exhibiting high immune-MP4 activity, constituted a higher proportion of tumor cells in BR1 compared to BR3 BrMs (Figure 6C-E and S6B). Cluster T2, the most abundant in BR1, displayed the highest activity for MP4 and the cell cycle-associated MP1 (Figure 6C-E and S6B). This data demonstrates that the distinct transcriptomic profiles of BR1 and BR3 are driven by differentially distributed tumor cell states. Furthermore, our results suggest that the expression of an immune response-related signature in BR1 could explain its ICB-favorable BrTME, whereas the enrichment of brain homeostasis-associated programs in BR3 cells likely results in a blunted immune response.

### BR1 and BR3 tumor cell-intrinsic programs shape the microglia functional states that drive T cell phenotypes

Cell-cell interactions are essential to shape the TME and drive antitumor immunity. Based on the BR1 and BR3 cell-intrinsic programs, we hypothesized that tumor cells communicate with microglia, skewing their functional state and downstream interaction with T cells. To test this, we performed interactome analysis of tumor cell – microglia – T cells to infer ligand-receptor (L-R) pairs implicated in their crosstalk (Figures 6F-H; S6C-D). In agreement with our hypothesis, we observed the highest predicted interaction number and strength from tumor cells to microglia in both models (Figure 6F and S6C). This crosstalk occurred among distinct microglia and tumor states and involved different types of interactions in BR1 compared to BR3 (Figure 6G-H). In BR3, we found stronger interactions from tumor clusters displaying the highest neuro-MP (T3) and melanin-MP activities (T3-T5) to homeostatic microglia (Mg1-2 and Mg8; Figure 6G). Among the most upregulated interactions in BR3 were L-R pairs previously associated microglia anti-inflammatory pathways (CD200_CD200R1, GAS6_MERTK)73–75 as well as those involved in maintenance of microglia homeostatic function, including phagocytosis (e.g., APOE_TREM2_TYROBP)76 and neuro-function (e.g., PTRM-PTRM)77 (Figure 6H). The predominant predicted tumor-to-microglia interactions in this model were driven by tumor junctional adhesion molecule (JAM)78 and extracellular matrix (ECM) ligands, such as collagens (e.g., COL4A2, COL6A3)79,80 and laminins (e.g., LAMA3, LAMC1; Figure 6H)79,80.In contrast, in the BR1 BrTME we observed enhanced communication from the tumor cell cluster enriched in immune and cell-cycle-MPs (T2) to reactive microglia states (Mg9, Mg12-13; Figure 6G-H), including upregulation of the microglia activating MIF_CD74_CD44 axis81–83. We also observed enrichment of the LGALS9 axis, known to induce MHCII expression on macrophages and DC and strong anti-tumor responses in murine melanoma models84,85.

We then investigated the downstream microglia – T cell crosstalk. Although a similar number of microglia – T cell interactions were observed between BR1 and BR3, we found significantly stronger interactions from microglia to T cells, specifically CD8+ T cells, in BR1 (Figures S6C and 6F). The reactive microglia states, which receive signals from BR1 tumor cells, were the ones predicted to preferentially interact with T cells in BR1 (Figure 6G). This crosstalk involved all the necessary signals to efficiently activate T cell responses, such as adhesion molecules, antigen presentation, TCR and co- stimulatory signaling (Figures 6G-H and S6D), explaining the T cell landscape observed in BR1. The inferred communications from microglia to CD8+ T cells were also characterized by T cell-attracting chemokines (Figure 6H)86–88, further suggesting that BR1- microglia are responsible for CD8+ T cell recruitment . Importantly, we confirmed that these chemokine-encoding genes were predominantly expressed by BR1 microglia, with significantly higher protein levels found in BR1 compared to BR3 brains (Figures S5C, 6I-J, S6E). In marked contrast to BR1, the few microglia – T cell interactions predicted in BR3 involved only cell-cell adhesion processes between microglia homeostatic states (Mg1, Mg2, and Mg8) and naïve T cells (SELL) (Figures 6G-H and S6D).

To confirm these predicted interactions using a different approach, we performed a correlation analysis of cell populations identified by flow cytometry. Agreeing with the interactome analysis, we observed positive correlations between BR1-reactive microglia (Mg.D) and CD8+ T cells expressing activation markers (CD8.F3-F5) (Figures 6K, 4H). No correlations were observed between the BR3-associated microglia (Mg.A-C) and CD8+ T cell (Figures 6G-H, 6K, S6D). Altogether, these findings support our hypothesis that BR1 and BR3 tumor cell-intrinsic programs dictate microglia functional states, which in turn shape T cell phenotypes.

### The molecular features identified in our preclinical models predict T cell infiltration and survival in BrM patients

To further validate the clinical relevance of our findings, we interrogated the potential associations of our ICB-responder and ICB-resistant signatures with BrTME composition and patient survival in publicly available clinical datasets13,21. We first focused on the association between BR1 and BR3 BrM MPs and CD8+ T cell infiltration in a small cohort of untreated melanoma patients13. To do so, we extracted CD8+ T cells from their scRNA-seq dataset and re-analyzed them using unsupervised clustering (Figure S6F). Consistent with our preclinical data, we found that the frequency of total CD8+ T cells within the biopsies positively correlated with BR1 immune-MP (MP4) activity and negatively correlated with BR3 neuro-MP (MP6) (Figure 7A). Out of the nine CD8+ T cell clusters identified in the BrM patients, eight shared similarities with the subsets found in our BrM models (Figure S6F-G, 4B-D). Although not reaching statistical significance due to the small number of patients, we observed correlation trends consistent with our pre-clinical data. All BR3 enriched MPs (MP2, MP6, MP7) showed a trend toward positive correlation with the CD8+ T cluster resembling BR3 CD8+ T cells (Figures 7B, S6G, 4B-D). Conversely, CD8+ T cell clusters resembling BR1 CD8+ T cells showed a trend of positive correlation with BR1 MPs and negative with BR3 MPs (Figures 7B, S6G, 4B-D). Among these clusters, we identified a subset sharing molecular signatures with a population recently reported as “potential tumor reactive T cell” both in patients’ BrM (BrM pTRT)19 and non-brain tumors89–93 (Figures 7C, 4C-D, S7H-I, and Table S6).

We corroborated our observations using pseudo-bulk RNA-seq analysis of data from a larger patient cohort of pan-cancer BrM, composed of individuals untreated or treated with various therapies, including ICB21. Total CD8+ T and BrM pTRT CD8+ T cell signatures showed a significant positive correlation with BR1 immune-MP4 and a negative correlation with BR3 neuro-MP6 (Figure 7D-E, S7J, and Table S6). Furthermore, we found that high expression of total CD8+ T, BrM pTRT, and BR1 MP1 signatures associated with better progression-free survival (Figure 7F, Table S6). We further generated three other signatures based on interactome and DEGs between BR1 and BR3: “BR1 pro- inflammatory”, “BR1 chemokine”, “BR3 Neuro” (Table S6) We stratified patients based on high (>=median) or low expression (< median) of these signatures and found that “BR1-proinflammatory” and “BR1 chemokine” signatures significantly associated with better patient outcomes (Figure 7F, Table S6). In contrast, the “BR3 Neuro” signature significantly associated with poorer patient outcomes (Figure 7F, Table S6). Taken together, our data demonstrate that the ICB-responder and ICB-resistant MPs and the signatures that we identified in our preclinical models are expressed in patient BrMs, correlate with their TME composition, and can predict patient outcomes.

## DISCUSSION

BrM remains a critical challenge in cancer treatment with most cases being resistant to ICB3–5,7,94,95. Unfortunately, the restricted availability of patient biopsies and lack of representative preclinical models for diverse ICB responses observed in clinical settings23–25,96 has hampered our ability to identify biomarkers and determinants of response. Here, we have developed two translationally relevant preclinical melanoma BrM mouse models that overcome these limitations, enabling us to identify tumor molecular signatures that influence the BrTME and therapy responses. In particular, we show that ICB-responder BrM (BR1) expresses a molecular program of response to IFN, antigen presentation, and inflammatory pathways that polarizes microglia into reactive states. These microglia are characterized by high expression of MHC, co-stimulatory molecules, and T cell recruiting chemokines, which enhance T cell recruitment and activation favoring ICB efficacy. In contrast resistant BrM (BR3) express neurological features and evade the immune response by preserving a homeostatic state within the BrTME. Importantly, we confirmed the translational relevance of our findings demonstrating that these ICB-responder and -resistant tumor signatures were present in human BrM. Agreeing with our preclinical data the BR1 responsive BrM signatures positively correlated with T cell infiltration, and were associated with improved patient outcome, whereas the BR3 resistant signature showed negative correlation with T cells and was associated with poorer patient outcome.

To date, the general assumption has been that circulating T cells play an important role in brain tumor control and therapy28,97,98. With respect to BrM, a previous study using a preclinical model in which melanoma cells were injected directly into the brain, has shown that the presence of extracranial disease – created by subcutaneous injection of the same cells – activated peripheral T cells promoting brain ICB response28. However, in our study, we found that naturally occurring extracranial metastases did not improve ICB efficacy. In contrast, BR1, which is associated with minimal extracranial disease, responded to therapy, while BR3, which has a higher systemic metastatic burden, did not. Although here we didn’t explore the circulating T cell pool, we found striking differences between responders and resistant models in the BrTME T cell repertoire. The presence of a proliferative T cell subset in the responder model suggests that local expansion likely plays a role in the brain T cell landscape. An interesting finding from our study is that therapy induces an increase in T cell infiltration in both responder and non-responder models; however, the phenotypic characteristics of these cells were markedly distinct. These observations may help explain contradictory results observed from studies showing a positive correlation between T-cell infiltration and improved patient outcomes vs. others that did not21,100–103, stressing the importance of studying other immune populations that may influence T-cell function.

In the context of ICB therapy, the focus in the field has been mostly placed on the T cell compartment. However, our findings reveal that not only T cells but also microglia distinguish responders from non- responders. In fact, we show that specific reactive microglia states are significantly enriched in ICB responders and positively correlate with CD8+ T cell infiltration. Although the relationship between microglia – T cell crosstalk and therapy responses has not been explored in patients, recent studies have reported positive correlations between MHC-II expression on microglia and CD8+ T cells in BrM patients19,100. Supporting our results, previous data have shown that microglia sharing some of the BR1 signatures can attract or activate T cells in other contexts, such as CNS infection104,106, Alzheimer’s disease108, or glioblastoma (GBM) treated with ICB110. Of note, CXCL9 alone or MHCII+ reactive microglia, were recently found to be required for GBM response to PD-1 or CTLA-4 blockade, respectively110,112. In our study, reactive microglia had stronger interactions with CD8+ T cells in both untreated and combo therapy conditions. In contrast, the GBM report observed these interactions uniquely after anti-CTLA-4 blockade, where they specifically controlled CD4 T cells110.

Other studies using breast and lung BrM preclinical models have recently shown that microglia with some BR1-reactive characteristics could suppress T cell activity and support BrM development61,115,120. These dual microglia functions are not mutually exclusive and suggest a context- dependent role. We observed that BR1-microglia states express different T cell immune checkpoints, which can paradoxically both induce and limit T cell activation in the absence of treatment. When these inhibitor axes are targeted by ICB, they unleash the T cells’ anti-tumor potential. Thus, our findings indicate that microglia can play different roles in BrM depending on the brain tumor subtype and the therapy utilized. Moreover, our observations can help explain the inconsistent pro- and anti- tumor impact of microglia depletion observed across independent preclinical studies10,61,120. While depleting microglia has been suggested as a potential therapeutic approach for brain tumors, our data indicate that such a strategy could be more harmful than beneficial depending on the context. Instead, modulating the balance between pro- and anti-tumor microglia states might be a more effective alternative.

Here we reveal three major types of interactions used preferentially by the ICB-resistant BR3 tumors to shape microglia states: (1) ligands known to inhibit microglia reactivity such as CD200 and Gas6105,107,109,111, (2) components of the extra-cellular matrix such as collagens and laminins, and (3) neurological features such as neurotransmitter (e.g., glutamate) and axon guidance (e.g., semaphoring).

In the CNS, these three axes constitute crucial crosstalk involving microglia and other cells (e.g., neurons, astrocytes, and endothelial cells) to maintain brain homeostasis and resolve neuroinflammation105,113,114,116–119. Others have described the pro-carcinogenic effect of CD200- CD200R and Gas6-MertK in brain primary tumors109, and a recent report implicated collagens in murine breast and lung BrM growth120. The latter study demonstrated that tumor-derived collagen polarizes pro-tumor microglia through TNFα-NF-κB signaling, suppressing T cell infiltration and activation120. We found this pathway upregulated in BR3 microglia, suggesting that similar interactions might contribute to the BR3 BrTME profile and its ICB-resistance. Additionally, while no direct link has been established between neurotransmitters and BrM response to therapy, independent studies have shown that glutamate can support GBM growth127,129 and decrease microglia reactive states118. Thus, our findings suggest that the ICB-resistant BR3 BrM model hijacks natural CNS tolerance systems to support homeostatic microglia, inhibit reactive states, and promote pro-tumoral polarization. The molecular axes that we identified not only deepen our understanding of how BrM tumors shape the BrTME but also provide potential targets for overcoming therapy resistance.127,129

We extended our findings to a pan-cancer BrM cohort, confirming that the BR1 and BR3 signatures that we established can predict the BrTME and correlate with patient outcomes. These observations provide promising predictive biomarkers and valuable insights for treatment decision-making. Specifically, we show that BrM with proliferative and inflammatory signatures benefit from ICB therapies, whereas BrM subtypes enriched in neurological features such as axon guidance are less likely to respond and may require alternative or additional strategies, such as targeting myeloid cells or tumor ligands.

One of the most arduous obstacles to improving BrM therapy is the difficulty in accessing patient samples, underscoring the importance of relevant preclinical models to elucidate mechanisms of resistance. An important consideration when studying ICB therapy in BrM is maintaining a functional TME and physiological BBB to regulate tumor extravasation and drug distribution95,131,133,135. Our models not only meet this requirement but also recapitulate the cellular and molecular diversity of BrM tumors and TME observed in the clinic13–15,17–20,44,45,99. Moreover, they capture the differential ICB responses observed in patients. Therefore, the BR1 and BR3 models constitute a key resource to perform mechanistic studies. Using them, we conducted comparative studies identifying key molecular features associated with responses to ICB therapy. By offering critical insights into the mechanisms governing sensitivity and resistance to treatment, our study significantly contributes toward the development of more efficacious therapeutic options for BrM patients.

### Limitations of the study

Here we focused on the immune and the tumor compartments, but we cannot rule out the role of other important BrTME actors, such as astrocytes and endothelial cells. Further studies should investigate how these cell types can influence ICB responses. An important limitation in the field, that persists in our study, is the to directly associate ICB response status and different BrM features in patients due to the scarcity of available human BrM samples. Although we demonstrated that the BR1 and BR3 signatures are associated with the patients’ BrTME and survival in cohorts that included ICB treatment, it will be important to test our findings when larger cohorts of treated patients become available. A remaining unmet need is the ability to identify valuable predictors of response employing less invasive approaches, such as monitoring blood or cerebrospinal fluid. Despite these constraints, our report significantly enhances our understanding of the mechanisms by which BrM tumor cells can impact BrTME and ICB responses. We anticipate that our findings will facilitate the development of new therapeutic approaches and help inform clinical trials for melanoma patients with brain metastases.
