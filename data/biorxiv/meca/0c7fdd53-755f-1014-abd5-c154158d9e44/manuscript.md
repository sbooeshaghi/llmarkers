## Abstract

AbstractInvestigation of cell behaviour and cell biological processes in human embryonic tissues is facilitated by creation of fluorescent reporters in human pluripotent stem cell lines, which can be differentiated into cell types of choice. Here we report use of a piggyBac transposon-mediated stable integration strategy to engineer human pluripotent stem cell reporter lines. These express a plasma membrane (pm) localised protein tagged with the fluorescent protein eGFP or mKate2, the photoconvertible nuclear marker H2B-mEos3.2, with or without pm-mKate2, and the cytoskeletal protein F-tractin tagged with mKate2. Focussing on neural development some of these lines were used to live image and quantify cell behaviours, including cell cycle progression and cell division orientation in spinal cord rosettes. Further, lipofection-mediated introduction of piggyBac constructs into human neural progenitors labelled single cells and small cell groups within rosettes, allowed monitoring of individual cell behaviours including neuronal delamination. Finally, using the F-tractin-mKate2 hiPSC line, actin dynamics were captured during proliferation in cortical neural rosettes. This study presents new tools and techniques with which to interrogate human cell behaviour and cell biology using live imaging approaches.

## Introduction

Live imaging is used to monitor the behaviour of cells within tissues and to reveal sub-cellular proteins dynamics. This includes important cellular processes such as cell cycle progression and changes in cell shape and adhesion as individual cells differentiate and cells group together to form structures during tissue morphogenesis. Such approaches provide insight into developmental and tissue homeostasis mechanisms and elucidate changes in disease states. The ability to genetically engineer human pluripotent stem cells to express fluorescent proteins facilitates such live imaging approaches and allows high resolution investigation of cell behaviour and cell biological processes in human tissue models.

DNA constructs can be introduced into human pluripotent cells in numerous ways. The piggyBac transposon system has advantages compared to other stable integration approaches, particularly for live cell imaging studies. In comparison with viral vectors, such as adenovirus or lentivirus (Lee, et al., 2017), the piggyBac system is more secure and convenient to generate (Ding, et al., 2005, Wang, et al., 2008, Woltjen, et al., 2009). Viral integration sites are non-random but diverse and there is no control over copy number and gene expression levels, (Cereseto and Giacca, 2004, Engelman, 2003, Sultana, et al., 2017): indeed viral integrations can alter endogenous gene expression, including vital regulatory genes and/or inactivation of tumour-suppressor genes, impacting cell behaviour and differentiation (e.g. (Hacein-Bey-Abina, et al., 2010, Hacein-Bey-Abina, et al., 2003). In contrast, piggyBac constructs flanked by a 13bp perfect terminal invert repeat sequence insert specifically into P-elements (TTAA segments) in the genome (Elick, et al., 1996, Lee, et al., 2011, Liu, et al., 2013). Other transposon systems are also used including hAT-Tol2, (Kawakami, 2007, Kawakami and Shima, 1999) or Tcl-like transposons, Sleeping Beauty and Frog Prince, (Ivics, et al., 1997, Miskey, et al., 2003, Narayanavari, et al., 2017). However, while Sleeping Beauty and piggyBac have the highest transposition activity in mammalian cells (Kim and Pyykko, 2011), Sleeping Beauty is less reliable, with a tendency for local hopping and leaving a CAG footprint following excision (Wang, et al., 2008, Wu, et al., 2006).

In comparison with other stable integration approaches, Transcription Activator-like Effector Nucleases (TALENs) (Hockemeyer, et al., 2011, Joung and Sander, 2013, Miller, et al., 2011, Wood, et al., 2011) and CRISPR/Cas9 (Charpentier and Doudna, 2013, Doudna and Charpentier, 2014, Jinek, et al., 2012, Pacesa, et al., 2024), which achieve precision gene engineering of single copy insertions at a defined locus, the piggyBac transposon system will insert multiple copies of fluorescently tagged proteins within the many P-elements/ TTAA chromosome sites (Elick, et al., 1996, Lee, et al., 2011) (Liu, et al., 2013, Woodard and Wilson, 2015). This feature of the piggyBac system has a particular advantage for long term live-imaging by increasing transgene copy number and expression, and so detection sensitivity for fluorescently tagged proteins. This in turn reduces exposure times and thereby potential phototoxicity.

Nevertheless, gene editing using CRISPR/Cas9 and other nucleases has been successfully used to express fluorescently tagged endogenous proteins for live cell imaging (Dambournet, et al., 2014) and these include hiPSC lines expressing, for example, mEGFP-nuclear lamin B1 (Haupt, et al., 2018, Roberts, et al., 2017). This has the advantage of endogenous regulatory control of fusion protein expression but does rely on this having sufficient strength for live detection and for longer term live cell imaging. Fusion of an endogenous protein with large exogenous sequences such as GFP may also compromise protein function. Indeed, such approaches typically report low recovery of biallelic edits, which may reflect lack of tolerance for two tagged copies of an indispensable protein, but this is likely to be protein specific (Roberts, et al., 2017). Inclusion of an amino acid linker sequence between the native and fluorescent protein can mitigate this risk. Such engineered hPSC lines require quality controls, including confirmation of pluripotency, karyotype and growth rate as well as comparison with endogenous protein localisation and cell behaviour in well-defined differentiation assays.

Here we describe use of a piggyBac mediated engineering strategy to create a series of human pluripotent stem cell reporter lines by introducing fluorescent proteins which localise to the plasma-membrane, chromatin, or actin cytoskeleton. We characterise these lines and deploy them, or lipofected piggyBac constructs introduced during differentiation, in live imaging protocols to uncover fundamental characteristics of human neural progenitor cell behaviour in in vitro assays for spinal cord and cerebral cortex development.

## Results and Discussion

### Generation of human pluripotent cell fluorescent reporter lines using the piggyBac-transposon stable integration system

To facilitate analysis of cell behaviour in live tissue derived from human pluripotent stem cells we generated a series of fluorescent reporter hESC or iPSC lines (Dady, et al., 2022)(Figures 1A,B). To monitor cell shape and dynamics we used a plasma membrane (pm) localised protein tagged with eGFP or mKate2 (pm-eGFP or pm-mKate2) (Sanders, et al., 2013). To allow monitoring of the cell nucleus we engineered a line expressing H2B-mEos3.2, the latter localising in the nucleus and mEos3.2 having the potential for laser-mediated photo-conversion (Zhang, et al., 2012) and so the labelling and tracking of individually selected cells. This provides the potential for clonal analysis in combination with immunofluorescence for cell type specific marker proteins. We also co-transfected H2B-mEos3.2 and pm-mKate2 expressing plasmids to allow monitoring of labelled nuclei and changes in cell shape. Finally, to monitor actin cytoskeletal dynamics we created an F-tractin-mKate2 cell line.

To generate these reporters, we used the hESC line H9 (for pm-eGFP, pm-mKate2, H2B-mEos3.2), or hiPSC line ChiPS4 for F-Tractin-mKate2 and a further pm-eGFP line (Dady, et al., 2022) and we deployed the piggyBac transposon-mediated stable integration strategy (Sanders, et al., 2013, Sarkar, et al., 2003, Yusa, et al., 2009, Yusa, et al., 2011). The piggyBac vectors are composed of the specific fusion protein of interest whose expression is driven by a CAG promoter (Figures 1A,B). This cargo is then flanked by two Inverted Terminal Repeat (ITR) sequences which allow a hyperactive piggyBac transposase (HyPBase, characterised by highly stable enzymatic activity), to cut and paste the cargo within the specific TTAA-chromosomal sites (Yusa, et al., 2011). Critical to efficient stable integration was transfection of 5 times more piggyBac vector than transposase.

The cloning strategy for generating these constructs is described in the Methods. Once complete these were introduced to a suspension of 1×106 hPSCs by electroporation with a final concentration of 1.2 μg/μl of piggyBac constructs and HyPBase (Yusa, et al., 2011) (Figures 1C Methods). Transfected hPSCs were then FAC-sorted and selected using their specific fluorescence properties: this optimized protocol delivered a high rate of transfection (Figure S1). Using this approach, polyclonal cell lines were generated for each fusion protein of interest: these were composed of cells with this construct randomly inserted within each genome. Polyclonal lines were sorted with easily detectable, but not high, fluorescence for live imaging studies. We also made a monoclonal line for pm-eGFP in ChiPS4 for future use as this approach would obviate the remote possibility of cell-to-cell variation impacting cell behaviours captured in live imaging experiments (Figure 1D).

### Using human pluripotent cell fluorescent reporter lines to monitor and quantify cell behaviour in human spinal cord rosettes

These fluorescent reporter hPSC lines allowed the development of in vitro assays for monitoring and quantifying human cell behaviour. Here we first focussed on capturing fundamental parameters of human neurogenesis using our established spinal cord rosette assay (Dady, et al., 2022, Verrier, et al., 2018)(Figure 2A). This involves differentiating hPSCs into Neuro-Mesodermal Progenitor-like cells (NMP-like cells) and their subsequent differentiation into SOX2 expressing neural progenitors, which self-organise into neural rosettes exhibiting localised expression of apico-basal polarity markers such as NCAD/CDH2 by Day 6 (D6) following NMP-L cell plating (Dady, et al., 2022). This differentiation assay generates neural crest progenitors and dorsal neural progenitors, with the first neurons apparent from D8, peak neurogenesis at D14 and gliogenesis onset by ∼ D20. This sequence of events is in line with those documented in the human embryonic spinal cord with dorsal neurogenesis at forelimb level commencing by CS12/13 and gliogenesis by CS18 (Dady, et al., 2022, Rayon, et al., 2020).

The cell lines expressing pm-eGFP and pm-mKate with H2B-mEos3.2 lend themselves to analysis of cell shape change. We wished to monitor the dynamics of neuroepithelial cell behaviour as these cells progressed through the cell cycle during inter-kinetic nuclear migration (IKNM)(Sauer, 1935, Spear and Erickson, 2012). This behaviour is characteristic of pseudo-stratified epithelia and involves movement of the nucleus from the basal to the apical side of a cell in G2 phase, subsequent mitosis at the apical (ventricular) surface and return to the basal surface in G1: here a neuroepithelial cell may then either re-enter S-phase or exit the cell cycle and commence neuronal differentiation. To monitor this behaviour in spinal cord rosettes we first optimised our assay to address a major challenge of longer-term live imaging, increasing thickness of the developing tissue over time (D6 rosettes are typically ∼ 100μm). This reduces the ability to maintain cells of interest in focus, even when deploying a long working-distance lens and deconvolution algorithms (see Methods). To reduce the thickness of rosettes we developed a re-plating regime in which D6 rosettes were manually micro-dissected and replated on glass-bottom dishes suitable for microscopy (Figure 2B). This resulted in formation of small satellite micro-rosettes (SmR) surrounding the replated primary rosette (PrR) (Figure 2C). Satellite micro-rosettes were polarised as indicated by apical actin localisation. Most importantly, SmR were flatter (∼ 50μm thick) allowing longer term high-resolution live cell imaging.

Human spinal cord satellite rosette cells derived from hESCs expressing pm-eGFP or pm-mKate and H2B-mEos3.2 were then imaged at intervals for 24h at D13-D14 and at D18-D19 (Figs. 2D-E, movie 1). Apical visiting time (defined as the period during which a basally located nucleus transits to the apical surface, divides and returns to a basal position) and used as an indication of changing cell cycle duration, increased from D13/D14 to D18/D19 and approximately doubled over this 6-day period (D13, 11h ± 3h; D19, 25h ± 7h (Fig. 2F). This real time measurement confirmed that cell cycle time extends as human ESC derived spinal cord progenitors age (Rayon, et al., 2020). These findings further correlate with studies in mouse (Calegari, et al., 2005, Calegari and Huttner, 2003, Takahashi, et al., 1995), and chick (Olivera-Martinez, et al., 2014) embryos showing that neuron-generating divisions, which appear later in development, are characterized by longer IKNM and cell cycle duration.

### Labelling and live-imaging individual human spinal cord rosette cells using temporally defined lipofection of piggyBac constructs

While it is possible to follow individual cell profiles as pm-FP expressing cells approach the apical surface, divide and return to a basal position we also wished to monitor sub-cellular behaviour within individual cells, including mitotic spindle orientation and final cleavage plane as well as cell delamination events within this human neuroepithelium. To achieve this, we designed a new strategy taking advantage of the precise timing of neurogenesis (Fig. 2A) (Dady, et al., 2022) and a less efficient mode of cell transfection (Fig.3A): a mixture of piggyBac constructs and the HyPBase transposase was transfected into D8/D9 human spinal cord neural progenitors using lipofection (Felgner, et al., 1987)(Figure 3A, see Methods). Lipofected neurogenic neural rosettes were then allowed to differentiate to later stages (Fig.3A). This approach led to labelling of single or small groups of human spinal cord progenitors within each rosette (Figs.3B,C).

Lipofection of cells with the pm-mKate and H2B-mEos3.2 plasmids allowed us to monitor cell shape and chromatin configuration and so facilitated analysis of the orientation of the mitotic spindle and final cleavage plane. Rosettes containing such cells were cultured until D13 or D18 and imaged at 10 min intervals for 24-30h. Most divisions within each rosette between D13 and D14 were symmetrical, with vertical cleavage planes and resulting horizontal divisions, while between D18 and D19 most divisions were asymmetric, with horizontal cleavage planes and resulting vertically orientated divisions (Figs 3D-F, movie 3). These findings demonstrate that division orientation alters as development proceeds in human spinal cord neuroepithelium. This change correlates with the longer apical visiting times and the accumulation of neurons in later stage rosettes (Dady, et al., 2022).

To monitor delamination of new-born neurons within rosettes we followed individual cells with a basally located nucleus and a long cellular process inserted into the apical surface of the rosette. In a subset of these cells, we were able to observe delamination from the apical surface, a step characteristic of new-born neurons (Das and Storey, 2014). Moreover, this involved the abscission of an apical membrane particle in a manner characteristic of the apical abscission mechanism first found in the chicken spinal cord (Das and Storey, 2014, Kasioulis, et al., 2017)(Fig. 3G, 3H and movie 5). Overall, these analyses of human neuroepithelial cell behaviour during differentiation revealed the expansion of the early human neural progenitor cell pool in developing spinal cord, followed by a later phase characterized by slower cell cycle progression, increased asymmetric cell division and the generation of neurons.

### Live imaging F-tractin in human cerebral cortex progenitors uncovers known and novel actin dynamics

Filamentous actin is a fundamental cytoskeletal component involved in the mechanics of cell division, cell motility and force transduction within tissues, and recent work has further uncovered roles around (Davidson and Cadot, 2021) and within the nucleus (Gunasekaran, et al., 2022, Hyrskyluoto and Vartiainen, 2020) where actin regulates gene expression as part of the nucleoskeleton in cell type and cell state specific ways, e.g. (Okuno, et al., 2020). Within the developing vertebrate neuroepithelium dynamic actin mediates aspects of IKNM (Spear and Erickson, 2012, Yanakieva, et al., 2019), apical cell adhesion and constriction leading to neuronal delamination, as well as intercellular filopodial contacts between non-neighbouring cells, e.g. (Das and Storey, 2014, Kasioulis, et al., 2022, Kasioulis, et al., 2017). In humans, actin cytoskeleton dysfunction in the cerebral cortex leads to periventricular heterotopia characterised by failure of neuronal delamination, altered cell adhesion and reduced brain size/cell proliferation, and is associated with seizures, dyslexia and psychiatric disorders (Lian and Sheen, 2015). Periventricular heterotopia is linked to mutation of actin binding proteins such as Filamin A (Sheen, et al., 2005). Moreover, the uniquely human fusion gene CHRFAM7A acts at least in part by activating actin in neuronal progenitors, impacting cell shape, growth cone extension and dendritic spine formation and so synaptic clustering (Szigeti, et al., 2023). The mechanisms by which actin mediates such critical processes in the developing human brain have yet to be elucidated.

The F-tractin-mKate2 hiPSC line was used to capture detailed actin dynamics in cerebral cortex (cortical) neural rosettes. A dual-SMAD forebrain differentiation protocol was followed, after (Chambers, et al., 2009) and neuroepithelial rosette structures were generated by day 28. The appearance of Tbr2/EOMES positive cells (Fig. S2A) indicated that some basal progenitors were present and confirmed forebrain tissue identity, while Beta-Tubulin labelling (Fig. S2B) established neuron generation. Live imaging analyses focussed on actin dynamics in cell behaviours characteristic of early cortical development. F-Tractin localised as expected to the neuroepithelial cell cortex and was enriched at the apical cell junctions around apical end feet and in rounding mitotic cells (Fig. 4A and movie 4): a similar distribution was revealed by Phalloidin staining of fixed rosettes (Fig S2C). Apical enrichment of F-Tractin was also observed during cell delamination from the apical surface (Fig 4B, movie 5). F-Tractin was detected at low levels in the cytoplasm, distinguishing the nucleus (where F-Tractin was not detected) and nuclear envelope breakdown (NEB) (Figs 4C-E). Most divisions (97%) took place at the apical surface; 92% were symmetrical with a vertical cleavage plane (Fig. 4C, movie 6), while the remaining divisions were asymmetric with a horizontal cleavage (Fig. 4D, movie 7). A few basal divisions within the plane of rosettes (Fig. S2D, movie 8) correlated with low numbers of Tbr2 expressing cells at this time (Fig. S2A).

Focussing on entry into prophase, a transient F-Tractin enrichment localised to the basal nuclear membrane prior to NEB: this was never observed on the apical side of the nuclear membrane and disappeared as soon as NEB occurred (7.8±2.8’ later, Fig 4C, and Fig 4E, 5’). This pattern of actin localisation is a novel observation, which to the best of our knowledge has not been described before. Actin polymerises around the entire nuclear envelope of mitotic U2OS human cells just prior to NEB and associates with nuclear envelope remnants to facilitate chromosome congression and correct segregation (Booth, et al., 2019). We speculate that, in cortical progenitor cells, polarised actin polymerisation at the basal nuclear surface may similarly prevent basal chromosome displacement and enable apically localised mitosis. Following NEB, a strong enrichment of F-Tractin was further observed in the bulk of the basal cortex as it moved toward the apical surface (Figs 4C-E). This movement ended 15.6±5.2’ after NEB (Fig 4C) with the cell rounding at the apical surface. At this stage, a thin basal process persisted (Fig 4C, 26’) and cytokinesis occurred 12.4±6.1’ later. A role for actin in apical nuclear movement during IKNM has been reported in the vertebrate neuroepithelium (Norden, et al., 2009, Spear and Erickson, 2012, Yanakieva, et al., 2019) and in Drosophila larval wing disk cells, where perturbation of actin polymerisation in the basal process disrupted apical movement during IKNM (Meyer, et al., 2011). Overall, this analysis of F-tractin localisation in human cortical rosettes revealed novel (accumulation at the basal nuclear membrane) as well as expected actin localisations and dynamics. Next steps might include identification of actin binding proteins that regulate specific actin functions and development of this tool to include a nuclear localisation signal to investigate the roles of nuclear actin.

The human pluripotent fluorescent protein reporter cell lines generated and characterised here and the piggyBac strategy used to generate such reporters, will be valuable for the investigation of the cell biology of human neurogenesis. This could be extended from neural rosettes to brain organoids to elucidate mechanisms underlying human cortical expansion and indeed to any cell or tissue type that can be differentiated from these pluripotent cell lines. Such tissue assays could be used to screen for the impact of environmental factors (e.g. extreme heat, toxins, and other stressors) on human cell behaviour and differentiation. The ability to visualise the actin cytoskeleton and other key cell biological elements in pluripotent cells and their derivatives may also pave the way for biomechanical studies in human tissues. These reporter cell lines could also be used in combination with pharmacological studies, chemical genetics approaches, or in functional assays involving acute and targeted degradation of proteins using proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTACs) to elucidate molecular mechanisms underlying cellular and developmental processes.

## Materials and Methods

### Human pluripotent cell authentication and quality control

Human ESC line H9 was obtained from WiCell and human iPSC line ChiPS4 from Cellartis AB, now Takara Bio Europe. These suppliers provided documentation confirming cell identity. STR DNA profiling for ChiPS4 and ChiPS4-pmGFP (polyclonal), H9 (WA09) confirmed identity of these lines in our laboratory. All cell lines were expanded and banked on arrival. Prior to experiments representative lots of each cell bank were thawed and tested for post-thaw viability and to ensure sterility and absence of mycoplasma contamination. Sterility testing was performed by direct inoculation of conditioned medium into tryptic soya broth and soya bean casein broth and no contamination was observed. Mycoplasma testing was carried out by DAPI staining of fixed cultures and using the mycoalert mycoplasma detection kit (Lonza). We confirm that the cell lines used in this study were free of mycoplasma and aerobic bacteria and fungi.

H9 (WA09) hES cells were supplied at passage 24, the cells were thawed, and cell banks prepared at passage 29: for experiments the cells were used between passage 31 and 41. ChiPS4 hiPS cells were supplied at passage 9 and cell banks prepared at passage 13: for experiments the cells were used between passage 15 and 25. Two passages after thawing cells were checked for uniform expression of pluripotency markers (OCT4, SOX2, NANOG, SSEA-3, SSEA-4 TRA-1-60 and TRA-1-81), absence of differentiation markers (SSEA-1, HNF3-beta, beta-III-tubulin and smooth muscle alpha actinin) by immunofluorescence.

### Human Pluripotent cell culture

Human pluripotent stem cells were maintained as feeder-free cultures in TESR medium (Ludwig, et al., 2006) supplemented with bFGF (30ng/mL, Peprotech) and Noggin (10ng/ml, Peprotech) on Geltrex matrix coated plates (10 μg/cm2, Life Technologies) at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2 in air. Cells were enzymatically dispersed twice a week using TrypLE select (Life Technologies), counted using a hemacytometer and seeded on Geltrex coated plates at a density of 3e4-5e4 cells/cm2. For single-cell passaging, the medium was supplemented by addition of the Rho kinase inhibitor Y-27632 (10 mM, Tocris). All work with hESCs was undertaken in accordance with the UK Stem Cell Bank steering committee (license number SCSC14-29).

### Plasmid expression constructs

The APX1 (Advanced PiggyBac Expression) plasmid was synthetically engineered to harbor improved PiggyBac inverted terminal repeats (ITRs), the cytomegalovirus (CMV) enhancer/chicken Beta-actin (CAG) promoter, woodchuck post regulatory element (WPRE), and the Life Technologies Gateway recombination cassette allowing for efficient phiC31-mediated recombination (Sanders, et al., 2013)(Figure 1A). Subcellularly localized fluorescent protein gene cassettes were synthesized by overlap extension PCR and cloned into the Gateway Entry Vector, pENTRDTOPO, sequence verified. High efficiency transposase-mediated insertion of the PiggyBac transposon cassette was accomplished through transient co-expression of the mammalian optimized PiggyBac transposase (CMV HyPBase, (Yusa, et al., 2011)).

For multicolour labelling of subcellular structures, we utilized monomeric enhanced green fluorescent protein (mEGFP) (gift from Karel Svoboda, Addgene plasmid #18696) (Harvey, et al., 2008) and far-red mKate2 (Evrogen). These proteins were selected for their photostability, low toxicity, high brightness and distinct emission spectra for dual-colour assays making them suitable for live-cell imaging. To label the outer leaflet of the cell membrane, membrane-associated palmitoylated proteins were generated by appending 20-amino acid sequence of ratGAP-43 (MLCCMRRTKQVEKNDEDQKI) to the N-terminus (pm-XFP). To label the cell nucleus, mEos3.2 and green-to-red photoconvertible protein (a gift from Michael Davidson & Tao Xu (Addgene plasmid 54525) was coupled to Histone 2B (H2B) (Zhang, et al., 2012). For labeling of filamentous actin, F-Tractin which contains the inositol triphosphate 3-kinase actin binding domain was coupled to mKate2. Cell lines expressing both H2B-mEos3.2 and pm-mKate2 were then created by co-transfection of each construct and selection for dual expressing cells using FACS.

### hPSCs transfection

H9 hESCs or hIPSCs were transfected using a Neon electroporator using 10 μl tips (Life Technologies). Briefly, cells were dispersed, resuspended in TESR medium and counted as described above. For each transfection 1e6 cells were collected by centrifugation at 300 xg for 2 minutes, washed with 1ml of PBS, then resuspended with 10μl of Neon electroporation buffer R. 1 μg of piggyBac vector and 0.2 μg of CMV HyPBase vector was then added to the cells in a volume of 2 μl. The cells were then electroporated (1150v, 30 mSec, 1 pulse), resuspended in TESR medium supplemented with bFGF, Noggin and Y27632 and plated on Geltrex coated dishes. Cells were maintained as described above then FAC-sorted 10 days after transfection.

### Flow cytometry and cell sorting

Transfected hESCs or hiPSCs were dispersed and re-suspended as described above, then sorted by FACS using a SH800 (Sony) cell sorter as a bulk pool of cells with a narrow gate selected for robustly expressing cells. Quadrant gates used to estimate the percentage of positive cells and level of fluorescence needed were designed based on fluorescence levels detected in the non-transfected control samples. Sorted cells expressing medium levels of fluorescence were expanded and frozen then representative lots of each cell line were thawed and tested for mycoplasma contamination using the MycoAlert assay (Lonza), and fungi and aerobic bacteria sterility by inoculating conditioned medium into trypticase soya broth (Sigma Aldrich). No contamination was observed. In some instances (pm-eGFP in hIPSC4) monoclonal cell lines were made from the polyclonal FACS sorted pools. To make monoclonal cell lines, 400 cells were seeded into 60 mm dishes and cultured until the colonies were 2 – 3 mm in diameter. They were then picked using 3.2 mm cloning discs (Sigma Aldrich) soaked in TrypLE select and seeded into 96 well plates. Individual clones were then expanded in TESR medium. Controls for pluripotency were undertaken as described above.

### Neural differentiation assays

#### Spinal cord rosette differentiation

This was carried out following (Dady, et al., 2022, Verrier, et al., 2018). Briefly, hESC were plated on Geltrex matrix (20 μg/cm2, Life Technologies) at a density of 4e4 cells/cm2 in TSER medium as described and allowed to attach for 24h. Cells were differentiated for 48 hours in Neurobasal medium plus 1X B27, 1xN2, 1x non-essential amino acids and 1x glutamax (all Thermo Fisher) (N2B27 medium) supplemented with 3 µM Chiron99021 (Tocris) and 20 ng/ml bFGF (PeproTech) followed by a further 24 hours in N2B27 medium supplemented with 3 µM Chiron99021, 20 ng/ml bFGF, 10 mM SB431542 (Tocris) and 50 mg/ml Noggin (Peprotech) to obtain NeuroMesodermal progenitors (NMPs). For neural differentiation of NMPs, these cells were then passaged using PBS-EDTA 0.5mM and seeded back at controlled density (1e5 cells/cm2) on Geltrex (20μg/cm2, Life Technologies) in N2B27 medium supplemented with 10 mM SB431542, 50 mg/ml Noggin, 100 nM all-trans retinoic acid (Sigma Aldrich) and 10 mM Y27632 (Tocris). After 24 hours the medium was removed and replaced with N2B27 medium containing 100 nM all-trans retinoic acid for the remainder of the differentiation. As described previously, dorsal spinal cord neural rosettes formed from day 6 (Dady, et al., 2022) and medium was refreshed every 3 days.

#### Forebrain/Cortical rosette differentiation

hPSCs were dispersed as described above, counted, and re-suspended in TESR medium supplemented with bFGF (30 ng/ml), Noggin (10 ng/ml) and Y27632 (10 μM) at a concentration of 1e5 cells/ml. 100 μl of cell suspension (1e4 cells) was then added to the required number of wells of 96 well v-bottom plates and the cells pelleted by centrifugation at 300 xg for 5 minutes to form embryoid bodies (EBs). EBs were cultured for 48 hours then collected from the 96 well plates using an 8-channel pipette, pooled together, and washed twice with DMEM/F12 medium (Gibco). EBs were then resuspended in DMEM/F12 containing 20% knockout serum, 1x glutamax, 1x NEAA, 0.1 mM beta-mercaptoethanol (all Gibco), SB431542 (10 μM, Tocris) and LDN193189 (0.5 μM Sigma Aldrich) and 50 EBs transferred to each well of a low attachment 6-well plate in 2ml of medium to begin differentiation. Every 48 hours 1ml of medium was removed from each well and replaced with Neurobasal (Gibco) containing 1x glutamax, 1x NEAA, 1x N2 (Gibco), 1x B27 (Gibco), SB431542 (10 μM) and LDN193189 (0.5 μM). On day 9 of differentiation the EBs were recovered from the 6 well plates and replated on Geltrex coated (10 μg/cm2) 8 well chamber slides (Ibidi) using 10 – 15 EBs per well in Neurobasal containing 1x glutamax, 1x NEAA, 1x N2, 1x B27, SB431542 (10 μM) and LDN193189 (0.5 μM). The medium was refreshed every 48 hours until day 15 of differentiation when it was changed to Neurobasal containing 1x glutamax, 1x NEAA, 1x N2, 1x B27, BDNF (20 ng/ml, Peprotech) and GDNF (20 ng/ml, Peprotech). The medium was then refreshed every 3 days, neural rosettes were evident from day 18 of differentiation.

### Labeling individual cells in human spinal cord rosettes

For single cell or small cell group transfection, NMP-L cells were replated at end of D3 and cultured for 8 to 9 days and transfected using Lipofectamine 3000 kit (Thermo Fisher) following manufacturer’s instructions with a combination of piggyBac plasmids (pm-eGFP + HypBase or pm-mKate +H2B-mEos3.2 + HypBase) for a final concentration of 2 μg/μl of DNA mix. These lipofected human neural progenitors were then cultured and imaged for the desired period.

### Generating satellite human spinal cord rosettes

From NMP cell passaging, transfected cells were cultured in N2B27 containing 100nM RA (SIGMA) until day 6 when neural rosettes were easily observable. Using a very sharp glass needle (0.29-0.31 mm) (Vitrolife), neural rosettes were individually micro-dissected and gently deposited on a 35 mm glass bottom petri dish (WPI) previously coated with Geltrex (20µg/cm2, Life Technologies). Micro-dissected rosettes were then cultured in N2B27 containing 100nM RA (SIGMA) until the desired stage of observation.

### Live imaging of human neural rosettes

Cells in satellite rosettes formed from primary micro-dissected spinal cord rosettes or individual lipofected neural progenitors were imaged using a DeltaVision Core microscope system, allowing detection of fine cellular detail at low fluorescence levels with minimal exposure time, in a WeatherStation environmental chamber maintained at 37°C. The spinal cord rosette culture medium was buffered with a 5% CO2/95% air mix and maintained in a humid chamber. Images were acquired using an Olympus 40x 1.30 NA objective using a Xenon light source and a CoolSnap HQ2 cooled CCD camera (Photometrics). 30-40 optical sections (exposure time, 10-50 milliseconds for each channel, 1024×1024 pixels, 2×2 binning) spaced 1.5μm apart were imaged for each micro-satellite neural rosette at 10-minute intervals for periods up to 36h. Images were deconvolved and maximum intensity projections of Z-stacks were made using SoftWorx imaging software (Applied Precision) (Das, et al., 2012).

Confocal live imaging of cortical rosettes expressing F-Tractin::mKate2 was undertaken at 37°C with a 5% CO2/95% air mix using a Leica SP8 Stellaris confocal microscope with a x63 NA1.4 oil objective. Stacks of 18 slices optical z-section separated by 0.8 µm, covering a 154 × 154 ×14.4 µm region were acquired over multiple positions every 310 seconds for up to 24 hours. We applied a 3D gaussian blur with a sigma of 0.8x 0.8x 0.8 µm.

### Quantification of cell behaviour

In spinal cord rosettes, apical visiting time was assessed by measuring time taken for a single labelled cell located in the basal region of one spinal cord rosette (rosette periphery) to move to the apical region and divide (rosette centre). To be consistent, observed rosettes were selected by their similar diameter ∼100-150 μm. At least 5 different cells per spinal cord rosette and at least 5 different rosettes were analyzed for each time point (D13, D14, D18, 1D9).

Cell division symmetry was assessed by measuring the angle of the metaphase plate relative to the apical surface at the lumen wall of the spinal rosette during cell division using Fiji. Cell divisions were considered symmetric when the metaphase plate was perpendicular to the axis of cell division. On the contrary, cell divisions were considered asymmetric when the metaphase plate formed an angle of 40-45° to the axis of cell division (with one daughter cell remaining longer at the apical surface. All cells were analysed in 3D. At least 5 different cells per spinal cord rosette and at least 5 different rosettes were analyzed for each time point.

In cortical rosettes, we measured the time between cell cycle stages in rosettes expressing F-Tractin-mKate2. Nuclear Envelope Breakdown was identified as the moment when cytoplasmic F-Tractin entered the position held by the nucleus during the previous timepoint. Completion of apical-to-basal migration as the moment when the cell appeared rounded at the apical surface. Cytokinesis onset was defined as the timepoint when the first membrane cortical contraction was detected. Cells in 12 different rosettes imaged for 22 hours from D29-D30 were analysed.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis for spinal cord rosettes was performed using non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test for non-normally distributed data using Prism V8. Results are represented as means ± SEM or SD as indicated in figures and p-values are indicated with *p<0.05, **p<0.01, *** p<0.001, and ****p<0.0001.
