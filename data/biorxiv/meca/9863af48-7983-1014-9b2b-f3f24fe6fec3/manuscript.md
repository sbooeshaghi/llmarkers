## Abstract

AbstractA recently developed TDP-43 RNA aptamer (TDP-43APT) with greater sensitivity and specificity for detecting pathological TDP-43, compared to currently available antibodies, has revealed novel pathologies in ALS, including early nuclear aggregation preceding disease symptoms. Moreover, whilst ALS has traditionally been considered a disease of the central nervous system (CNS), non-CNS manifestations of disease are gaining increasing awareness. Employing an antibody approach to detect pathologically phosphorylated TDP-43, we previously uncovered pre-symptomatic, systemic TDP-43 pathology in archived non-CNS tissue, taken during life. Here, using two recently developed tools, (i) TDP-43APT to identify pathological TDP-43, and (ii) in situ hybridisation probes directed to a splice target of TDP-43 (Stathmin-2) to demonstrate concurrent loss of TDP-43 function, we re-examined non-CNS tissues from a variety of organ systems from this cohort of ante-mortem tissue taken from people who went on to develop ALS. Amongst non-CNS organs with evidence of pathological TDP-43 aggregation, and concurrent TDP-43 loss-of-function, occurring prior to motor symptom onset, were colon, skin, muscle, blood vessels, gallbladder, and lymph nodes. Cell types affected include sebocytes, endothelial cells, peripheral neuronal cells, dendritic cells, chondrocytes - all cells with a shared cell linage from the neural crest, implying a neurodevelopmentally-defined cell-type specific predisposition to TDP-43 pathology. This cell-type predisposition, along with TDP-43 pathology identified in tissue mobile, circulating inflammatory cells (T-cells and macrophages) may help to guide early, pre-symptomatic peripheral biopsy and biomarker development. Our findings extend the number of tissue and cell types with pathological TDP-43 aggregation outside of the CNS prior to motor symptoms, showing this is a common feature of ALS, affecting a number of organ systems that are readily accessible for biopsy. We propose that skin and gastro-intestinal tract tissues provide the most promising potential for ease of biopsy, with potential applications for ALS diagnosis and monitoring prior to motor symptom emergence amongst general and at-risk populations.

## Introduction

Pathological protein misfolding and accumulation is a histological feature of many neurodegenerative diseases (Bossy-Wetzel et al., 2004). The hallmark proteinopathy seen in the majority of people with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is the aberrant accumulation of the TAR-DNA binding protein 43 kDa (TDP-43) in both the nucleus and cytoplasm (Huang et al., 2024, Neumann et al., 2006, Spence et al., 2024). Previously thought of as a disease affecting only the motor system, non-motor symptoms of ALS are now becoming better recognised (see Beswick et al., 2022, Fang et al., 2017, Nash and Sitty, 2021, Shojaie et al., 2023). These non-motor symptoms include cognitive symptoms of frontotemporal dementia (McHutchison et al., 2024), and systemic symptoms affecting a wider range of organ systems than the brain alone — extensive evidence of non-motor symptoms in non-CNS tissues in ALS is provided by pathological, patient reported, epidemiological, and clinical investigative studies, which extend to several organ systems and involvements including the gastrointestinal system (Beswick et al., 2023, Conroy et al., 2023, Günther et al., 2016, Marsden et al., 2023, Nakamura et al., 2012, Nicholson et al., 2021, Niu et al., 2025, Nübling et al., 2014, Pattle et al., 2023, Samara et al., 2021, Sonnenberg et al., 1994, Sun et al., 2021, The ALS Association, 2019, Toepfer, 1999, Toepfer et al., 1999, Toepfer et al., 1997), skin (Dalla Bella et al., 2016, deCarvalho et al., 2021, Hammad et al., 2007, Hayashi et al., 2007, Kolde et al., 1996, Nolano et al., 2024, Nolano et al., 2017, Ono et al., 1998, Ono et al., 1986, Paré and Gros-Louis, 2017, Pattle et al., 2023, Pugdahl et al., 2007, Truini et al., 2015, Wallace et al., 2014, Weis et al., 2011), bladder (Nübling et al., 2014), blood vessels (Kolde et al., 1996, Omar et al., 2025), lymph nodes (Pattle et al., 2023), skeletal muscle (Cykowski et al., 2018, Kurashige et al., 2022, Kurashige et al., 2025, Mori et al., 2019, Zhang et al., 2024) and myocardial muscle (Cykowski et al., 2018, Mori et al., 2019, Zhang et al., 2024). Crucially, non-CNS pre-symptomatic TDP-43 pathologies have been shown in ALS by identifying toxic TDP-43 gain-of-function (Kurashige et al., 2022, Pattle et al., 2023, Zhang et al., 2024), or TDP-43 loss-of-function through splicing repression (Irwin et al., 2024) prior to clinical symptoms, and sometimes over a decade before (Pattle et al., 2023). This indicates that early non-motor symptoms, and non-CNS tissues along with fluid biomarkers, provide potential for early diagnosis of ALS.

Cognitive and systemic symptoms correspond well with accumulation of TDP-43 aggregates detectable using classical antibody-based approaches in non-motor brain regions in the central nervous system (CNS) (Gregory et al., 2020b) and in different organs, such as the skin and GI tract in non-CNS tissues (Pattle et al., 2023). However, antibody-based detection may be limiting our ability to translate these findings into biomarker development strategies, due to impaired specificity (poor classification of negatives (Gregory et al., 2020b)), limiting diagnostic accuracy.

We have recently improved our ability to detect TDP-43 pathology beyond demonstration of TDP-43 aggregates alone through the combined application of, (i) a TDP-43 RNA aptamer (Zacco et al., 2022) optimised for use in histopathological staining of human tissue (Spence et al., 2024, Waldron et al., 2024) to detect TDP-43 pathology (TDP-43APT), and (ii) in situ hybridisation probes for cryptic exons of STMN-2, thereby simultaneously demonstrating TDP-43 loss-of-function (cryptic exon detection) in tissues, and increasing the accuracy of detection of TDP-43 gain-of-function (aggregation events detected by TDP-43APT (Spence et al., 2024)). Together, these new tools facilitate the detection of a wider range of pathological events compared to classical antibody approaches alone.

Here, we revisit a previously published cohort of ante-mortem tissue taken from people who went on to develop ALS, where the investigation of non-CNS tissues using classical antibodies to TDP-43 (pTDP-43 and C-terminal antibodies) demonstrated organs with and without systemic, non-CNS, TDP-43 pathology burden (Pattle et al., 2023). In the study presented here, we take advantage of the improved specificity and sensitivity of our recently published TDP-43 RNA aptamer (TDP-43APT) (Spence et al., 2024), in combination with STMN-2 cryptic exon in situ hybridisation probes (Spence et al., 2024), to re-examine this published ante-mortem tissue cohort and establish the burden of systemic, non-CNS, TDP-43 pathology with greater sensitivity and specificity, and to assess whether TDP-43 loss-of-function effects are detectable in these non-CNS tissues. Our aim was to identify, with improved tools, readily accessible organs with evidence of early TDP-43 pathology that could be used as sites for future pre-symptomatic peripheral diagnosis or for target engagement in future clinical trials, and to shed further light on possible pathomechanisms underlying the diverse non-CNS symptomatology emerging in ALS.

## Methods

### Tissue provision and ethics

All tissue was requested from the Lothian NRS BioResource RTB ethical approval 15/ES/0094, covering use of residual tissue surplus to diagnostic requirements taken as standard of care with approval number SR1684. All archived formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tissue material with an NHS Lothian diagnostic code (e.g. any tissue samples taken for diagnostic or surgical purposes at any time during a patient’s life, under the care of NHS Lothian and NHS Fife) was requested for 48 patients with known diagnosis of sporadic ALS or ALS with a C9orf72 hexanucleotide repeat expansion (for detailed cohort information, see Figure 1A and Table 1 of Pattle et al., 2023). The tissue was then assessed for sufficiency by two independent histopathologists (SBP & JMG) using a single H&E. No identifiable clinical or demographic data were used in this study. Tissue provided by the BioResource had been fixed in 10% formalin for a minimum of 72 hours, then dehydrated in an ascending alcohol series (70–100%) followed by three 4-hour washes in xylene. Three successive 5-hour paraffin wax embedding stages were performed, with subsequent cooling and sectioning of tissue on a Leica microtome into 4 μm thick serial sections that were collected on Superfrost (ThermoFisher Scientific) microscope slides. Sections were dried overnight at 40°C before staining. Bleach treatment was performed (on skin cases where warranted) by incubating slides immediately post antigen retrieval in 10% hydrogen peroxide for 12 hours overnight. This step was followed by the normal protocol in full, including the additional 3% hydrogen peroxide step used to block endogenous peroxidases, following a previously published and optimised protocol (Lawrence Li et al., 1999).

### Immunohistochemistry and BaseScopeTM in situ hybridisation

Sections were dewaxed using successive xylene washes followed by alcohol hydration and treatment with picric acid for removal of formalin pigment. For TDP-43APT staining, antigen retrieval was carried out in citric acid buffer (pH 6) in a pressure cooker for 15 min, after which immunostaining was as described previously (Spence et al., 2024, Waldron et al., 2024). In situ hybridisation was performed on tissue sections using BaseScopeTM reagents (Advanced Cell Diagnostics) as per the manufacturer’s instructions (Wang et al., 2012) and adapted as described previously (Gregory et al., 2020a). Probe hybridisation was performed using BaseScopeTM probes for STMN-2 CE mRNA transcripts (catalogue number 1048231-C1). Slides were counterstained using haematoxylin and lithium carbonate, washed in xylene, and coverslips were mounted using DPX Mountant. Slides were imaged using an EVOS M5000 microscope using brightfield settings at 40x magnification.

## Results

### Improved sensitivity of TDP-43APT facilitates wider detection of TDP-43 pathology in peripheral tissues and cell types

Using a more sensitive and specific tool (TDP-43APT) than currently available antibodies to detect TDP-43 pathology, we examined tissue from 8 organ systems of 13 people who later went on to develop ALS (sALS n=12, C9-ALS n=1; for detailed cohort information, see Figure 1A and Table 1 of Pattle et al., 2023). Many organs that we have shown previously to contain TDP-43 aggregates (using less sensitive antibody-based methods) such as the gastrointestinal tract (gallbladder and colon) and lymph nodes (Pattle et al., 2023) also exhibited TDP-43 pathology here, as defined by the presence of both TDP-43 aggregates and STMN-2 cryptic exons (Figure 1A). In addition, now using TDP-43APT, we now show (i) TDP-43 pathology in muscle biopsies, that was previously not identifiable, and (ii) more consistently detectable TDP-43 pathology in all skin (n=7/8 biopsies from a total of 7 patients; previously 2/8 biopsies from 7 patients in Pattle et al., 2023) and lymph node biopsies (2/2; previously 1/2 in Pattle et al., 2023) in the cohort. Organs for which we detected no evidence of TDP-43 pathology included the pleura, the bladder, the female reproductive tract and the thyroid (Figure 1A). Whilst we did not detect TDP-43 in these organs with the TDP-43 RNA aptamer (or previously with TDP-43 antibodies; Pattle et al., 2023), the numbers of individuals and samples for these organ systems were low, therefore further work to investigate involvement of these under-represented organs may be warranted.

### TDP-43APT consistently detects TDP-43 pathology in skin biopsies with improved accuracy compared to antibody approaches

Seven individuals had undergone a skin biopsy prior to symptom onset, ranging from 48 months prior to symptom onset through to the same year as symptom onset (median 24 months; Figure 1B). We detected TDP-43 pathology by TDP-43APT staining in all seven individuals (Supplementary figure 1), most frequently observing pathology within dendritic cells of the dermis, peripheral nerve bundles and small dermal blood vessels (Figure 2A). However, one individual had two biopsies, one with pathology and one without (Figure 2B). This individual, who had a C9orf72 mutation, had two biopsies taken from the same site (case 29; Supplementary Figure 1). The first biopsy was taken 36 months prior to symptom onset and the second was taken from the same site 12 months prior to symptom onset (Figure 2B). The first biopsy showed no evidence of TDP-43 pathology, however, the second biopsy showed florid TDP-43 pathology within the dendritic cells of the dermis, the sebocytes within the sebaceous glands and within the peripheral nerve bundles surrounding the sebaceous glands. The peripheral nerve pathology was confirmed using STMN-2 cryptic exon in situ hybridisation probes detecting neuron-specific TDP-43 loss of function (Figure 2C).

### Muscle biopsy reveals TDP-43 pathology in myocytes and surrounding neurovascular bundles

pTDP-43 antibody staining failed to identify TDP-43 aggregates in both muscle biopsies in this cohort (Figure 3A). However, a serial section from each biopsy stained with TDP-43APT revealed TDP-43 puncta within, (i) the cytoplasm of the myocytes (sarcoplasm), and (ii) the myocyte nuclei (myonuclei) which are dispersed along the inner surface of the sarcolemma at the periphery of each fibre (Figure 3A & 3B). Furthermore, TDP-43APT pathology can be visualised within the neurons of the peripheral nerve bundles running between fascicles (Figure 3C), confirmed by in situ hybridisation detecting a neuron-specific splice target of TDP-43 called STMN-2 (Figure 3D). Loss of TDP-43 function (by virtue of its pathogenic aggregation state) leads to loss of cryptic exon suppression and therefore STMN-2 cryptic exons can be detected by in situ hybridisation (Figure 3D; Supplementary Figure 2).

### Improved sensitivity of TDP-43APT facilitates detection of TDP-43 pathology in a wider range of cell types including sebocytes and myocytes

In addition to the extensive list of non-CNS cell types from which we were able to identify pathological TDP-43 using classical antibody approaches (i.e. dendritic cells, macrophages, endothelial cells, T-cells and chondrocytes; see Pattle et al., 2023), improved sensitivity of TDP-43APT here allowed for the detection of pathological TDP-43 sebocytes (see Figure 2B), and myocytes (see Figure 3B), not previously detected.

### Aggregate-laden macrophages present across non-CNS tissues

Amongst non-CNS tissues examined here, we note the presence of morphologically abnormal macrophages. Aberrant morphologies include aggregate-laden macrophages, staining positively for TDP-43 pathology, and macrophages full of large vacuoles indicative of lysosomal dysfunction. We observed such aberrant macrophage morphologies in gastrointestinal tract, skin, and lymph node specimens (Figure 5) – all of which were positive for pathological TDP-43 detected with RNA aptamer.

### Blood vessel involvement by TDP-43 pathology in pre-symptomatic ALS

All of the peripheral biopsy samples in the cohort included a contingent of small blood vessels (mostly arterioles, venules and small veins as per normal anatomical composition of tissues). Within the samples with demonstrated TDP-43 pathology, we identified variable blood vessels (including veins, venules and arterioles) that were both involved and uninvolved by TDP-43 pathology (Figure 4). Smooth muscle and endothelial cells both exhibit evidence of TDP-43 pathology in different organs, with skin representing the most accessible site biopsied.

## Discussion

In the past 20 years there have been over 125 clinical trials, investigating 76 drugs and recruiting more than 15 000 people with ALS. Whilst our understanding of the pathobiology underpinning ALS over this timeframe has vastly improved, translational success has been much less apparent (Wong et al., 2021). The reasons for this are multifactorial but there are several key missing factors in our pursuit of translational success, the first is targeting individuals sufficiently early, prior to substantial neuronal damage, and the second is having access to accurate biomarkers to do this. Targeting of the central nervous system for early diagnosis is not feasible or scalable for point of care biofluid and/or biopsy testing and may only yield diagnostic results too late in the disease process − indeed, between 50-70% of motor neurons can be lost prior to symptom onset (Rowland and Shneider, 2001).

Given the recent increase in awareness of systemic symptoms affecting people with ALS in non-CNS organs such as the skin (Hayashi et al., 2007, Nolano et al., 2024, Wallace et al., 2014) and the gastrointestinal tract (Beswick et al., 2023, Conroy et al., 2023, Günther et al., 2016, Marsden et al., 2023, Nakamura et al., 2012, Niu et al., 2025, Nübling et al., 2014, Samara et al., 2021, The ALS Association, 2019, Wallace et al., 2014), these peripheral tissues may represent a more readily accessible site for biopsy. Our previous approach, to assess peripheral tissues from people who later went on to develop ALS, employed antibodies to detect pathologically phosphorylated TDP-43 (Pattle et al., 2023) which whilst specific, lacks sensitivity missing early aggregation events (see Spence et al., 2024). Recently we have developed two new tools to improve sensitivity in detecting TDP-43 pathology. The first, a TDP-43 RNA aptamer, which binds only to pathological TDP-43, but with improved sensitivity due to its small size and ability to decorate the surface of pathological aggregates (Spence et al., 2024). The second is an in situ hybridisation tool used to detect an alternate splice form of STMN-2 that only arises when TDP-43 splice function is lost (Spence et al., 2024). Using these tools, we now have unprecedented power to detect TDP-43 loss- and gain-of-function pathology, in readily accessible organs with evidence of early TDP-43 pathology, for future pre-symptomatic ALS diagnosis.

Organs with evidence of pathological TDP-43 aggregation and TDP-43 loss-of-function, were colon, skin, muscle, blood vessels, gallbladder, and lymph node; pathological TDP-43 aggregation was detectable in tissues taken long before motor symptom onset was recorded clinically. Amongst the seven people who went on to develop ALS for whom skin biopsies were taken prior to disease diagnosis, all seven were positive for pathological TDP-43. In one case of note, an individual with a C9orf72 mutation had two temporally distinct biopsies from the same skin site. The first biopsy (a diagnostic excision biopsy of skin from the chest, containing a squamous cell carcinoma in situ) was taken 36 months prior to symptom onset. 24 months later, 12 months prior to diagnosis with ALS – a second biopsy (a further diagnostic excision biopsy, containing a benign squamoproliferative lesion) was taken from the same anatomical site as the first. We observed no pathological TDP-43 in the first biopsy but found florid TDP-43 pathology in the second biopsy (taken 12 months prior to diagnosis), identifying a phenoconversion time-window within which skin TDP-43 pathology emerged. Whilst this is only one case, it demonstrates the potential of peripheral biopsy sites as readily accessible and pathologically relevant sites for future diagnosis in ALS. Furthermore, the presence of TDP-43 pathology within the sebaceous glands is reminiscent of individuals with Parkinson’s disease (PD), where pre-symptomatic alpha-synuclein pathology can be detected from skin samples (Kuang et al., 2024), and lipidomic analysis on skin swabs can differentiate people with PD, Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and healthy subjects (Briganti et al., 2021). This raises the further possibility of a skin-swab point of care test for neurodegenerative diseases outside of the context of PD.

Blood vessels, along with parts of peripheral nerve bundles, are contained in most peripheral tissue biopsies as per the normal anatomical composition of tissues. In this study, we identified TDP-43 pathology in both vascular and nerve structures. Indeed, vascular TDP-43 pathology has been reported previously, with recent observations demonstrating pathological TDP-43 in the cholesterol plaques of atherosclerotic lesions (Umahara et al., 2020) and demonstrating an association with comorbid moderate-to-severe brain arteriolosclerosis (Katsumata et al., 2018). Recently (Omar et al., 2025) endothelial cell nuclear TDP-43 was found to be reduced in ALS, frontotemporal dementia (FTD) and AD human prefrontal cortex postmortem samples. Furthermore, other groups have demonstrated that loss of TDP-43 function in endothelial cells can lead to classical neuropathological features of neurodegenerative diseases such as neuroinflammation (Arribas et al., 2024) and loss of blood-brain-barrier integrity (Cheemala et al., 2023), and blood-brain barrier associated gene pathways (Omar et al., 2025). Other aggregation-prone proteins, such as beta-amyloid, have also been observed to behave similarly, contributing to small vessel disease (Muir and Smith, 2024). Whether TDP-43 pathology, in this same location, could be contributing to a wider systemic vasculopathy in the same way as beta-amyloid, remains to be proven. However, the ability to detect TDP-43 in end-organ blood vessels, does hold hope for developing peripheral blood- and/or biopsy-based diagnostic tests that could act as early biomarkers of disease in those at risk.

Skeletal muscles represent another organ type with potential for readily accessible peripheral biopsy. In our cohort, we previously did not observe TDP-43 pathology in skeletal muscle biopsies using classical antibody approaches (see Figure 2A of Pattle et al., 2023), although other groups have demonstrated detection of TDP-43 pathology using pTDP-43 antibodies (Cykowski et al., 2018, Kurashige et al., 2022, Mori et al., 2019, Zhang et al., 2024) in muscle. However, using TDP-43APT here, we do observe TDP-43 pathology in the two skeletal muscle biopsies in our cohort, in similar pathological distributions to that demonstrated in other cohorts, i.e. within the sarcoplasm of myocytes and within their myonuclei, located on the inside surface of the sarcolemma (Zhang et al., 2024). We also demonstrate staining within the adjacent neurovascular bundles. Not only do we demonstrate TDP-43APT pathology in skeletal muscle here, but we also found loss-of-function cryptic exon events within the nerve bundles and blood vessels between myocyte units. Whilst physiological TDP-43 misfolding has been observed in myocytes the context of excitotoxicity and hyperexcitability (Versluys et al., 2022, Weskamp et al., 2020), the presence of loss-of-function events in the intervening neurovascular bundles, out with the muscle unit, are indicative of a pathological process. This site is not only readily biopsied, but also often carried out in the diagnostic work up of a person with ALS once symptoms have started. This could therefore be an optimal site for early confirmatory diagnosis, rather than patients experiencing long diagnostic delays once their symptoms have begun.

Amongst non-CNS tissues we observed the presence of abnormal macrophage morphologies including aggregate- and vacuole-laden macrophages, consistent with histological evidence of lysosomal dysfunction. These aberrant morphologies were observed in gastrointestinal tract, skin, and lymph node specimens. Vacuole-laden macrophages are a characteristic histopathological feature of lysosomal dysfunction, which has been implicated in the onset and development of FTD and ALS (reviewed in Root et al., 2021). The significance of our observation of lysosomal dysfunction in some non-CNS tissues prior to ALS symptom onset is not clear at this point, and warrants further investigation, but from our limited evidence here a role for lysosomal dysfunction early in development of ALS cannot be ruled out.

Recent research has revealed an association between higher income and a younger onset of ALS, suggested to be attributable to socioeconomic factors affecting the timing of diagnosis rather than the likelihood of the condition (Shojaie et al., 2024). Currently, all ALS diagnoses are made in hospital settings and often some time after motor symptom onset. Skin biopsies to test for TDP-43 pathology could represent a relatively straightforward, and arguably less intimidating, earlier ALS detection methodology with potential to increase societal health equity across socioeconomic, geographical, and health education and awareness divides. Gut biopsies, although more invasive than skin biopsies, are routinely taken from people presenting with significant GI symptoms – whilst these biopsies are primarily taken to investigate for common gut pathologies, such as inflammatory bowel disease, a gut biopsy provides an opportunity for early TDP-43 pathology detection. The significance of pre-symptomatic TDP-43 pathology in peripheral tissues of people with ALS warrants further investigation and consideration. Whilst, for Parkinson’s disease, the brain-first and body-first subtypes for disease ontogeny have garnered wide acceptance, two aspects of these staging systems are still debated; (i) whether pathology arises unifocally or multifocally, and (ii) whether olfactory bulb and enteric nervous system could be the primary sites of origin. Spreading through the Vagus nerve has been suggested as a plausible means of propagation under the body-first staging system (see Braak et al., 2003, Hawkes et al., 2007) but recent findings incorporating Lewy body positive postmortem datasets with tissues from CNS and the peripheral nervous system identify two distinct body-first subtypes, one with parasympathetic vagal predominance and another with sympathetic predominance, in equal proportion (Andersen et al., 2025). ALS associated gene carriers, such as people with C9orf72 hexanucleotide gene expansions, could monitor progression of TDP-43 pathology, with potential early detection and triaging for clinical trials. Clinical trials themselves could monitor the effect of their interventions on TDP-43 pathology using tissue biopsies, non-invasive or otherwise, as an alternative to, or in conjunction with CNS or blood monitoring. With early detection in mind, there is already evidence that pre-symptomatic TDP-43 loss-of-function can be detected through cryptic splicing events, with cryptic peptides (Irwin et al., 2024) representing an intriguing possibility for clinical sampling, as these peptides are more stable than mRNA. Tissue biopsies could supplement these approaches and/or provide direct evidence of TDP-43 pathology in situ. Therefore, more work needs to be carried out to assess the utility of non-CNS tissue biopsies for early detection of ALS, providing another tool in in the toolkit for the detection of pathology, and the monitoring of disease progression and target engagement in ALS clinical trials.

Taken together, we have identified several organs that have evidence of TDP-43 pathology prior to motor symptom onset in ALS. The cell types affected, at least in some instances, share a common cell linage as long-lived cells that derive from the neural crest (for example peripheral/enteric neurons, chondrocytes, dendritic cells, connective tissue, and smooth muscle of peripheral blood vessels). This could imply a cell-type specific predisposition to TDP-43 pathology that occurs in many organs, most of which are more readily accessible than the CNS. Understanding the distribution of the systemic pathological burden of TDP-43 not only allows us to identify people earlier but also allows us to target biofluid studies appropriately to organs that we know to be affected by TDP-43 pathology.
