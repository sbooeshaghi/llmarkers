## Abstract

SUMMARYPrecision oncology including immunotherapy with checkpoint blockers for patients with colorectal cancer (CRC) continues to lag behind other cancer types. The immune composition of the tumor microenvironment (TME) has a major impact on the therapeutic response and clinical outcome. Here, we comprehensively characterize the TME at the single-cell level by first building a large-scale atlas that integrates 4.27 million single cells from 1,670 samples and then complementing the atlas by profiling single cells with low mRNA content. The analysis of the atlas allows refined tumor classification into four immune phenotypes: immune desert, B cell enriched, T cell enriched, and myeloid enriched subtypes. Within the myeloid compartment we uncover distinct subpopulations of neutrophils that acquire new functional properties in blood and in the TME, including antigen-presenting capabilities. Further, spatial multimodal single-cell profiling reveals that neutrophils are organized in clusters within distinct multi-cellular niches. Finally, using an orthotopic mouse model we show that cancer-derived systemic signals modify neutrophil production in the bone marrow, providing evidence for tumor-induced granulopoiesis. Our study provides a high-resolution resource for CRC TME and suggests novel therapeutic strategies targeting neutrophils.

## INTRODUCTION

Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains the most common gastrointestinal cancer and the second leading cause of cancer-related death according to the GLOBOCAN data1. The growing incidence in some countries reflects a modification in lifestyle and its consequences related with “Westernization” such as diet, obesity, physical inactivity, and alcohol consumption. Similarly, “Westernization” of diets and lifestyle have been attributed to the alarming growing incidence of early-onset CRCs (individuals <50 years of age)2. Despite the improvement of early detection screening policies and the development of many novel treatments, the median overall survival (OS) for patients with metastatic CRC (mCRC) is ∼30 months3. Progress in targeted therapy for mCRC has been limited, relative to other solid cancers like lung cancer or melanoma4. The genetic heterogeneity5 as well as paucity of drugs targeting common driver genes, pose considerable challenges for developing precision oncology approaches for patients with CRC. Moreover, CRC seems to be refractory to therapy with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) with the notable exception of 4% of metastatic patients with tumors characterized by microsatellite instability (MSI) or POLE proofreading mutations6. This is somehow paradoxical since CRCs are known to be under immunological control, as we have shown in the past7. Thus, there is an urgent need to explore new immunomodulatory strategies for the vast majority of CRC patients with microsatellite stable (MSS) tumors. Strategies that convert the “immune cold” tumor microenvironment (TME) to “immune hot” could sensitize cancers to ICI therapy. A number of clinical trials with drug combinations have been carried out to overcome resistance to immunotherapy in MSS CRC8,9. The benefits of using a dual combination of ICIs (CTLA-4 and PD-1 blockade) or adding ICIs to chemotherapy appear to be very limited8,10. Combination strategies that exploit synergistic potential of ICI and targeted drugs showed increase of the anti-tumor activity, however, the objective response rates were disappointingly low8. Thus, to address this unmet clinical need a better understanding of the immunosuppressive TME and its heterogeneity is of utmost importance.

The intrinsic complexity of the interaction of the two interwoven systems, the tumor and the immune system, poses considerable challenges and requires comprehensive approaches to interrogate cancer immunity during tumor initiation and progression, and following therapeutic modulation thereof. A number of established and novel high-throughput technologies enable the generation of the necessary data and thereby provide the basis for mechanistic understanding. Specifically, next-generation sequencing (NGS) of cancer samples provide large data sets that can be mined for immunologically relevant parameters (see our reviews11,12). Using these tools we were able to chart the TME in CRC13 and developed The Cancer Immunome Atlas14. While these attempts uncovered the enormous heterogeneity of MSS CRC and revealed unique cancer cell and TME subtypes with prognostic and therapeutic relevance, the advent of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) technologies opened fundamental new avenues to dissect cancer heterogeneity. We recently developed a novel computational approach to dissect the cellular diversity in the TME at high resolution by integrating expression profiles of single cells from multiple studies using cutting-edge artificial intelligence (AI) methods15. Similar effort was recently carried out in the context of colorectal cancer16, enabling stratification of patients. However, limited by the sampling bias of the commonly used 10x Genomics platform, this atlas is lacking neutrophils. Neutrophils are the most abundant cells circulating in the human body and are emerging as important regulators of cancer17–20. Neutrophils are highly abundant in intratumoral as well as stromal regions in CRC18 and co-localize with CD8+ T cells19. Neutrophils exhibit remarkable plasticity, show different pro-tumor and anti-tumor functions20, and can acquire anti-tumor phenotype in response to immunotherapy21. The importance of neutrophil-T cell interactions is supported by the observation that CD4+ PD-1+ T cells in granulocyte-dominated cell neighborhoods are associated with favorable prognosis in CRC22. Hence, deep characterization of neutrophils in the TME, in normal tissue, and blood from CRC patients would provide novel insights into the myeloid compartment and the myeloid-lymphoid cell-cell interactions.

Here, we first integrated CRC single-cell RNA sequencing datasets spanning 4.27 million cells from 650 patients and 1,670 samples. We then carried out single-cell profiling of samples from CRC patients using optimized protocol and platform that efficiently recovers low mRNA-content immune cells. The analyses refined tumor classification into four immune phenotypes. Within the myeloid compartment we uncovered distinct subpopulations of neutrophils that acquire new functional properties in the TME including antigen-presenting capabilities (HLA-DR+ neutrophils). Furthermore, spatial multimodal single-cell profiling revealed that neutrophils are organized in clusters within distinct multi-cellular niches. Finally, using an orthotopic mouse model we show that cancer-derived systemic signals modify neutrophil production in the bone marrow, providing evidence for tumor-induced granulopoiesis.

## RESULTS

### Generation of a large-scale CRC single-cell atlas

Based on our previous approach15 we compiled CRC related scRNA-seq data from 48 studies and 76 datasets comprising 4.27 million cells from 650 patients and 1,670 samples (Figure 1A). This comprehensive CRC single-cell atlas integrates curated and quality-checked transcriptomic data from publicly available studies, complemented with extensive metadata curation, and encompasses the full spectrum of disease progression, from normal colon to polyps, primary tumors, and metastases, covering both early and advanced stages of CRC (see STAR Methods, Figures 1B-C and Supplementary Figure S1A-E). Study characteristics are summarized in Table S1. In total, the atlas includes transcriptomic data from 487 CRC patients (median: 58 years [22-91]), 99 patients with polyps (median: 60 years [40-75]), and 64 control individuals (median: 57 years [23-79]). Of the 487 CRC patients, 254 had colon cancer (COAD), 63 rectal cancer (READ), and 170 were not otherwise specified. Of these, 89 were classified as microsatellite instable (MSI), 268 as microsatellite stable (MSS), and 130 had an unknown microsatellite status. For disease stage 41 patients were classified as TNM stage I, 121 as stage II, 171 as stage III, and 110 as stage IV, while the stage was not reported for 44 patients. CRC samples include tissue of the primary tumor (n=674) or CRC liver metastasis (n=175) that were obtained by surgical resection. Among the control samples, 284 were derived from distant non-malignant tissue of colon tumor patients, of which 220 have a patient-matched tumor sample. 186 non-tumor control samples as well as 116 polyp and 90 matched normal samples were obtained from patients undergoing colonoscopy. Further, 103 blood samples of CRC patients as well as 24 samples derived from tumor affected lymph nodes, with another 18 non-tumor affected lymph node samples were included. Of the control patients none had a history of inflammatory bowel diseases.

Although neutrophils play a prominent role in cancer biology, they are underrepresented in most available scRNA-seq datasets generated with the widely used 10x Genomics technology23 (Figure S1 F). To address this, we generated a scRNA-seq dataset from peripheral blood, adjacent normal tissue, and tumor tissue of 12 CRC patients using the BD Rhapsody platform and our optimized protocol24 that allows capture of cells with very low mRNA content23. The data was then integrated into the final high-resolution single-cell atlas for CRC. Overall, the atlas integrates 4,264,946 single cells, which are annotated to 12 coarse cell type identities and 62 major cell subtypes or cell states (e.g. dividing cells) based on previously established canonical single-cell signatures including 1,146,196 epithelial cells, 1,552,108 T/NK cells, 804,042 B/Plasma cells, 385,589 myeloid cells, and 33,9767 stromal and endothelial cells (Figure 1D). Cancer cells (in total 600,740 cells, from primary tumor and metastatic tissue) in the atlas showed high heterogeneity in their transcriptomic profiles (Figure 1B). Due to the large patient cohort, we were able to apply high-resolution cancer cell classification based on their specific marker gene expression signatures (Figure S1D). We divided the following main clusters: 1) colonocyte-like (FABP1, CEACAM7, ANPEP, SI), 2) BEST4 (BEST4, OTOP2, SPIB), Goblet-like (MUC2, FCGBP, ZG16, ATOH1), 3) Crypt-like (LGR5, SMOC2), and 4) TA-like (TOP2A, UBE2C, PCLAF, HELLS, TK1) (Figure 1D, Figure S1C). There are highly mitotic/proliferative clusters (LGR5, TOP2A, MKI67) of both COAD and READ that resemble highly aggressive and invasive cancer cells. In addition, EPCAM expressing cells in the blood of CRC patients were annotated as circulating cancer cells.

With a total of 2,741,739 immune cells, this single-cell atlas enables high-resolution classification of immune cell types across both innate and adaptive subsets (Figure 1C, Figure S1A-C). These include T cells - CD4 (CD4), CD8 (CD8A), and T regulatory cells (FOXP3) - as well as NK cells (NKG7, TYROBP). B cells are represented as naïve (IGHD, TCL1A), memory (CD27,CD44), and activated (CD69, CD83) subsets, while plasma cells (JCHAIN) express immunoglobulins such as IgA (IGHA1), IgG (IGHG1), and IgM (IGHM). The myeloid lineage encompasses monocytes (VCAN, CD14), macrophages (C1QA, C1QB), mast cells (TPSB2, CPA3), and dendritic cells, including classical (CLEC9A, CD1C) and plasmacytoid subsets (IL3RA, GZMB), as well as eosinophils (CLC, CCR3) and neutrophils (CXCR2, FCGR3B). Specialized populations such as gamma-delta T cells (TRDV1, TRGC1) and innate lymphoid cells (ILCs; IL4I1, KIT) are also depicted, along with immune cell developmental stages, such as progenitors (CD24, AXL) and stem-like cells (LEF1), and cycling states (MKI67, CDK1).

### Single-cell composition of the TME highlights distinct immune infiltration patterns in primary CRC tumors

Utilizing the high-resolution atlas, we stratified 284 CRC untreated patients with primary tumor samples based on the infiltration patterns of their tumor microenvironment (TME). The high number of patients enabled us to perform unsupervised clustering of batch-corrected cell-type fractions, revealing four major immune clusters with distinct subclusters (Figure 2A and Supplementary Figure 2A): 1) immune deserted tumors (ID; i.e. no significant immune cell infiltration but high cancer cell fraction); 2) the subtype of tumors with B cell dominance (B; B cell, plasma IgA and IgG cell); 3) the subtype of tumors with myeloid dominance (M; macrophage/monocyte); and 4) subtype of tumors with T cell dominance (T; CD8+, CD4+, T regulatory cells). Using logistic regression, we did not find any association of the four different patient strata to tumor stages (early I/II vs. advanced III/IV), sex, or other features. However, patients in the M (p=0.013) or B (p=0.089) subcluster were associated with a higher likelihood of being in an early tumor stage (I/II). In addition, the CD8 (p=0.026) subcluster was associated with a higher likelihood of the tumor being MSI compared to MSS.

In order to identify tumor-cell based TME imprinting characteristics, we next analyzed differentially enriched pathways25 in the cancer cells of each immune infiltration type. The immune B and M subtype showed significant downregulation (FDR < 0.1) of the androgen pathway (Figure 2B).

### Integration of bulk RNA-seq data reveals genotype-immune phenotype associations

The majority of the scRNA-seq studies lack both comprehensive cancer genotype information and survival data. In contrast, the TCGA reference dataset includes this information, alongside bulk RNA-sequencing data. Using the computational method SCISSOR26, we integrated bulk RNA-seq data from TCGA with single-cell transcriptomic signatures to assess the relationship between specific genotypes and survival outcomes in CRC (Figure 3A). We evaluated the association of cell type transcriptomic signatures derived from the atlas with genotype and survival data from the TCGA reference dataset, which includes 629 patients (UICC I-IV)5. Building on our previous work that identified genomic features influencing immune phenotypes in a pan-cancer study using bulk RNA-seq data14, we leveraged the high resolution of the single-cell CRC atlas for a more detailed analysis of these determinants. We therefore analyzed the cell type transcriptomic signatures and their association with survival in 629 patients from the TCGA cohort. Overall, the presence of high levels of IgA+ plasma cells, CD4+ T cells CD8+ T cells and B cells showed a prominent association with improved survival in CRC patients. Conversely, CRC patients with tumors enriched in Schwann cells were associated with worst survival outcomes, as also reported previously30. Notably, high infiltration of various endothelial cells and fibroblasts also emerged as strong negative predictors of survival (Figure 3A). Mutations in BRAF and KRAS were associated with distinct immune infiltrate patterns (Figure 3B-C). For example, CRC patients with BRAF mutations exhibited high infiltration of cDC1 and cDC2, as well as increased levels of IgM+ plasma cells. Similarly, IgM+ plasma cell infiltration was also elevated in patients with KRAS mutations. Conversely, BRAF mutations were linked to low levels of Plasma IgA infiltration, while KRAS mutations were associated with high Plasma IgA infiltration. Additionally, MSI and MSS status displayed opposing cell type infiltration patterns (Figure S3A and S3B).

Finally, we identified gene signatures based on the patient immune infiltration groups by selecting the top 15 differentially expressed genes. These gene signatures were then used to conduct a survival analysis utilizing data from the TCGA COAD-READ cohorts (n = 629). The Kaplan-Meier curves in Figure 2D demonstrate that higher expression of the gene signatures of the B (p-value = 0.0015), M (p-value = 0.0096), and T (p-value = 0.00078) subgroups is significantly correlated with improved overall survival.

### Comprehensive characterization of neutrophils reveals highly diverse and plastic phenotypes

We then carried out comprehensive characterization of the data we generated using the BD Rhapsody platform. The obtained scRNA-seq dataset comprises 126,991 single cells categorized into 23 main cell types based on specific marker genes, including 55,245 epithelial/tumor cells, 4,428 stromal/endothelial cells, and 65,782 immune cells, including 30,924 neutrophils (Figure 4A). Compositional analysis using scRNA-seq and flow cytometry showed high concordance when comparing blood, normal, and tumor tissue samples (Figure 4B). As expected, neutrophils were the most abundant leukocyte subtype in blood samples (60–70%), followed by T lymphocytes and monocytes (Figure 4C). Normal-adjacent tissue was dominated by epithelial cells, while tumor tissue contained a markedly higher proportion of immune cells. Notably, neutrophils comprised ∼20% of all cells and ∼40% of leukocytes in tumor tissue but were present only in small numbers in normal-adjacent tissue (Figure 4C). Also within the granulocytes lineage, neutrophils were the most abundant cells (26.2% of all cells) followed by eosinophils (1.2%) and mast cells (0.4%).

We observed distinct transcriptomic differences between blood versus tumor-associated neutrophils (TAN) (Figure 4D) with 2,214 genes differentially expressed (padj<0.05; Figure 4E). We evaluated previously described marker genes distinguishing TAN from normal tissue neutrophils (NAN) across cancers, including OLR1, VEGFA, CXCR2, and PLAU27. TAN markers (OLR1, PLAU, VEGFA) were highly upregulated in TAN, whereas CXCR2, a marker of mature neutrophils, was highly expressed in blood neutrophils but downregulated in TAN (Figure 4F). Spatial analysis of Lectin-type oxidized LDL receptor 1 (LOX-1; encoded by OLR1) expression using multiplex immunofluorescence (IF) staining demonstrated the presence of segregated LOX-1⁺ neutrophil cell clusters exclusively in CRC tumor tissue, but not in normal-adjacent colonic tissue (Figure 4G; Supplementary Figure S4A) LOX-1⁺ cells co-express the neutrophil marker CD15). Of note, LOX-1 has previously been identified as a marker for TAN in lung cancer15, indicating LOX-1 as a putative tumor-agnostic TAN marker.

Sub-clustering of neutrophils revealed three distinct blood neutrophil clusters (BN1-BN3) and four TAN clusters (TAN1-TAN4) (Figure 4H). Trajectory analysis indicated a progressive transition from BN1 to BN3 and subsequently to TAN1 (Figure 4I), demonstrating that neutrophil migration from the bloodstream into tissue is accompanied by distinct transcriptomic changes. From TAN1, neutrophils diverged into two trajectories: one leading to a putative pro-tumor TAN phenotype (TAN4) characterized by high IL1A expression, a key driver of emergency myelopoiesis that fuels inflammation and immunosuppression in the TME28, and another toward a phenotype defined by high expression of antigen-presentation-associated markers, such as HLA-DRA (TAN2/TAN3) (Figure 4J). Neutrophils with antigen-presenting capabilities, referred to as hybrid neutrophils27, have previously been identified in early-stage lung cancer and are associated with an anti-tumor phenotype15,29. Interestingly, the expression of HLA-DRA in blood or tissue-resident neutrophils was significantly higher in early-stage CRC compared to advanced-stage CRC (Figure 4K), suggesting that these antigen-presenting neutrophils may be diminished as the tumor progresses to more advanced stages, potentially contributing to immune evasion. Finally, we validated HLA-DR protein expression using flow cytometry and confirmed that the expression was markedly higher in TAN compared to both blood neutrophils and NAN (Figure 4L).

### Spatial single-cell multi-modal profiling reveals neutrophil clusters within multicellular niches

Increasing evidence indicates that immune cells in the TME occupy spatially distinct areas or patches characterized by specific cellular compositions and discrete histological properties: so-called cellular neighborhoods22 or cell niches. Evidence for this type of segregated myeloid populations in CRC was recently shown using a limited immunofluorescence (IF) panel30 as well as in our data (Figure 4G). In order to quantify spatial niches we analyzed the TME of MSS CRC patients using a spatial transcriptomic technique with 380 genes and sub-cellular resolution (10x Genomics Xenium). The spatial single-cell transcriptomic profiling revealed distinct spatial architecture and cell type composition in both, center of the tumor and the invasive margin (Supplementary Figure 5A). Spatial co-localization of T cell and B cell clusters pinpointed tertiary lymphoid structures in these samples (Figure S5B-C). Neutrophils were the other immune cell type that was organized as clusters in the tumor and the invasive margin (Figure 5B). This spatial organization of neutrophils was confirmed using immunohistochemistry (IHC) stainings of the tissue from the same patients (Supplementary Figure 5D).

In order to quantify spatial multicellular interactions we used the graph deep-learning method NicheCompass that utilizes existing knowledge of inter- and intracellular interaction pathways to identify distinct cellular niches31. At the resolution level of two niches, we identified cancer and non-cancer niches (Supplementary Figure 5E), whereas at the resolution of three niches we identified cancer, boundary, and stroma niches (Supplementary Figure 5F). At the resolution level of ten we quantified five cancer-cell dominated cell niches (CN1-CN5), two fibroblast-dominated niches (CN6-7) and a plasma-cell-enriched niche (CN8) comprising the tumor-stroma boundary regions, a stromal niche (CN9) and an immune-cell dominated niche that identified tertiary lymphoid structures (CN10) (Figure 5A). The cancer-cell dominated niche CN5 was enriched with macrophages and neutrophils (Figure 5B). Analysis of cancer cells within this niche revealed elevated expression of the chemokines CXCL1, CXCL3, CXCL10, and CXCL11, along with the immunomodulatory enzyme IDO1 (Figure 5C). Neutrophils may amplify IDO1 expression in cancer cells32, contributing to an immunosuppressive milieu. The identified chemokines, which recruit neutrophils through CXCR2 and CXCR3 signaling33, likely play a central role in driving cancer-cell mediated localized neutrophil aggregation and cluster formation.

We then used an orthogonal method based on imaging mass-cytometry (IMC) and profiled FFPE samples from patients with stage III colon cancer treated with adjuvant chemotherapy (CAPOX) and for whom relapse and overall survival data were available (n=30). We previously developed and evaluated a panel of antibodies for IMC on FFPE samples for a comprehensive overview of the TME and cancer-immune cell interactions34, including lineage and functional immune cell markers, surrogates of cancer cell states (proliferation, apoptosis) and structural markers (epithelium, stroma, vessels). Following data-driven identification of single-cell phenotypes segmentation, and image analysis, we quantified the densities of major cell types (Figure 5D). The spatial protein profiling revealed similar cellular composition of the TME as identified using single-cell mRNA profiling. Given the limited number of features, quantification of cell niches based on protein expression was not possible. We therefore used a statistical approach to define niches and applied cell neighborhood analysis by defining cell nuclei and associating polygons (Voronoi diagram) to each nucleus34 (see Methods), thereby allowing cells of different sizes and distances to be assessed as neighbors. We used a permutation approach34 to identify pairwise interactions between cell phenotypes that occurred more or less frequently than expected by chance. This spatial information revealed neutrophil clusters similar to the clusters quantified using transcriptomic data (Figure 5E). It appears that the neutrophil clusters are more frequent in the tumor compared to the invasive margin, albeit the differences in this cohort were not significant (Figure 5H). Analyses of the survival data showed that the neutrophil clusters in both regions are associated with worse prognosis (Figure 5I).

Overall, the multimodal profiling revealed that neutrophils are not randomly distributed but are localized in spatial clusters. Moreover, neutrophil clusters are situated within distinct multicellular niches composed of different fractions of cancer cells, endothelial cells, and other immune cells.

### Mouse neutrophils recapitulate human neutrophil phenotypes in various compartments

To carry out functional studies and define underlying mechanisms, we deployed an orthotopic mouse model based on tumor organoid transplantation (Figure 6A-B). AKPS organoids – harboring the four most common CRC-driving mutations (Apc-LOF (loss of function), p53-LOF, Kras-GOF (gain of function), Smad4-LOF) – were successfully engrafted following colonoscopy-guided injection into the colonic mucosa of C57BL/6JRj mice35 (Supplementary Figure 6A). Tumors developed within six to eight weeks (Figure 6C) and progressively expanded through the colonic layers toward the peritoneal cavity (Supplementary Figure 6B), mirroring some of the invasive characteristics of late-stage human CRC. Notably, neutrophil infiltration was significantly higher in the tumor compared to the healthy colon (Figure 6D-F). Interestingly, there was already an increase of neutrophils within the blood of AKPS-tumor bearing compared to naive mice (Figure 6G) suggesting a systemic effect during tumor development. Analysis of neutrophil activation markers like Siglec-F, Cd11b, CD80 and PD-L1 further showed a progressive activation of neutrophils from the blood to the tumor (Figure 6G-J) while MHC-II proteins were upregulated in tumor and colon tissue compared to blood neutrophils (Figure 6L). Overall, the results confirm the validity of the ortothopic mouse model for human CRC.

### Cross-species analyses reveal tumor-induced granulopoiesis in CRC

In order to carry out cross-species analyses of mouse and human CRC neutrophils, we performed scRNA-seq on CD45-enriched leukocytes isolated from tumors and blood of mice six weeks post-AKPS-injection. Colons and blood of naïve (control) mice were further included as controls.

Additionally CD45-enriched leukocytes were isolated from the bone-marrow of both tumor and non-tumor bearing mice. Neutrophils from all tissues and phenotypes were extracted and integrated (Figures 7A andB). Clustering analysis identified 10 clusters with distinct transcriptional patterns (Figures 7C and D) and a high concordance of BN and TAN marker genes derived from our human neutrophil dataset (Supplementary Figure 7A), especially within the colon and tumor specific clusters. We then performed pseudotime trajectory analysis, which showed a progressive transition of neutrophils from the bone-marrow to the blood and colonic tissues (Figures 7E and F). Differential gene expression (DEG) analysis between tumor and healthy bone-marrow and blood revealed that neutrophils already changed their transcriptional phenotype prior to their recruitment into the tumor tissue (Figures 7G and H). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) of KEGG terms showed changes in the metabolic phenotypes (Supplementary Figure 7C). Neutrophils from the tumor bone-marrow upregulated multiple genes encoding for ribosomal proteins (Rps2, Rpl32, Rpsa, Rps27l) and proteins involved in cell proliferation36 (Ccnb2, Mcm4, Pcna, Fen1). Overall, the results from the single-cell profiling suggest tumor-induced granulopoiesis which is likely driven by secreted factors.

To provide further evidence for tumor-induced granulopoiesis we carried out scRNA-seq profiling using blood samples from healthy volunteers and compared the expression profiles of neutrophils with the expression profiles of blood neutrophils from the CRC patients (Figure 7I). The results showed profound changes in the expression with 2287 genes differentially expressed (padj<0.05; Figure 7J). GSEA on KEGG terms indicated changes in the metabolic phenotypes. We then carried out functional experiments with neutrophils isolated from blood of healthy donors and treated the neutrophils with supernatants from a colorectal adenocarcinoma cell line (CaCO) and a non-small cell lung cancer cell line (SW900). Seahorse analysis showed different O2 consumption rates (Figure 7K), indicating context-dependent metabolic rewiring of the neutrophils. Together, these findings provide evidence of tumor-induced granulopoiesis and metabolic rewiring which is likely driven by secreted factors from the TME.

## DISCUSSION

We built a large-scale atlas of single-cell transcriptomes of CRC through integration of 4.27 million cells from 650 patients and 1,670 samples representing 7 billion expression values. We then complemented the atlas with single-cell data enriched with immune cells with low-mRNA content including neutrophils, and carried out multi-modal spatail profiling at the single-cell level of the TME in CRC. Our unique resource provides novel biological findings and has important translational relevance.

First, we provide a high-resolution view of the TME in CRC with 61 major cell types/states and show different cell type composition patterns including more precise functional transcriptomic classification of malignant epithelial cells. The single-cell composition of the TME in CRC enabled refined tumor classification and patient stratification into four immune phenotypes: immune deserted, myeloid, B cell, and T cell subtypes. These findings may have important implications for enabling cancer immunotherapy in CRC. For example, combination therapies that target both, myeloid cells and lymphoid cells could represent an immunotherapeutic strategy to treat M subtypes as shown recently in a melanoma mouse model37. Similarly, given the heterogeneity of the intratumoral B cells and the importance of tertiary lymphoid structures38, further analysis of the B cell subtypes might open promising therapeutic avenues by additional refined B cell targeting.

Second, we characterized for the first time neutrophil subsets in various compartments in CRC patients and show remarkable diversity and plasticity. Of particular interest for cancer immunotherapy is the TAN phenotype with immunogenic antigen-presenting feature. This observation implies acquisition of antigen presenting-like properties by neutrophils at the tumor site, as previously reported39. Such conversion of neutrophils to antigen presenting cells may elicit anti-tumor immunity and has recently been shown in a murine model40.

Third, multi-modal spatial profiling revealed that neutrophils build clusters of cells within distinct multicellular niches. Similar neutrophil clustering has been recently observed in the context of wound healing using a zebrafish model41. The driving force for this type of myeloid structures remains unknown and encourages further studies in the context of CRC and possibly other solid cancers.

Finally, using an orthotopic mouse model and cross-species analyses, we show that tumor-derived signals modulate the neutrophils in the bone-marrow, implicating tumor-induced granulopoiesis. Expression and functional data indicate metabolic rewiring in the bone marrow and imprinting the neutrophil cell fate via progenitors.

Beyond these biological insights, the results from this study have also important translational implications. As shown here, TANs can acquire antigen presenting properties and such conversion of abundant neutrophils to antigen-presenting cells could overcome the limitations of the low abundance of cross-presenting DCs. Additionally, our study highlights CRC as a systemic disease that requires therapeutic strategies beyond the primary disease site. Such a strategy was recently shown for lung cancer42. However, as we show here, the disease context has profound effects on the neutrophil phenotypes and hence, requires further disease-specific investigations.

In conclusion, we provide a CRC atlas with single-cell resolution as well as a web portal that enables interactive exploration of the dataset through cell-x-gene (https://crc.icbi.at) that allows visualization of metadata and gene expression. The biological insights we present here and future discoveries arising from the exploitation of the high-resolution CRC atlas could provide the basis for developing combination therapies for CRC patients who are not sufficiently responding to immune checkpoint blockers.
