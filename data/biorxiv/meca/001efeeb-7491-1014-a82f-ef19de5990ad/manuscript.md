## Abstract

AbstractProtein phosphatases constitute a critical superfamily of signalling enzymes, yet their tissue- and tumour-specific roles remain incompletely defined. Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH) is a rare and poorly understood kidney malignancy that lacks validated biomarkers or therapeutic targets. To address this gap, a phosphatase-wide, multi-omic integration of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), supported by validation across ten independent GEO microarray cohorts, systematically charted the human phosphatome across cancers. This analysis pinpointed the dual-specificity phosphatase DUSP15 as the most KICH-selective phosphatase (Tau = 0.977), showing near-exclusive expression and outstanding discriminatory performance (AUC = 0.975; OR = 4.83; p = 6.2e-7). Transcriptome-wide correlation and enrichment analyses revealed that DUSP15 expression is associated with activation of oxidative phosphorylation, MYC targets, and DNA repair, together with suppression of interferon and inflammatory signalling. Immune deconvolution confirmed reduced infiltration of regulatory T cells, dendritic cells, and overall immune content, consistent with an immune-desert phenotype. Single-cell RNA-seq further localised DUSP15 expression to a rare regenerative epithelial population enriched for acid–base transport genes. Collectively, these findings identify DUSP15 as a tumour-selective phosphatase that shapes immune-metabolic tumour programming in KICH, underscoring the utility of integrative multi-omics for revealing novel biomarkers and regulatory axes in rare cancers.

## Introduction

Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH) is a rare histological subtype of renal cell carcinoma (RCC), accounting for approximately 5% of all renal malignancies (1). While generally associated with an indolent clinical course, a subset of patients with advanced or metastatic disease exhibits resistance to conventional therapies and lacks effective targeted options (1,2). In contrast to clear cell and papillary RCC, KICH remains profoundly understudied, with no molecular biomarkers currently approved for diagnosis, prognosis, or therapeutic stratification (3). This critical knowledge gap underscores the urgent need for tumour-specific molecular markers that can illuminate the biology of KICH and guide precision oncology efforts.

Dual-specificity phosphatases (DUSPs) are key regulators of cellular signalling, modulating MAPK cascades, metabolic homeostasis, and immune responses through their ability to dephosphorylate both tyrosine and serine/threonine residues (4–7). Although several DUSPs have been implicated in cancer biology, their tumour-type specificity and diagnostic potential remain largely undefined. Here, a systematic pan-cancer screen of phosphatase expression across The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) identifies DUSP15 as the most chromophobe-selective phosphatase, showing near-exclusive enrichment in KICH and outperforming all other DUSPs and renal phosphatases in diagnostic power. This exceptional specificity was independently validated in a combined analysis of ten GEO microarray datasets, where DUSP15 consistently discriminated KICH from other renal subtypes, demonstrating robust cross-platform reproducibility and generalizability.

Beyond its striking specificity, DUSP15 expression delineates a distinctive immunometabolic tumour state. Pathway-level enrichment analyses reveal that DUSP15-high KICH tumours are characterized by coordinated activation of mitochondrial bioenergetics, oxidative phosphorylation, and DNA repair programs, coupled with broad suppression of immune signalling pathways, including interferon gamma response and TNF-α signalling. These dichotomous signatures converge within a coherent transcriptional network structure, where metabolic activation and immune silencing segregate into opposing, mutually exclusive modules. Notably, DUSP15 expression is inversely correlated with classical immune checkpoints such as PDCD1 and CTLA4, but positively co-expressed with SIGLEC15, a non-redundant immunosuppressive ligand implicated in immune escape in PD-L1–resistant tumours (8). This pattern defines a previously unrecognized immune evasion axis that is not reliant on canonical T-cell checkpoints, but rather on alternative immunoregulatory programs.

To resolve the cellular origin of this transcriptional state, single-cell RNA-seq analysis was performed on primary KICH tumours. DUSP15 expression was confined to a rare regenerative epithelial population comprising just ∼3% of tumour cells. These cells co-expressed acid-base handling genes such as ATP6V1B1, CLCNKA, and FOXI1, and were devoid of immune effector transcripts or checkpoint ligands, directly linking the DUSP15-driven bulk tumour phenotype to a metabolically specialized, immune-silent epithelial niche.

Together, this study identifies DUSP15 as a highly specific, diagnostically powerful, and mechanistically informative biomarker in chromophobe RCC. Its expression demarcates a unique immune-metabolic tumour state marked by SIGLEC15-driven immune escape and rare epithelial plasticity. These findings establish DUSP15 as a molecular gatekeeper of KICH pathobiology and nominate the DUSP15-SIGLEC15 axis as a potential target for therapeutic intervention in immune-cold renal tumours.

## Methods

### Data Sources and Processing

A total of protein-coding phosphatase genes (n = 265) were retained for downstream analysis, based on manually curated lists from the literature and gene ontology annotations. Gene expression data for tumours were retrieved from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) (9). Gene expression values in TCGA were downloaded as Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads (FPKM). Gene expression and clinical data for chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH), kidney clear cell carcinoma (KIRC), and kidney papillary carcinoma (KIRP) were also obtained from TCGA using the UCSC Xena browser and TCGAbiolinks R package.

For independent validation, DUSP15 expression data were compiled from 10 publicly available Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) microarray series containing KICH, KIRC, and KIRP samples (GSE2748, GSE11024, GSE11151, GSE12090, GSE16441, GSE17895, GSE19982, GSE36895, GSE46699, and GSE53757) (10). Datasets were retrieved using the GEOquery R package. All datasets were generated using comparable Affymetrix or Agilent microarray platforms, enabling cross-cohort validation of DUSP15 expression in renal tumour tissues. For each dataset, probe-to-gene mapping was performed using the corresponding platform annotation file, and probes matching the DUSP15 gene symbol were retained. Expression matrices were inspected to determine the scale of the reported values; datasets with raw or linear-scale expression were log₂-transformed using log₂(x + 1) to match the scale of already log₂-normalised datasets. DUSP15 expressions were assessed using the Kruskal–Wallis test, followed by pairwise Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with Benjamini–Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons.

Single-cell RNA-seq data were obtained from the publicly available GEO dataset GSE159115, which includes multiple RCC subtypes (11). Three tumour samples were selected specifically annotated as chromophobe RCC (GSM4819725, GSM4819726, and GSM4819727). Filtered gene-barcode matrices (HDF5 format) were downloaded and processed using the Seurat v5.0 package in R. Low-quality cells were excluded based on standard quality control metrics, including total feature count, UMI count, and percentage of mitochondrial reads. After filtering, a total of 4,774 cells were retained for downstream analysis. Cells were normalized using log-normalization with a scale factor of 10,000, followed by identification of highly variable features, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) for dimensionality reduction. Clustering was performed using a shared nearest neighbour (SNN) algorithm with a resolution parameter of 0.5, resulting in 15 transcriptionally distinct cell types that were annotated based on canonical marker gene expression. Cells expressing DUSP15 above 0.5 log-normalized units were classified as DUSP15⁺. Differential expression between DUSP15⁺ and DUSP15⁻ cells was assessed using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test.

### Differential Expression Analysis

Differential expression of DUSP15 between KICH tumours and adjacent normal tissues in the TCGA-KICH dataset was assessed using both Wilcoxon rank-sum tests (log₂-transformed FPKM) and DESeq2-normalized counts. Genes with FDR-adjusted p < 0.05 and log₂ fold change > 1.5 were considered significant.

### Diagnostic Performance and Logistic Regression

To evaluate diagnostic potential of DUSP15 and other DUSP genes, Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis in the TCGA and GEO microarray datasets were performed using the pROC package in R. Binary classification was defined as KICH = 1 and non-KICH (KIRC + KIRP) = 0. Area Under the Curve (AUC) values were generated, with additional ROC analysis comparing TCGA-KICH vs. normal tissues (tumour = 1, normal = 0). For DUSP15, binary logistic regression was applied using log₂-transformed values to estimate odds ratios (OR), 95% confidence intervals (CI), and p-values.

### Principal Component Analysis

PCA was conducted on expression values of DUSP genes across KICH, KIRC, and KIRP tumours using TCGA cohort. Expression matrices were scaled and centred using the prcomp function in R. PCA plots were annotated by cancer subtype, and 90% confidence ellipses were overlaid. Loadings and explained variance for each principal component were reported.

### Clinical and Pathologic Correlation

DUSP15 expression was analysed across AJCC tumour stage, pathological T/N/M categories, histological variants, tumour laterality, patient age, and survival status using the TCGA-KICH dataset. Statistical comparisons were made using Kruskal-Wallis tests and Wilcoxon rank-sum tests. Post hoc analyses were adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg method. Spearman correlation was used to assess associations between tumour volume, age at diagnosis and DUSP15 expression.

### Transcriptome-Wide Correlation and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis

Spearman correlation was computed between DUSP15 and all protein-coding genes across TCGA-KICH tumours. Genes were ranked by correlation coefficients, and those with |ρ| > 0.3 and FDR < 0.05 were considered significant. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) (14) was performed using Hallmark gene sets from MSigDB (v2025.1.Hs) via the clusterProfiler R package. Enriched pathways (FDR < 0.05) were used to construct Gene-Concept Networks by linking core enriched genes to their respective terms. Networks were visualized in Cytoscape (v3.10.3) (15) using the yFiles Organic Layout, with pathway nodes colour-mapped by normalized enrichment score (NES).

### Immune Deconvolution and Immune Checkpoint Correlation

The xCell algorithm was applied to TCGA-KICH tumours to estimate relative enrichment of 64 immune and stromal cell types (16). Tumours were grouped into DUSP15-Low (Q1) and DUSP15-High (Q4) categories. ImmuneScore and MicroenvironmentScore were compared between groups using Wilcoxon tests. FDR correction was applied to all comparisons. Spearman correlation analysis was performed between DUSP15 expression and curated immune checkpoint/TAM markers followed by Wilcoxon comparison between expression quartiles.

### Software and Visualization

All analyses were performed primarily in R (v2024.04.2+764), using Seurat (v5.0) for single-cell RNA-seq processing, DESeq2 for differential expression analysis, pROC for ROC and AUC estimation, and clusterProfiler with org.Hs.eg.db for gene set enrichment analysis. Data visualization and statistical plotting were conducted using ggplot2, ggpubr, and pheatmap, while immune cell deconvolution was carried out using xCell. For Gene-Concept Network construction, core enriched genes from GSEA results were processed using Python (v3.11) with pandas and networkx and visualized in Cytoscape (v3.10.3) using the yFiles Organic Layout for optimized network clarity.

## Results

### DUSP15 Defines a Highly Specific Transcriptomic Signature of Chromophobe Renal Cell Carcinoma Among Human Phosphatases

A systematic pan-cancer analysis was conducted to identify protein phosphatases with tumour-type selective expression across 21 cancer types from TCGA. Tissue specificity was quantified using the Tau score, a stringent metric for transcriptomic restriction (Supplementary Table S1). This unbiased screen revealed a discrete subset of phosphatases exhibiting near-exclusive expression in single tumour types, including ACP3 (Tau = 0.999), a known prostate cancer marker (17), and G6PC1 (Tau = 0.998), recently reported as a liver-specific biomarker in hepatocellular carcinoma (18). Among dual-specificity phosphatases, DUSP15 ranked highest with a Tau score of 0.977, showing strong and selective expression in chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH). Heatmap visualization of z-score-normalized expression for the top 50 most tissue-specific phosphatases confirmed the unique enrichment of DUSP15 in KICH compared to all other tumour types (Fig 1A).

To further contextualize this selectivity, the expression of the entire DUSP family was examined across the TCGA cohort. Among 21 tumour types, DUSP15 was uniquely and robustly upregulated in KICH, with a median expression of 5.62 log₂(FPKM) and detection in 98.4% of KICH samples (Fig 1B). In contrast, most other DUSPs (e.g., DUSP1, DUSP3, DUSP5) were broadly expressed across multiple malignancies with little tissue specificity. These results identify DUSP15 as a molecular outlier among DUSPs, reinforcing its potential as a tumour-selective biomarker.

Quantitative statistical analysis confirmed the strength of this tumour specificity. Kruskal-Wallis testing revealed highly significant variation in DUSP15 expression across cancers (P < 0.0001). Pairwise comparisons using Dunn’s test with Benjamini-Hochberg correction demonstrated that DUSP15 expression in KICH was significantly higher than in nearly all other tumour types, including liver hepatocellular carcinoma (LIHC; adjusted P = 4.85 × 10⁻⁸⁴), lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD; P = 1.43 × 10⁻⁶⁵), and colon adenocarcinoma (COAD; P = 3.06 × 10⁻³¹) (Fig 1C; Supplementary Table S2). The most pronounced difference was observed with head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC) (Z = –20.83; P = 2.34 × 10⁻⁹⁵). Collectively, these results define DUSP15 as one of the most tumour-restricted phosphatases in the TCGA transcriptome, with exceptional specificity for chromophobe renal cell carcinoma.

To benchmark DUSP15 against other members of the dual-specificity phosphatase family, its diagnostic performance was evaluated using Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis across all detected DUSP genes in TCGA-KICH, -KIRC, and -KIRP tumours (Table 5). Among all family members, DUSP15 achieved the highest area under the curve (AUC = 0.976), indicating exceptional diagnostic accuracy for distinguishing KICH from non-KICH renal tumours (Fig 2C). By comparison, DUSP11 (AUC = 0.930), DUSP8 (AUC = 0.927), and DUSP12 (AUC = 0.894) also showed promising performance, but none matched the sensitivity and specificity of DUSP15. Most other DUSPs exhibited modest to poor diagnostic capacity, with several, including DUSP21 and DUSP29, falling below the performance threshold (AUC < 0.50). These data establish DUSP15 as the most reliable and discriminative biomarker for KICH within the DUSP family. To validate the ROC findings from the TCGA cohort in an independent dataset, the same classification analysis was performed for DUSP15 using the integrated GEO kidney cancer dataset. ROC curve analysis yielded an AUC of 0.837 (95% CI: 0.735-0.939; p = 1.06 × 10⁻¹⁰) for distinguishing KICH from other subtypes (Fig 2D). The optimal expression threshold of 8.45 provided a sensitivity of 70.6% (95% CI: 52.5-84.9%) and a specificity of 98.6% (95% CI: 96.0-99.7%). These findings independently validate the selective upregulation and diagnostic power of DUSP15 in KICH observed in the TCGA cohort, reinforcing its potential as a robust biomarker for chromophobe renal cell carcinoma.

To evaluate the correlation between expression abundance and diagnostic accuracy, mean FPKM expression in TCGA-KICH was plotted against the AUC for each DUSP gene (Fig 2E). DUSP15 uniquely occupied the high-expression, high-AUC quadrant, underscoring its ideal combination of detectability and specificity. Other high-AUC candidates such as DUSP11 and DUSP8 resided in the low-expression range, which may limit their translational utility. This analysis further reinforces DUSP15 as a uniquely advantageous biomarker, one that couples robust expression with nearly perfect classification potential. To explore whether DUSP15 also contributes to transcriptional divergence between renal cancer subtypes, principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using the expression profiles of the DUSP genes across KICH, KIRC, and KIRP using TCGA cohort (Supplementary Table S4). PCA revealed clear segregation of KICH tumours from the other two subtypes along the first principal component (PC1), which explained 15.5% of total variance (Fig 2F). DUSP15 exhibited the strongest negative loading on PC1 (–0.394), indicating that it was the dominant contributor to this axis of variation. While DUSP11 and DUSP12 also contributed meaningfully, their influence was comparatively lower. Taken together, these results establish DUSP15 not only as a potent diagnostic biomarker for chromophobe RCC but also as a key molecular driver of KICH-specific gene expression programs within the phosphatase landscape.

### DUSP15 expression is maintained across clinical and demographic strata but modestly declines in advanced KICH

To evaluate whether DUSP15 expression is modulated by clinicopathologic or demographic variables, its distribution was analysed across TCGA-KICH clinical annotations. Consistent with its diagnostic utility, DUSP15 expression remained robustly elevated across most subgroups. Stratification by AJCC pathological stage revealed a modest but statistically significant reduction in stage IV tumours compared to earlier stages (Fig 3A), suggesting potential transcriptional remodelling in a subset of advanced cases. Analysis by pathological T stage demonstrated a significant decrease in DUSP15 expression between T2b and T4 tumours (adjusted P = 0.0418), and a near-significant reduction between T2 and T1a (adjusted P = 0.0511) (Fig 3C), while other comparisons showed no significant changes, indicating overall stability across early stages. Although reduced DUSP15 levels were observed in N1 and M1 tumours relative to N0 and M0 groups, the extremely limited sample sizes (n = 2) preclude definitive interpretation (Fig 3D, 3E). Expression levels in NX and MX cases were comparable to early-stage tumours, further suggesting that declines in N1/M1 reflect sample variability rather than consistent trends.

### DUSP15 is a quantitatively predictive and tumour-selective diagnostic biomarker in KICH

To evaluate the diagnostic potential of DUSP15 in TCGA-KICH, its expression levels between primary tumour tissues and adjacent normal tissue was compared (Supplementary Table S5; Fig 4A). This analysis revealed a highly significant upregulation of DUSP15 in tumour tissues, as assessed by a Wilcoxon rank-sum test with continuity correction (P = 3.81 × 10⁻¹²), supporting its robust differential expression and selective enrichment in KICH tumours. To assess the capacity of DUSP15 to discriminate tumour from normal tissue, ROC curve analysis was conducted (Fig 4B). The resulting AUC was 0.973, indicating near-perfect sensitivity and specificity. This exceptional classification performance confirms DUSP15’s strong discriminatory power and further supports its role as a diagnostic biomarker in chromophobe renal tumours.

### DUSP15 expression defines a metabolic-immune dichotomy in KICH

To delineate the molecular landscape associated with DUSP15 expression in KICH, a genome-wide Spearman correlation analysis was conducted using RNA-seq data from TCGA-KICH tumours. This analysis uncovered extensive transcriptional co-regulation, identifying 1,882 genes positively correlated and 2,195 genes negatively correlated with DUSP15 expression, indicating that DUSP15 is linked to broad and diverse biological processes (Supplementary Table S6).

To systematically characterize DUSP15-associated transcriptional programs, Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) was performed using Hallmark gene sets, ranking all genes by their Spearman correlation with DUSP15 expression (Supplementary Table S7). The enrichment profile of DUSP15-high tumours was dominated by mitochondrial and biosynthetic programs, with oxidative phosphorylation emerging as the top-enriched pathway (NES = 2.881, FDR = 4.17 × 10⁻¹⁰), alongside upregulation of MYC targets, cholesterol homeostasis, and DNA repair (Supplementary Figure S1A). Conversely, DUSP15 expression was negatively associated with multiple immune and inflammatory signatures, including interferon gamma response, TNF-α signalling via NF-κB, and KRAS signalling (Supplementary Figure S1B). To visualize the functional connectivity of these pathways, a Gene-Concept Network was constructed by linking significant GSEA terms (FDR < 0.05) to their core enriched genes. The resulting bipartite network revealed two distinct modules (Fig 5): a tightly clustered metabolic arm composed of positively enriched pathways, and a large, interconnected immune-suppression module dominated by negatively enriched signatures. This structural separation underscores the transcriptional duality of DUSP15, which appears to promote metabolic activation while concurrently repressing pro-inflammatory signalling in KICH.

### Transcriptional Co-Regulation and Immune Depletion Characterize DUSP15-High Tumours

To complement pathway-level insights from GSEA and the gene-concept network, DUSP15-associated gene expression programs were interrogated at single-gene resolution using genome-wide Spearman correlation analysis in TCGA-KICH tumours. The 50 most positively and 50 most negatively correlated protein-coding genes were extracted, z-score normalized, and visualized across DUSP15 expression quartiles, revealing marked expression stratification between DUSP15-high (Q4) and DUSP15-low (Q1) groups (Fig 6). Among the top positively correlated genes, SPTBN4 (ρ = 0.744, adjusted p = 2.94 × 10⁻⁹), a cytoskeletal adaptor, showed the strongest association with DUSP15. Mitochondrial and biosynthetic factors such as MRPL34, MICOS13, MRPL54, and NDUFB7 (ρ ≈ 0.69–0.71; adjusted p < 10⁻⁷) were prominently enriched, suggesting enhanced translation, cristae organization, and metabolic activation in DUSP15-high tumours. Additional co-expressed genes included PPDPF, a Ras/MAPK pathway modulator, and the apoptosis regulator FEM1A, as well as splicing components (SNRNP25, YJU2) and the long non-coding RNA LINC03073, highlighting a tightly co-regulated biosynthetic axis.

In contrast, negatively correlated genes were enriched for immune and cytokine signalling programs. IL1R1 (ρ = –0.673) and its decoy receptor IL1RL1/ST2 (ρ = – 0.652) were among the most repressed, alongside SGIP1 (endocytosis), AOC1 (immune recruitment), and HSD17B11 (oxidative stress), all with ρ > 0.63 and adjusted p < 2 × 10⁻⁵. Additional suppressed transcripts included SYNJ2 (phosphoinositide signalling), the DNA repair factor APLF (ρ = –0.619), and STARD13, a Rho-GTPase–activating protein, collectively suggesting attenuation of inflammatory sensing, stress responses, and cytoskeletal remodelling.

To determine whether this transcriptional signature was mirrored by changes in the tumour microenvironment, xCell deconvolution was applied to TCGA-KICH transcriptomic data (Supplementary Table S8; Fig 7). Tumours were stratified by DUSP15 quartiles, and enrichment scores were compared between Q4 and Q1. DUSP15-high tumours exhibited a markedly immune-depleted phenotype. The global ImmuneScore was significantly reduced (median = 0.0079 vs. 0.0254; P = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴, FDR = 0.0034), and the MicroenvironmentScore was similarly attenuated (median = 0.0255 vs. 0.0739; P = 8.7 × 10⁻⁴, FDR = 0.0116), indicating broad suppression of immune and stromal components. Specific immune populations such as conventional dendritic cells (cDCs) and regulatory T cells (Tregs) were significantly depleted in DUSP15-high tumours, consistent with an immune-excluded microenvironment.

Unexpectedly, DUSP15-high tumours demonstrated enrichment of non-immune stromal lineages, including megakaryocyte-erythroid progenitors (MEPs), neuronal signatures, and mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs). MEPs showed a striking increase (P = 1.7 × 10⁻⁵, FDR = 0.0012), and MSCs were similarly elevated (P = 1.1 × 10⁻³, FDR = 0.0122), suggesting stromal reprogramming and potential erythroid skewing. Erythrocyte signatures were also higher in DUSP15-high tumours (P = 1.6 × 10⁻³, FDR = 0.0130), further supporting this hypothesis. While Th1 cells were unexpectedly enriched (FDR = 0.0073), most immune subsets were depleted, reinforcing the presence of a noncanonical immune-desert state.

Together, these findings define a dual-axis program associated with high DUSP15 expression in KICH: intrinsic transcriptional upregulation of mitochondrial and biosynthetic machinery, and extrinsic remodelling of the tumour microenvironment toward immune exclusion and stromal transformation. This immune-metabolic reprogramming highlights DUSP15 as a putative orchestrator of tumour adaptation and a potential target for therapeutic intervention.

### DUSP15-high tumours exhibit a transcriptionally immune-silent state with selective upregulation of SIGLEC15

To dissect the immunoregulatory landscape associated with DUSP15 expression, both correlation-based and groupwise differential analyses were performed using TCGA-KICH transcriptomic data. Spearman correlation revealed that DUSP15 was strongly inversely associated with numerous markers of tumour-associated macrophages (TAMs), particularly M2-polarized and immunosuppressive subtypes (Supplementary Table S9; Fig 8). Notable negative correlations were observed with CCL2 (ρ = –0.717, adj. P = 3.99 × 10⁻¹⁴), MRC1, NOS2, TNF, and IL12A, all essential mediators of macrophage recruitment, polarization, and cytokine release. In parallel, multiple T-cell co-stimulatory and activation genes, including CD86, CTLA4, LAG3, and PDCD1 (PD-1), also demonstrated significant negative correlation with DUSP15, pointing to a broadly immunosuppressive transcriptomic program.

To extend and validate the correlation-based immunogenomic findings, differential expression analysis was performed between the Q1 and Q4 quartiles of DUSP15 expression (Supplementary Table S9; Fig 9). This comparison reinforced the immunosuppressive transcriptional program associated with DUSP15, revealing significant downregulation of key tumour-associated macrophage (TAM) markers and T cell co-stimulatory genes in DUSP15-high tumours. For example, CD86 expression decreased from a median of 8.46 in Q1 to 6.44 in Q4 (P = 1.5 × 10⁻⁴), while CTLA4 declined from 3.70 to 1.58 (P = 0.0014). Similar repression was observed for CD163, CSF1R, IL10, and TNFRSF9, confirming a transcriptional landscape indicative of impaired antigen presentation, co-stimulation, and macrophage-mediated immune support.

In stark contrast to the global suppression of classical immune checkpoints, SIGLEC15 emerged as a striking outlier. It showed the strongest positive correlation with DUSP15 among all immune genes (ρ = 0.568, adj. P = 2.46 × 10⁻⁸) and was significantly upregulated in DUSP15-high tumours (median: Q1 = 8.44, Q4 = 9.78; P = 0.034). As a non-redundant immune checkpoint operating independently of the PD-1/PD-L1 axis, SIGLEC15 has been implicated in immune evasion mechanisms particularly in PD-L1–low contexts. Its selective enrichment in DUSP15-overexpressing tumours, amidst suppression of canonical checkpoints, suggests the presence of a distinct immunoevasive program driven by a SIGLEC15-centric axis.

Collectively, these data delineate a transcriptionally and immunologically unique subclass of chromophobe renal cell carcinoma defined by broad suppression of immune activation genes and preferential upregulation of SIGLEC15. This immune-silent, checkpoint-refractory phenotype positions DUSP15 as a potential orchestrator of non-canonical immune escape, with SIGLEC15 representing a rational target for precision immunotherapeutic strategies in DUSP15-high tumours.

### Single-cell transcriptomics identifies a rare, metabolically reprogrammed epithelial niche as the origin of DUSP15 in KICH

To elucidate the cellular basis for the immune-silent, metabolically active phenotype of DUSP15-high tumours, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) was performed on 4,774 tumour cells from three chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH) specimens (GSE159115) (Supplementary Table S10). Unsupervised clustering identified 15 distinct tumour-infiltrating cell types, including epithelial, stromal, and immune lineages. DUSP15 expression was strikingly restricted, only 155 cells (3.2%) exhibited detectable expression (Fig 10A), and nearly half of these (47.8%) were confined to a rare regenerative epithelial population comprising just 1.9% of all tumour cells (Fig 10B). This represents a ∼12.5-fold enrichment relative to background, pinpointing a highly specific cellular niche as the principal reservoir of DUSP15.

Regenerative epithelial cells co-expressed SPINK1, NXPH2, and TMEM213, and were transcriptionally enriched for vacuolar ATPases (ATP6V1B1, ATP6V0A4), ion channels (CLCNKA, CLCNKB), and FOXI1, a master regulator of intercalated cell identity. This signature is consistent with an acid-base handling program reminiscent of type A intercalated cells in the distal nephron. Notably, DUSP15 expression within this cluster was both abundant and homogeneous (median = 0.48; expressed in 47.8% of cells), in sharp contrast to negligible or absent expression across immune, stromal, and fibroblast populations. For example, T cells (20.5% expressing) and macrophages (97% expressing) exhibited extremely low DUSP15 transcript levels (mean = 0.21 and 0.008, respectively), confirming that the immunosuppressive signature observed in DUSP15-high tumours does not arise from immune cell-intrinsic expression.

Differential gene expression between DUSP15⁺ and DUSP15⁻ cells revealed robust upregulation of genes involved in mitochondrial respiration (e.g., MICOS13, ATP5F1D), ion transport (SLC4A1, KCNJ15), epithelial remodelling (LYPD1, TMEM72), and ribosome biogenesis, reinforcing a metabolically primed state (Fig 10C). In parallel, genes associated with immune signalling and antigen presentation were conspicuously absent, highlighting a transcriptional program geared toward stress adaptation, cellular renewal, and immune invisibility.

Collectively, these findings define DUSP15 as a lineage-specific marker of a metabolically reprogrammed epithelial subpopulation that is rare yet transcriptionally dominant in shaping the immune-metabolic landscape of chromophobe RCC. This population likely serves as the cellular origin of the DUSP15-driven transcriptomic program observed at bulk level, and may represent a critical node of tumour maintenance, therapeutic resistance, and immune evasion in KICH.

## Discussion

Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (KICH) remains a poorly characterized subtype of kidney cancer, lacking robust molecular markers and precision therapeutic strategies (19,20). Through integrative transcriptomic, immunogenomic, and single-cell analyses, this study identifies and functionally defines DUSP15 as a highly selective and biologically informative biomarker for KICH. DUSP15 expression demonstrated near-complete tumour-type specificity, outperforming other dual-specificity phosphatases in discriminating KICH from clear cell and papillary RCC. This specificity was confirmed across multiple independent cohorts, with ROC analysis yielding AUCs of 0.973 (TCGA) and 0.837 (GEO), establishing DUSP15 as a reproducible diagnostic marker across platforms and populations.

Beyond its diagnostic utility, DUSP15 expression demarcated a unique transcriptional and immunological state. Pathway-level analysis using GSEA revealed strong enrichment of oxidative phosphorylation, DNA repair, and MYC targets in DUSP15-high tumours, consistent with mitochondrial bioenergetic activation and proliferative drive. Conversely, negatively enriched pathways included interferon gamma response, TNF-α signalling, and other immune-related programs, indicating broad immunological suppression. These dichotomous signatures were reinforced by the construction of a gene-concept network, which revealed two clearly segregated modules, one comprising highly interconnected metabolic pathways and the other composed of suppressed immune programs. The spatial architecture of this network underscored the coherence and mutual exclusivity of metabolic activation and immune evasion in the DUSP15 transcriptional landscape.

At the gene level, DUSP15 expression strongly correlated with mitochondrial ribosomal components, ion transporters, and RNA processing genes, biological programs that support tumour proliferation and stress adaptation (21–23), suggesting coupling between metabolic reprogramming and post-transcriptional control. In contrast, negatively correlated genes were enriched for cytokine signalling (e.g., IL1RL1, IL10, TNF), stress responses, and extracellular matrix regulation, reinforcing a model of immune-quiescent and stress-tolerant tumour states. Immune deconvolution using xCell confirmed the depletion of dendritic cells, regulatory T cells, and global immune infiltration in DUSP15-high tumours. Intriguingly, non-immune lineages such as megakaryocyte-erythroid progenitors, mesenchymal stem cells, and neuroendocrine-like populations were enriched, implicating DUSP15 in niche reprogramming and stromal remodelling.

Immune checkpoint profiling revealed that DUSP15 expression was inversely correlated with canonical checkpoints (e.g., PDCD1, CTLA4, LAG3), yet positively associated with SIGLEC15, a non-redundant immunosuppressive ligand implicated in PD-L1–resistant tumours (8). Among all immune regulators examined, SIGLEC15 emerged as the strongest positive correlate of DUSP15, and was significantly upregulated in DUSP15-high tumours. This pattern suggests that DUSP15 delineates an immune escape program that bypasses conventional T cell checkpoints in favor of alternative axes centred on SIGLEC15. Differential expression analysis between DUSP15-high and -low tumours confirmed broad repression of co-stimulatory and macrophage-associated genes, alongside selective induction of SIGLEC15, further supporting this non-canonical immunosuppressive profile.

To pinpoint the cellular origin of this transcriptional program, single-cell RNA sequencing of chromophobe RCC samples was performed. DUSP15 expression was confined to a rare regenerative epithelial subset comprising ∼3.2% of tumour cells, nearly half of which were localized to an acid-base-handling epithelial cluster expressing FOXI1, ATP6V1B1, ATP6V0A4, and CLCNKB. These cells lacked immune effector genes or checkpoint ligands yet displayed strong enrichment for mitochondrial and ion transport pathways, confirming that the metabolic-immune phenotype observed in bulk tumours originates from a metabolically specialized, immune-silent epithelial lineage. In addition, differential expression analysis between DUSP15⁺ and DUSP15⁻ single cells reinforced this conclusion, highlighting upregulation of biosynthetic and epithelial remodelling genes uniquely in the DUSP15⁺ population.

Collectively, this study identifies DUSP15 as a highly KICH-selective phosphatase that marks a metabolically reprogrammed, immune-excluded tumour state. By integrating bulk and single-cell data, it reveals a rare epithelial subpopulation as the source of this signature and uncovers SIGLEC15 as a key co-regulated immunoregulatory partner. These findings position DUSP15 not only as a robust diagnostic marker, but also as a molecular gatekeeper of immune-metabolic reprogramming in chromophobe RCC. Targeting the DUSP15-SIGLEC15 axis may offer a therapeutic opportunity in this immune-cold malignancy, where conventional checkpoint blockade has shown limited success.
