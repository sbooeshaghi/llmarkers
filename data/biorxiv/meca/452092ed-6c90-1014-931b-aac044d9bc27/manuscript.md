## Abstract

Abstract
Multiplex immunohistochemistry (mIHC) and multiplexed ion beam imaging (MIBI) platforms have become increasingly popular for studying complex single-cell biology in cancer patients. In such studies, researchers test for association between functional markers and survival in a two-step process. First, they count the number of positive cells, defined as the number of cells where a functional marker is significantly expressed. Then, they partition the patients into two groups and test for association between the group label with survival. Consequently, the approach suffers from subjectivity and lack of robustness. In this paper, we propose a threshold-free distance metric between patients solely based on their marker probability densities. Using the proposed distance, we have developed two association tests, one based on hierarchical clustering and the other based on linear mixed model. Our method obviates the need for the arduous step of threshold selection, getting rid of the subjectivity bias. The method also intuitively generalizes to joint analysis of multiple markers. We assessed the performance of our method through extensive simulation studies and also used it to analyze two multiplex imaging datasets.

## Introduction

In recent years, various technologies are being used for probing single-cell spatial biology, for example, multiparameter immunofluorescence (Bataille and others, 2006), imaging mass cytometry (IMC) (Giesen and others, 2014; Chang and others, 2017; Ali and others, 2020), multiplex immunohistochemistry (mIHC) (Halse and others, 2018; Tan and others, 2020) and multiplexed ion beam imaging (MIBI) (Angelo and others, 2014; Seal and others, 2021b). These technologies, often referred to as multiplex tissue imaging, offer the potential for researchers to explore the bases of many different biological mechanisms. Multiplex tissue imaging platforms such as Vectra 3.0 (Akoya Biosciences) (Huang and others, 2013), Vectra Polaris (Akoya Biosciences) (Pollan and others, 2020), MIBI (Ionpath Inc.) (Keren and others, 2019; Ptacek and others, 2020) produce images of a similar structure: 2-dimensional images of tissues at the resolution of cells and nuclei, where proteins in the sample have been labeled with antibodies called “markers”that attach to cell membranes and proteins. Typically, mIHC (Vectra platform) images have 6-8 markers, whereas MIBI images can have more than 40 markers. Majority of these markers are surface markers (primarily used for cell type identification), such as CD4, CD3, CD8, CD68 etc. (Jondal and others, 1972; Zola and others, 2007; Shipkova and Wieland, 2012). There are several functional markers as well, such as HLA-DR (Jendro and others, 1991; Oczenski and others, 2003; Saraiva and others, 2018), PD-1, PD-L1 and Lag3 (Nguyen and Ohashi, 2015; Han and others, 2020; Phillips and others, 2021) that dictate or regulate important cell-functions.

With such informative markers, researchers are often interested in finding out if abundance or over-expression of the markers in the tumor micro environment (TME) (Whiteside, 2008; Binnewies and others, 2018) have significant impact on clinical parameters, such as patient survival (Sahlberg and others, 2014; Koguchi and others, 2015; Johnson and others, 2020). For this purpose, a commonly used approach, particularly popular in flow and mass cytometry paradigm, is a method based on thresholding of marker values (Bulian and others, 2014; Costa and others, 2017; Missassi and others, 2021). The first step of the method is to identify the cells (or, pixels) positive for a marker defined as the cells which show significant marker expression. Next, researchers proceed in either of the two ways: 1) use the proportion of the positive cells (or, pixels) directly in a univariate linear regression framework to test association with the outcome (Patwa and others, 2021), or, 2) consider one or more thresholds to group the patients into two or more clusters based on the proportion of the positive cells and use the vector of cluster labels in a univariate linear regression framework to test association with the outcome (Chen and others, 2016; Chang and others, 2018; Yang and others, 2019; Johnson and others, 2021).

The thresholding-based method clearly requires judicious selection of the cut-offs that greatly influence the subsequent steps of the analysis. The result is bound to vary for different choices of the thresholds and a poor choice of thresholds may produce an uninformative and uninterpretable result. There is a plethora of helpful guidelines on choosing these thresholds in different contexts (Barnett and others, 1999; Kimball and others, 2018; Cossarizza and others, 2019; BIO-RAD, 2021). However, there is no universal solution or rule of thumb, and the method remains prone to subjectivity bias and lacks statistical robustness.

In this paper, we propose a threshold-free method for distinguishing the difference between the patients with respect to markers of interest. We treat the expression of every marker as a random variable having realizations in the cells (or, pixels) of a patient. For every marker, we compare its probability density across different patients. Our algorithm is as follows. First, for every patient, the probability density of each marker is estimated using kernel density estimation (KDE) (Silverman, 1981; Sheather and Jones, 1991; Ghosh and others, 2006). Then for every marker, a density based distance metric (Basu and others, 1998; Jones and others, 2001) known as Jensen-Shannon distance (Endres and Schindelin, 2003; Fuglede and Topsoe, 2004) is used to quantify the difference in the estimated density across the patients. The distance matrix obtained for every marker can then be used to classify the patients into meaningful groups using hierarchical clustering (Murtagh, 1985; Murtagh and Legendre, 2014). In a simple linear regression framework, the cluster-labels can then be tested for association with a outcome of interest, such as overall survival of the patients. The distance matrix can also be directly used in a linear mixed model (Hoffman, 2013; Seal and others, 2021a) or equivalently, a kernel machine regression framework (Liu and others, 2008; Hua and Ghosh, 2015; Ge and others, 2016; Jensen and others, 2019). To study the joint effect of markers, one can also sum up the individual marker-specific distance matrices to create an aggregated distance matrix. Using our proposed method, we have analyzed an mIHC dataset on lung cancer (Johnson and others, 2021) from the University of Colorado School of Medicine, finding out that HLA-DR marker probability density impacts 5-year overall survival of the patients. We have also applied the proposed method on a publicly available triple negative breast cancer (TNBC) MIBI data (Keren and others, 2018) finding out immunoregulatory protein PD-1 to have significant effect on overall survival. We have performed extensive simulation studies mimicking the real datasets to check the reliability and robustness of our method.

## Materials and Methods

Suppose there are N patients with the j-th patient having nj cells and M markers. Let X1ij, X2ij, …, XMij denote scaled expression of M markers in i-th cell of j-th patient for i = 1, 2,…, nj, j = 1, 2,…, N. Let Y (N × 1 vector) be an outcome of interest and C be an N × p matrix of p covariates.

### Traditional thresholding based approach for clustering patients

To study if abundance of marker k is associated with a patient”s survival or any other outcome of interest (Y), the most popular way is to classify the patients into two or more groups using a thresholding based approach. First, the researcher would consider a threshold, t1 and check how many of the nj cells of patient j have marker expression more than that threshold i.e. the number of cells with Xkij > t1. Such cells are often referred to as the cells positive for marker k. Lets denote the proportion of the cells positive for a marker k in patient j as, , where I(.) denotes the indicator function. Next, the researcher would choose one more threshold, t2 and check how many patients have more than t2% cells positive for a marker k i.e. the patients with pkj > t2. Thus, the patients are clustered into two groups, one group with patients having pkj ≤ t2 and the other with pkj > t2. Then, the researcher would test if these two clusters of people have differential rate of survival (or, associated with some outcome of interest). ### Proposed Method: Distance based clustering using individual marker probability density

Keeping the limitation of the thresholding based approach in mind, our aim is to propose a distance metric between the patients based on each marker k that would be devoid of any degree of subjectivity and can easily be tested for association with a outcome of interest.

## Results

We first discuss application of our method on the real datasets. We analyzed two datasets: mIHC lung cancer dataset (Johnson and others, 2021) and TNBC MIBI dataset (Keren and others, 2018). The first dataset has a single functional marker, HLA-DR and the second dataset has four immunoregulatory proteins, PD-1, PD-L1, Lag3 and IDO. We applied the method proposed in Section 2.2 on both the datasets. In all the analyses, the markers were scaled to have expression value between 0 and 1.

### Application to mIHC Lung Cancer data

In the mIHC lung cancer dataset, there are 153 patients each with 3-5 images (in total, 761 images). The patients have varying number of cells identified (from 3755 to 16949). In total there are 1,590,327 cells from two different tissue regions: tumor and stroma. The cells are classified into either of the six different cell types: CD14+, CD19+, CD4+, CD8+, CK+ and Other based on their expression values of different phenotypic markers, such as CD19, CD3, CK, CD8 and CD14. A functional marker, HLA-DR (also referred to as MHCII), is also measured in each of the cells. Using a threshold-based approach, Johnson and others (2021) found out that 5-year overall survival of a patient is associated with the percentage of HLA-DR positive CK+ Tumor cells. Here, we were interested in a more general question: whether 5-year overall survival of a patient is associated with the HLA-DR density in CK+ Tumor cells. We first computed the JSD matrix between the patients as discussed in Section 2.2.1 based on the density of HLADR marker in CK+ tumor cells. Next, we performed a hierarchical clustering based on the computed JSD matrix to classify the patients into two groups and test for association with the survival vector as discussed in Section 2.2.2. Figure 1 shows the Kaplan-Meyer curves (Efron, 1988) of the two groups of patients. We noticed that Hazard Ratio (HR) is large (> 2) and the p-value is significant indicating that patient survival is associated with the probability density of HLA-DR in CK+ tumor cells. Figure 2 shows individual and mean HLADR probability density of different patients from the two clusters. We noticed that the individual densities from cluster 1 were more right-skewed compared to those from cluster 2 which led to the mean density of cluster 1 having very high mode compared to that of cluster 2. We also used the test based on Cox PH model with random effects from Section 2.2.2 in this case. The estimated variance of the random effect was 0.38. Following Therneau and others (2015)’s interpretation of the variance parameter in this context, we concluded that there are multiple patients in the study with quite large relative risks,  fold greater than the average patients. However, the LRT based on integrated partial likelihoods was not significant.

### Application to TNBC MIBI data

The triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) MIBI dataset has images from 41 patients. Keren and others (2018) categorized these patients into three groups: “cold”, “compartmentalized” and “mixed” based on the level of immune infiltration in the TIME. We were interested in studying the density of the immunoregulatory protein markers, PD1, PD-L1, and Lag3 which have been shown to have immunological relevance (Keren and others, 2018; Patwa and others, 2021). PD1 and Lag3 are primarily expressed in immune cells and “cold” patients have very few immune cells expressing them. Thus, we focused our analysis on 33 non-“cold” patients. For PD1 and Lag3, we studied their density only in immune cells of a patient and for PD-L1 we studied its density both in immune and tumor cells of a patient. For every marker, we computed the JSD matrix between the patients and performed a hierarchical clustering to classify the patients into two groups as discussed in 2.2.1. Then, we tested the vector of cluster labels for association with two available outcomes: recurrence and survival. Figure 3 shows the Kaplan-Meyer curves corresponding to the three markers for both survival (left column) and recurrence (right column). We noticed that the HR of survival was large (HR = 2.824) and significant (p < 0.0346) for PD1 marker, indicating that the differences in PD1 marker density is associated with risk of death. For PD1, the HR of recurrence was large as well (HR = 2.065) but was not significant. For other two markers, we did not find any significant results (at level 0.05). However, it is worth pointing out that the HR of both survival and recurrence for PD-L1 were quite large (3.49 and 2.84 respectively), alluding to a possible association of PD-L1 marker density with both risk of death and risk of recurrence. We should also keep in mind that the sample size for this particular analysis was quite low which could limit our power.

## Discussion

In multiplexed tissue imaging datasets, it is often of interest to stratify the patients based on the profile of functional markers for the purpose of risk assessment (e.g. risk of recurrence, risk of death etc.). The most common approach of grouping the patients into meaningful clusters is a thresholding based method which requires elaborate tuning of one or more thresholds. In consequence, the method remains remains largely subjective and varies from one researcher to another based on their interpretation of the data. In this paper, we develop a threshold-free method for classifying patients based on the marker density in the tumor micro environment (TME). The method is easy to interpret and free from the subjectivity bias.

In our method, we treat marker expression in a patient as a random variable and compute its kernel density estimate based on the cells of the TME. Once the marker density estimates for all the patients have been computed, a distance metric known as Jensen-Shannon distance is considered to quantify the difference in marker densities between the patients. If the distance between two patients is large, it means that they have very different marker expression profiles. Next, the computed distance matrix can be used in either of the following two ways. It can be subjected to hierarchical clustering to group the patients into clusters and the cluster-labels can be tested for association with outcomes of interest (e.g. recurrence, survival). Or it can be used directly in a linear mixed model setup (or, equivalently a kernel machine regression model) for testing association with outcomes of interest.

We analyzed two highly complex multiplex tissue imaging datasets, an mIHC lung cancer dataset from University of Colorado School of Medicine and a publicly available triple negative breast cancer MIBI data. In the mIHC dataset, we found out that the difference in HLA-DR marker density between patients was significantly associated with their 5-year overall survival probability. In the MIBI dataset, we found out that the difference in the density of immunoregulatory protein PD-1 was associated with the difference in overall survival. Next, we replicated the characteristics of the mIHC dataset in two simulation scenarios and showcased the robustness of our method in comparison with the thresholding-based method. In the final simulation setup, we aimed to simulate a dataset fully abiding the principles of the thresholding method. We showed that our method performed competently even in that scenario.

One point that we have not covered in great detail in this paper is how to summarize the joint information from different markers effectively. Recall that we have mentioned summing up the distance matrices corresponding to different markers creating a new distance matrix that would be expected to capture some sort of aggregated information. But, it needs to be studied in detail how to make sure that the individual distance matrices do not dominate the aggregated distance matrix. Otherwise, the new matrix will not be very meaningful. We believe that a carefully weighted sum of the individual distance matrices can potentially be one solution and intend to study this in further details as a part of our next work. Also, we would like to apply our method on datasets coming from other imaging platforms, such as CODEX (Goltsev and others, 2018) and Visium (Tippani and others, 2021).
