## Abstract

AbstractT cell exhaustion is one of the mechanisms that cancer cells get rid of control from the immune system. Single-cell RNA sequencing has showed superiority on immunity mechanism in recent studies. Here, we collected more than 6000 single CD8+ T cells from three cancers including CRC, HCC and NSCLC, and identified five clusters of each cancer. We obtained 71 and 159 DEGs for pre_exhausted or exhausted vs. effector comparison in all three cancers, respectively. Specially, we found some key genes including the four exhaustion associated genes of PDCD1, HAVCR2, TIGIT and TOX, and two vital genes of CD69 and JUN in the interaction network. Additionally, we identified the gene SAMSN1 which highly expressed in the exhausted cells had a poor overall survival and played a negative role in immunity. We summarized the putative interrelated mechanisms of above key genes identified in this study by integrating the reported knowledge. Furthermore, we explored the heterogeneous and preference of exhausted CD8+ T cells in each patient and found only one exhausted sub-cluster existed in the most of patients, especially in CRC and HCC. As far as we know, this is the first time to study the mechanism of T cell exhaustion with the data of single-cell RNA sequencing of multiple cancers. Our study may facilitate the understanding of the mechanism of T cell exhaustion, and provide a new way for functional research of single-cell RNA sequencing data across cancers.

## Introduction

T cell exhaustion (Tex), a hyporesponsive state of T cells, was originally described in CD8+ T cells during chronic lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) of mice[1]. In recent years, the phenomenon of Tex has also been found in cancer[2, 3]. It has been reported that exhausted T cells in cancers share many similarities with that in chronic infection[4], and play a significant role in tumorigenesis[5]. The main reasons leading to Tex in chronic infection and cancer are as following[6]: long-term and persistent explosion to antigens[7]; the upregulation of inhibitory receptors (IRs) including PDCD1, CLTA4, HAVCR2, LAG3 and 2B4[8]; complex effects of soluble factors such as cytokines IL-10 and transforming growth factor-β (TGFβ) [9]; the expression of transcription factor including T-bet and Eomes[10] and corresponding epigenetic regulation[11].However, at present, to reveal molecular mechanisms of exhausted T cells, most researches are still focusing on chronic infected mouse models by microarray or RNA sequencing[8, 12]. Tex is a general trend of tumor, which can be used as one of the main targets of immunosuppression therapy to save T cell from exhaustion and reactivate the cytotoxicity of T cells, providing a new opportunity for clinical immunotherapy[8]. Nevertheless, due to the complexity and heterogeneity of cancer, the concrete mechanism of Tex in cancer is still unclear. Thus, a deeper understanding of this mechanism is urgently needed.

Currently, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has clearly revealed some new mechanisms and phenomena of cancer with the advantages of high accuracy and reproducibility[13–15]. Using single cell transcriptome profiling, we can identify new types of immune cells which can’t be revealed at the original tissue level and can construct a developmental trajectory for immune cells which can reveal the random heterogeneity[16]. These new findings are useful to better understand the immune system and its mechanism of action on tumors. Notably, this technology makes it possible to explore complicated tumor microenvironment including tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) in melanoma, head and neck cancer, breast cancer and glioblastoma cancer[17–20]. Thus, using advantage of scRNA-seq to analyze T cells and obtain the hallmarks of exhausted T cells can bring a new therapeutic strategy on clinical cancer treatment.

In the present study, due to the vital role of CD8+ T cells in eliciting antitumor responses[21], we integrated single cell sequencing data from colorectal cancer (CRC), liver cancer (HCC), and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) to analyze CD8+ T cells in various cancers. By classifying cells into different clusters with the unique markers, we identified five clusters of each cancer. Furthermore, we compared the pre_exhausted or exhausted cells with effector cells to make a deeper understanding of exhaustion mechanism. Additionally, the sub-clusters of exhausted cells in each patient revealed the individual differences and preferences. Overall, the findings of molecules changed in pre_exhasuted and exhausted clusters may provide the potential targets to anticancer therapy, and the different exhausted sub-clusters of each patient help the understanding of exhaustion heterogeneity.

## Materials and methods

### Data Resources and Preprocessing

Single cell transcriptome profiling of human T cells in three cancers including CRC, HCC and NSCLC was obtained from the GEO database (GSE108989, GSE99254, GSE98638), including raw read count and TPM data. According to the annotations, we isolated CD8+ T cells from peripheral blood (PTC), adjacent normal (NTC) and tumor tissues (TTC). To filtered out the low-quality cells, we excluded cells with fewer than 3000 detected genes and the expression of the housekeeping gene (log2(TPMACTB/10+1)) below 2.5[22].

### Unsupervised Clustering

For each dataset, we selected the genes with top n highest variance where n changed from 500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000. Then the expression data of these genes was used to perform clustering by single-cell consensus clustering (SC3)[23]. To obtain the reliable result, we set the number of clusters (k) from 2 to 10. Only the genes with p value < 0.05 and auroc > 0.75 could be considered as markers.

For each SC3 run, we focused on three indicators - the silhouette, the consensus matrix and the cluster specific genes, which could help us to fix the suitable n and k. When determined the clusters, we mapped the markers to the CellMarker databases[24] to find out the characteristics of each cluster.

### Trajectory Analysis

To explore the potential functional changes of CD8+ T cell of different clusters for each cancer, we performed development trajectory analysis by Monocle[25] with the top 100 cluster-specific genes of each cluster. The differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between each two cluster pairs were identified by R package edgeR[26] using the criteria as following: (1) the mean of CPM value was greater than 1; (2) false discovery rate (FDR) < 0.05; (3) the absolute value of logFC > 1. We defined the cluster-specific genes in each cluster of each cancer based on the condition that its expression was upregulated when compared with those in the other clusters.

### Differentially Expressed Genes, Functional Annotation and Transcription Network Analysis

To further get known the potential mechanisms that leading to the exhausted CD8+ T cells, we compare genes between pre_exhausted or exhausted cluster in tumor and effector cluster in peripheral blood. The cell numbers of the two groups participating in the comparison were set as m and n, and the smaller one was defined as p. A gene was defined as exhaustion associated DEG if (1) the number of expressed cells > p; (2) FDR < 0.05; (3) the absolute value of logFC > 1. To find the common regulation mechanisms of Tex, we identified the DEGs with the same regulatory status in three cancers. Next, the DEGs were subjected to the Gene Ontology(GO), including biological process (BP), cell component (CC), molecular function (MF), and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis by clusterProfiler[27] with adjusted p value < 0.05.

To find out the interaction among these DEGs, Search Tool for the Retrieval of Interacting Genes database (STRING) was used to construct the protein-protein interaction (PPI) network, which was visualized by Cytoscape[28], based on the combined score of 0.4[29]. Additionally, the Molecular Complex Detection (MCODE) as a Cytoscape plugin was performed to explore the closely connected regions with default parameter[30]. Furthermore, to investigate the potential upstream regulators that affect gene differentially expression, we integrated three transcription factor targets databases, including ChIP-X enrichment analysis (ChEA)[31], Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE)[32] from Harmonizonme[33] and Transcriptional Regulatory Relationships Unraveled by Sentence-based Text-mining (TRRUST)[34] to reveal regulatory relationships among DEGs.

### Survival analysis

The TCGA data[35] including COAD (colon adenocarcinoma), LIHC (liver hepatocellular carcinoma), LUAD (lung adenocarcinoma), LUSC (lung squamous cell carcinoma) was used to explore the relationship between gene expression levels of the key genes and patients’ overall survival. LUAD and LUSC were integrated as NSCLC. The mRNA expression data and corresponding clinical annotation files were obtained from TCGA. To correct the effector of CD8+ T cells[36], the expression of genes were divided by that of the geometric mean of CD3D, CD3E, CD3G, CD8A and CD8B. The threshold was defined as the median of relative expression. The samples with relative expression below the threshold were classified as ‘‘low expression’’ group, and the samples with relative expression above the threshold were classified as “high expression” group. R package survival was used to perform survival analysis.

### Sub-cluster of exhausted cells

We further clustered the exhausted cells into more specific sub-clusters using the same pipeline of SC3 as mentioned above. Especially, we displayed the distribution of each sub-cluster of each patient. The differential genes expression analysis between exhausted sub-clusters and effector cells was performed by edgeR with the same criteria as mentioned above. To further explore the function of each sub-cluster, we performed gene set variation analysis (GSVA)[37], a sensitive technique that can detect underlying pathways over a sample population in an unsupervised manner, with the DEGs of the exhausted sub-clusters vs. effector comparison and the KEGG pathways in canonical gene sets (C2) from the Molecular Signature Database (MSigDB)[38] as the reference gene set. In addition, comparisons of single-cell enrichment scores were performed using the R package limma[39]. Differentially enriched pathways were defined as the number of genes >10 and FDR adjusted p value < 0.05 as described previously[40].

### CD8+ T cell clustering

To reveal the potential functional sub-clusters of CD8+ T cells, we performed unsupervised clustering by SC3. As for CRC, finally we chose the top 2500 genes with highest variance to divide cells into 5 clusters (Figure 1A). Similarly, the top 1000 genes were used to divide into 5 clusters in HCC and NSCLC (Figure 1B, 1C).

To identify each cluster of three cancers, we compared the markers of each 15 clusters with the CellMarker database. For example, the 9 of 10 markers (CCR7, LEF1, SELL, LDLRAP1, NOSIP, PRKCQ−AS1, TXK, TMEM123, NELL2) in CRC_C4 were found in the cell markers of naïve CD8+ T cell, then we identified it as naïve CD8+ T cell. Taken together, we defined the clusters for each cancer with known markers: CRC_C4, HCC_C5, NSCLC_C4 were identified as naïve T cells, CRC_C1, HCC_C3, NSCLC_C1 were identified as effector T cells, CRC_C3, HCC_C2, NSCLC_C3 were identified as exhausted T cells. CRC_C2 was identified as natural killer T (NKT) cells and HCC_C4 was identified as mucosal-associated invariant T (MAIT) cells (Table S1). While in NSCLC, C2 couldn’t be identified as a defined cluster according to the CellMarker, in which ZNF683 was one of the markers belonging to natural killer T (NKT) cell. Thus, we called CRC_C2, HCC_C4, NSCLC_C2 as unconventional T cells[41] temporarily which were excluded in the following analysis. We noted that there was a common cluster with a same marker named GZMK that couldn’t be well-defined, either. To figure out the potential biological functions of these clusters, we performed trajectory analysis by monocle (Figure 1D, 1E, 1F). Except for the known clusters, we could see that CRC_C5, HCC_C1, NSCLC_C5 located between effector and exhausted CD8+ T cells and appeared a transition state, thus them were defined as “pre_exhausted” CD8+ T cells. In order to check the classification effect, we used the t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE), a non-linear dimensionality reduction technique, by Rtsne package with all the markers of all clusters of each cancer to visualize the cell population clusters in two dimensions (Figure 1G, 1H, 1I). We could see that in CRC, five clusters with different functions were mostly clustered in different parts with obvious boundaries. In addition, the same clustering phenomenon could also be observed in HCC and NSCLC. We combined clusters of three cancers with their all markers and found that the same functional cells from the different cancer types were clustered into the same clusters (Figure 1J).

### Differentially expressed genes associated with exhaustion

The proportion of different clusters varied greatly in different tissues (Figure 2A). Peripheral blood had most naïve and effector CD8+ T cells in all three cancers, tumor tissues had most pre_exhausted and exhausted CD8+ T cells, while there was no regular pattern in normal tissues. The difference in normal tissue may be related to tissue specificity. Since the most of effector cells existed in peripheral blood in three cancers and there showed no difference among peripheral blood, normal and tumor tissues (Figure S1), we compared pre_exhausted or exhausted cells in tumor tissues with effector cells in peripheral blood to explore the exhaustion associated mechanism.

In the pre_exhausted vs. effector comparison, there were 73 DEGs existed in all three cancers, 71 of whom were in the same regulatory status (Figure 2B) including the known exhaustion marker PDCD1 (programmed cell deathLJ1) and CD27 (CD27 molecule). The top 15 significant GO and KEGG terms based on the p value were shown as Figure 2D and 2E. We could find that most terms were related to the production of chemokine and response to tumor necrosis factor which can facilitate the immunoregulation effects[42, 43]. Apart from that, we found other terms associated with immunity. For example, there were 7 upregulated DEGs, including GPR171 (G protein-coupled receptor 171), TNFAIP3 (TNF alpha induced protein 3), CD96 (CD96 molecule), SAMSN1 (SAM domain, SH3 domain and nuclear localization signals 1, also known as HACS1), ZFP36 (ZFP36 ring finger protein), DUSP1 (dual specificity phosphatase 1), PDCD1, enriched in the negative regulation of immune system process.

In the exhausted vs. effector comparison, there were 159 DEGs existed in all three cancers (Figure 2C) including multiple known exhaustion markers such as PDCD1, HAVCR2 (hepatitis A virus cellular receptor 2, also known as T cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3, Tim-3), TIGIT (T cell immunoglobulin and ITIM domain), TOX (thymocyte selection associated high mobility group box). The top 15 significant GO and KEGG terms were shown as Figure 2F and 2G. Several terms related to glycolytic process which was the main metabolism pathway of T cells to sustain effector function after antigen encounter[44] were enriched. Besides, pathways related to virus infection, NF-kappa B signaling pathway were also enriched. Correspondingly, there were 14 DEGs, including 9 upregulated genes PDCD1, TIGIT, HAVCR2, CCL3 (C-C Motif Chemokine Ligand 3), DUSP1, TNFAIP3, PAG1 (phosphoprotein membrane anchor with glycosphingolipid microdomains 1), CDK6 (cyclin dependent kinase 6), SAMSN1, participated in the negative regulation of immune system process.

Interestingly, we found several common terms between pre-exhausted and exhausted cells, including cell chemotaxis, leukocyte migration, chemokine signaling pathway, cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction and so on. Additionally, the pathways existed only in pre_exhausted cells included cytolytic granule and interleukin-2 production, which could represent the effector functions of CD8+ T cells, showing the features in transition of pre_exhausted cells. The exhausted cells most enriched in the negative regulation of cytokine production, showing the further dysfunction of T cells. The results of functional analysis of DEGs was shown in Table S2 and S3.

It was obvious that as the cell state changed from pre-exhausted to exhausted cells, the number of DEGs also increased, especially the exhaustion associated markers. Moreover, the expression level of the common markers increased from pre_exhausted to exhausted cells (Figure S2). Interestingly, we compared the genes existed in both pre_exhausted and exhausted cells and filtered those with greater fold change in the exhausted cells (Figure 3A). We found SAMSN1 was dysregulated during exhaustion state, which participated in the negative regulation of immune system process in both pre_exhausted and exhausted cells, showed poor survival in the validation based on TCGA data except for CRC (Figure 3B).

Moreover, we performed the PPI network analysis of all 193 DEGs in pre_exhausted or exhausted vs. effector comparison and found seven significant functional modules (Figure 3C and Figure S3). The module with the highest score consisted of 15 nodes and 101 edges, in which CD69 was the seed gene and linked with 26 genes.

To further explore the factors affecting the expression of DEGs, we integrated the transcription factor (TF) from CHEA, ENCODE and TRRUST databases, and found the TFs with dysregulated expression were ATM (ATM serine/threonine kinase), RORA (RAR Related Orphan Receptor A), TBX21 (T-Box Transcription Factor 21), JUNB (JunB Proto-Oncogene, AP-1 Transcription Factor Subunit), RBPJ (Recombination Signal Binding Protein For Immunoglobulin Kappa J Region), TNFAIP3 and JUN (Jun proto-oncogene, AP-1 transcription factor subunit) (Figure 3D, Figure S4).

ATM, RORA, TBX21, which were the TFs of DUSP1, TUBA1B (tubulin alpha 1b), CXCR3 (C-X-C motif chemokine receptor 3) and IFNG (Interferon Gamma) respectively, were significantly downregulated in exhausted cells. Among the upregulated factors, JUN regulated more than 100 DEGs and may play a critical role in the occurrence and development of exhausted CD8+ T cells. Therefore, these TFs may lead to the dysregulated expression of DEGs during T cell exhaustion.

### Sub-cluster of exhausted cells

Exhausted T cell populations are heterogeneous[45]. In the further classification of exhausted cells, the top 1500 genes with highest variance were used to divide exhausted cells into 2 sub-clusters in CRC, the top 3000 genes in HCC to 2 sub-clusters, and the top 500 genes in NSCLC to 2 sub-clusters (Figure 5A, 5B, 5C). In the visualization of exhausted sub-clusters, the distribution of exhausted cells of each patient tended to appear in one sub-cluster(Figure 5D, 5E, 5F), for instance, P0413, who was diagnosed at stage III of CRC, mostly exhausted in sub-cluster I, while P0909, who was diagnosed at stage I of CRC, mostly exhausted in sub-cluster II, illustrating the individual differences.

## Discussion

T cell exhaustion is a dysfunctional state and is possible to be used in immunotherapy because of its reversibility[47]. Combining with single-cell RNA sequencing, which can facilitate detecting the transcriptome on the level of single cell[48], we can shed light on the complication of tumor-infiltrating T cells. In order to study the common mechanisms of multiple cancers, we performed transcriptomic analysis of single CD8+ T cells isolated from three cancers including CRC, HCC, NSCLC. As expected, we identified some known genes related to T cell exhaustion, such as PDCD1, HAVCR2, TIGIT, TOX. Moreover, two vital genes CD69 and JUN in the interaction network and the gene SAMSN1 which highly expressed in the exhausted cells were also identified. Furthermore, the distribution of exhausted T cells for each patient tended to appear in one exhausted sub-cluster, which indicated that the exhaustion mechanism in each individual might have homogeneity.

In the functional enrichment analysis, several DEGs played a negative regulation of immune system process and led to the pre-exhausted or exhausted state of CD8+ T cells. To be specific, the gene PDCD1 encodes an inhibitory receptor and functions as an immune checkpoint. In the current study, PDCD1 was significantly upregulated in the pre_exhausted and exhausted cells, which was consistent with previous studies[49, 50]. The upregulation of PDCD1 played a crucial role in the regulation of CD8+ T cell exhaustion and blockade of the interaction between PDCD1 and its ligand PD-L1 could restore CD8+ effector function[49, 50]. It has been reported that the combination of PDCD1 and PD-L1 can recruit phosphatases[51], dampen TCR signaling[52], inhibit the PI3K signaling pathway[53], suppress proliferation and cytokine production and finally result in CD8+ T cell exhaustion. Besides, the gene HAVCR2, which encodes an inhibitory receptor, was upregulated in the exhausted cells in the present study. The previous studies indicated that the binding of its ligand Galectin 9 can mediate the release of BAT3 via tyrosine phosphorylation in the HAVCR2 tail and lead to inhibit the PI3K signaling pathway[54, 55]. In the models of mouse and human tumors, using the antibodies of HAVCR2 can rescue the exhausted T cells[56, 57], revealing that HAVCR2 was associated with exhaustion of CD8+ T cell. Moreover, the inhibitory receptor encoded by gene TIGIT acts as T cell intrinsic inhibitor with its ligand CD155 by restraining effector cell activation and proliferation, leading to the decreased effector function and cytokine production[58], and the knockdown of TIGIT can revert the decreased production of cytokines[59]. TIGIT was upregulated in the exhausted cells according to our study, showing its potential key role in the exhaustion of CD8+ T cells. Except for the inhibitory gene, the critical regulator of exhausted CD8+ T cells[60], TOX, was upregulated in the exhausted cells in the current study. As previously reported, TOX-knockout cells showed low expression of PDCD1, ENTPD1, TIGIT, HAVCR2 as well as high expression of the transcription factors genes TCF7, LEF1 and ID3[60, 61], while TOX-overexpressed cells resulted in reduced TNF and IFNγ expression as well as increased PDCD1, TIGIT, LAG3 levels[62], which supported that TOX could drive T cell exhaustion. The genes described above were all upregulated in pre-exhausted or exhausted T cells, which could result in exhaustion of CD8+ T cell.

Apart from that, we found some other crucial genes. CD69 is an early activation marker of T lymphocytes[63] and acts as a negative regulator of anti-tumor responses[64]. In the PPI network analysis, CD69 was a hub gene of the module with the highest score and was upregulated in both pre_exhausted and exhausted cells. CD69 can mediate the cell retention via the interaction with S1PR1 (Sphingosine-1-Phosphate Receptor 1) which acts as a central mediator of lymphocyte output[65]. CD69 was upregulated and S1PR1 was downregulated significantly in the exhausted cells according to our study (Figure 3A). Thus we deduced that upregulated CD69 binds with downregulated S1PR1, leading to the impairment of lymphocyte output during an immune response[65]. The upregulated CD69 expressed on tumor-infiltrating T cells might interact with unknown ligands in the tumor to lead to the persistent antigen stimulation, which resulted in T cell exhaustion with the overexpression of inhibitory genes such as PDCD1 and HAVCR2[66]. In the murine breast cancer model, Cd69-/- mice can reduce tumor growth and metastasis, increase the number of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, enhance IFNγ production and attenuate the T cell exhaustion[67]. In light of these researches, the upregulation of CD69 and downregulation of S1PR1 might promote the development of T cell exhaustion. Additionally, JUN was a proto-oncogene and involved in the JNK signaling pathway which played a vital role in the proliferation and differentiation of CD8+ T cells[68]. Based on our findings, JUN was upregulated in both pre_exhausted and exhausted cells and regulated over 100 DEGs. According to the previous researches, JUN was a positive regulator of T cell activation[69, 70] and can promote T cell proliferation and the production of cytokines[71]. It is reported that one of the reasons of T cell exhaustion is the continuous stimulation after the initial cell activation[45, 72], and the upregulation of JUN both in the pre_exhausted and exhausted cells showed the lasting role to regulate cell activation, which might lead to T cell exhaustion finally. The ENCODE database showed that JUN is a transcription factor of PDCD1 and TIGIT, thus we speculated that JUN may affect T cell exhaustion through transcriptional regulation of the exhaustion markers. However, the mechanisms of T cell exhaustion regulated by JUN needed further study. In addition, SAMSN1 is an immunoinhibitory adaptor and the knockdown of SAMSN1 can enhance the adaptive immunity in mice[73]. In the comparison of the common DEGs between pre_exhausted or exhausted and effector cells, SAMSN1 showed greater upregulation in the exhausted cells and was associated with poor prognosis in HCC and NSCLC based on TCGA data. The expression of SAMSN1 increased in B cells once stimulated by IL-4 through both PI3k/PKC/NF-κB and STAT6 pathway[74, 75]. SAMSN1 might be associated with T cell exhaustion according to our results, however, further study is needed to explore the exact relationship between SAMSN1 and T cell exhaustion.

By integrating above previous researches and our results, we summarized the key genes related to T cell exhaustion in Figure 4, including the upregulated genes of CD69, PDCD1, HAVCR2, TIGIT, TOX and JUN, as well as a downregulated gene S1PR1. The more specific mechanisms needed to be explored in the future study.

In the further classification of exhausted cluster to explore the heterogeneity, we found that each patient tended to have one exhausted sub-cluster. Although the sub-clusters of each cancer may be regulated by different hub genes, we found that these sub-clusters were dysregulated in the cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction. Different kinds of cytokine played various and complex effects on T cells, for instance, the decreased expression of effector cytokines such as IL-2 and TNF-α as well as the increased expression of inhibitory cytokines such as IL-10 and TGF-β were reported to be associated with T cell exhaustion[76]. Additionally, the upregulation of pathways in cancer was consistent with the immune evasion of tumor caused by T cell exhaustion[77]. What’s more, there were two downregulated pathways including natural killer cell mediated cytotoxicity and ribosome. There is a reciprocal relationship between NK and T cells, and most NK cell receptors are found on T cells[78]. Besides, there are similarities of NK cell and CD8+ T cell on the mechanisms of cell killing. They can release cytotoxic granules to the target cell such as tumor cells, and induce programmed cell death[79]. The dysfunctional cytotoxicity might attenuate the killing effector of CD8+ T cells on tumor cells.

Additionally, the pathway of ribosome was also downregulated. Ribosome were essential for production in all living cells and regulated the cell growth and proliferation[80, 81]. During T cell exhaustion, the cells were characterized by the dysfunctional activity and bioenergetic deficiencies[82], which might be related to the damaged functions of ribosome. In addition to the universal pathways, there existed different pathways owing to each sub-cluster. In the CRC_S1 pathways, wnt signaling pathway was upregulated in our study. It has been reported that wnt signaling pathway contributes to tumor progression[83] and its blockade can improve the generation of effector cells[84], thus the upregulation of wnt signaling pathway might contribute to T cell exhaustion and facilitate the growth of tumor; In the NSCLC_S1 pathways, the upregulated apoptosis may lead to the impaired function of T cells[85].

In the current study, we integrated three cancers and found some key genes associated with T cell exhaustion, including the known markers PDCD1, HAVCR2, TIGIT, TOX. In the gene interaction network, we found significant genes CD69 and JUN. Besides, we firstly identified a potential exhaustion associated gene, SAMSN1, which enriched in the negative regulation of immunity and showed poor survival. We summarized the putative interrelated mechanisms of above key genes identified in this study by integrating the previous knowledge. Furthermore, we explored the heterogeneous and preference of exhausted CD8+ T cells in each patient and found only one exhausted sub-cluster existed in most of patients, especially in CRC and HCC. Our study provides a universal molecular mechanism on T cell exhaustion and a new way for functional research of single-cell RNA sequencing data, which might promote the further realization of immunotherapy.
