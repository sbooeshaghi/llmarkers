## Abstract

AbstractThe interconnection between wound healing and cancer has long been recognized, as epitomized by the expression “cancer is a wound that does not heal”. However, the impact of inducing a wound, such as those from biopsies, on the progression of established tumors remains largely unknown. In this study, we examine the transcriptional heterogeneity of cancer cells in human basal cell carcinoma (BCC) and identify a wound response-associated gene program as a prominent feature of invasive cancer cells. We find that invasive cancer cells are prevalent in ulcerated BCC tumors and exhibit a distinctive spatial organization. To explore the causal relationship between wounding and cancer progression, we compare tumors at baseline and one week after biopsy. Our results reveal that biopsy collection locally induces a transcriptional switch in cancer cells from an indolent to an invasive state. Notably, this progression is coupled with the reprogramming of cancer-associated fibroblasts, which adopt a transcriptional state characteristic of therapy-resistant BCC. This study provides evidence that wounding triggers invasive progression of established human tumors and warrants further research on the potentially harmful effects of biopsies and wound-inducing treatments.

## Introduction

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC), the most frequent cancer in humans, displays extensive inter- and intra-tumoral morphological variability, with histologic patterns that can be classified within a spectrum from nodular to infiltrative1,2. The progression of BCC from a nodular to an infiltrative pattern, as typically observed, is most notably marked by a significant increase in invasiveness – a key hallmark of cancer3 – and associated with growing resistance to standard-of-care therapies4,5. The molecular triggers driving nodular to infiltrative transition in BCC are however largely unknown. Previous independent studies showed that infiltrative BCCs are not characterized by distinct somatic genomic alterations6,7 suggesting that invasive progression might be primarily driven by non-genetic changes. Consistently, we have previously shown that nodular to infiltrative transition is a fast-inducible phenomenon associated with progressive transcriptional changes in cancer cells8,9. Here, we aimed to investigate in more detail the association between the transcriptional heterogeneity of BCC cancer cells and invasive progression. We identified a wound response-associated gene program as the most distinctive feature of invasive cancer cells, leading us to investigate the impact of wounding on human BCC.

## Results

### Cancer cell-intrinsic transcriptional programs underpin invasion patterns in human basal cell carcinoma

Human BCC tumors, classified histopathologically into nodular or infiltrative types, display extensive intratumoral morphological heterogeneity (Fig. 1A). We hypothesized that this intratumoral morphological variability is reflected at the transcriptional level, by distinct patterns of gene expression programs. To identify the gene programs underlying this transcriptional heterogeneity, we first performed an unsupervised meta-analysis of single-cell RNA-seq data from BCC tumors of 11 patients (Fig. 1B and Fig. S1; Table S1) (GSE1819079; E-MTAB-1308510). Using a gene program discovery approach based on non-negative matrix factorization (see Methods), we identified seven robust gene metaprograms (MP 1 to 7) that consistently explain the cancer cell transcriptional heterogeneity across patients and algorithm initializations (Fig. 1C; Table S2). Comparison of the identified MPs with those from the comprehensive pan-cancer analysis by Gavish et al.11 revealed that MP1 and MP6 correspond to previously identified cell cycle and (heat-shock) stress response MPs, respectively (Fig. 1D and Methods). The remaining MPs did not show significant association with previously reported cancer cell programs. Examination of the most influential genes and gene set enrichment analysis suggested potential roles in cell radial projection for MP2 (LAMB1 and LRP8); in epidermal differentiation for MP3 (CLDN4, CALML5, LGALS7B and KRT75); and in epidermal response to wounding for MP7 (TIMP1, CHI3L1, and IFITM3) (Fig. 1D; Fig S2A).

We hypothesized that distinct MP activation patterns might be underpinning invasive progression. To address this, we took advantage of previous results where we identified, using GeoMx spatial transcriptomics, gene signatures of highly-infiltrative and highly-nodular BCC tumors9. These signatures allowed us to calculate a nodular-to-infiltrative (NI) score for each single cancer cell, ranging from -1 in fully nodular to 1 in fully infiltrative BCC cancer cells (see Methods). Next, we examined whether individual MP activities were associated at the single-cell level with the nodular or infiltrative pattern by examining their correlation with the NI score across cells and samples (Fig. 1E). MP2 displayed the highest negative correlation. A characteristic feature of nodular BCC is a radially-oriented architecture of the basal layer1,2, with apical projection of the primary cilium12, suggesting a potential role of MP2 in nodular pattern formation or maintenance. Interestingly, MP2 also included LGR5 (Fig. 1D), a gene marker for hair follicle stem cells with rapid adaptive capacity during wound healing13. In contrast, we found that MP7 displayed the highest positive correlation with NI score (Fig. 1E). This correlation was consistent across individual patients (Fig. S2B) suggesting an association of this metaprogram with the infiltrative pattern. Using a multivariate linear model, we verified that MP7 was indeed the strongest predictor of the infiltrative score (Fig. 1F, see Methods). In sum, we identified cancer cell-intrinsic gene expression programs characterizing BCC cancer cell heterogeneity and found that a gene metaprogram related to the epidermal wound response (MP7) is most strongly associated with BCC invasive progression.

### Cancer cell-intrinsic gene programs associated with invasive BCC are spatially organized

To assess whether MPs act independently or in a coordinated fashion, we evaluated the co-expression patterns of MPs in single BCC cancer cells (Fig. 2A). As expected, MP7 activity was most negatively correlated with MP2, a nodular-associated (infiltrative-antagonistic) program. Conversely, the activity of MP7 was most positively correlated with epidermal differentiation program MP3 (Fig. 2A). In fact, these two MPs shared a fraction of genes (Fig. S3 A-C), including some genes previously shown to be up-regulated in infiltrative cancer cells, such as KRT6A, INHBA and S100A79. However, when considering MP-specific genes, such as Tissue Inhibitor of Metalloproteinases 1 (TIMP1) for MP7 and Claudin 4 (CLDN4) for MP3 (Fig. 1D and Fig. S3C), we found a largely mutually exclusive activation pattern, magnified within the infiltrative, KRT6A-expressing tumor cells (Fig. 2B, Fig. S3B). This suggested that there might be two functionally distinct cancer cell populations in the tumor infiltrative niche, possibly with distinct spatial organization. To test this, we assembled a collection of BCC tumor samples, displaying nodular and infiltrative patterns. We first verified that tumor morphological complexity (see Methods) – a hallmark of infiltrative BCC – was higher in selected infiltrative compared to nodular areas (Fig. 2C). Infiltrative areas also displayed increased expression level of the infiltrative marker KRT6A, measured by RNA FISH (Fig. S3D). As predicted, staining for MP7- and MP3-specific markers (TIMP1 and CLDN4 respectively) revealed a distinctive spatial organization of the infiltrative niche (Fig. 2D). TIMP1-high tumor cells were found exclusively in the infiltrative niches and predominantly in contact with the basement membrane zone (BMZ) (i.e. with highest expression at an average distance of 0 cells from the BMZ; Fig. 2 E-F). Instead, CLDN4-high tumor cells were found in both nodular and infiltrative niches, but with a distinct spatial organization in the infiltrative niches. Whereas CLDN4 was expressed in nodular cores, CLDN4-high tumor cells in infiltrative niches were predominantly found at 1-cell distance from the BMZ, in close contact with TIMP1-high cells (Fig. 2 E-F).

Altogether, we found that MP7 and MP3 are activated in adjacent populations of cancer cells. In particular, MP7 cells tend to surround MP3 cells, forming a spatial architecture specifically found within the infiltrative niche of BCC.

### Ulcerated BCC tumors display invasive features

Injury to the epithelial barrier, or ulceration, is a salient feature of BCCs1,14,15. Although tumor ulceration has been associated with poor survival in metastatic skin cancers16,17, the association of ulceration with invasive features in BCC tumors has not been explored. Given the presumed role of both MP7-specific genes (TIMP118 and IFITM319), and MP3-specific genes (CLDN420, CALML521, LGALS7B22) in the epidermal response to injury, we hypothesized that these MPs would be upregulated and embedded in an infiltrative morphology in ulcerated compared to non-ulcerated tumor areas. To investigate this, we unbiasedly extracted BCC samples classified as nodular and ulcerated based on histopathological reports from our tissue biobank (see Methods). We found that in nearly all samples, despite prominent nodular characteristics, ulcerated areas displayed increased invasiveness, reflected by higher tumor complexity (Fig. S4A-B) and upregulated expression of KRT6A in tumor cells (Fig. S4C-E). Notably, we found a strong upregulation of TIMP1 (MP7 specific marker) and CLDN4 (MP3 specific marker) at 0- and 1-cell distance from the BMZ, respectively (Fig. S4F-G), recapitulating the characteristic spatial organization of infiltrative BCC. We refer to this transcriptional and spatial tumoral pattern as the “wound-related invasive” (WRI) architecture and established it as a hallmark of ulcerated BCC tumors.

### Tumor wounding triggers invasive progression in human BCC

Invasive BCC tumors may ulcerate as a consequence of outgrowing their blood supply. At the same time, it is an intriguing possibility that wounding might be a driving factor for further invasiveness, establishing a self-sustaining positive feedback loop for tumor progression. To test whether wounding could trigger nodular to infiltrative transition in human BCC, we designed a longitudinal experiment to compare tumors taken before and after inducing a wound by punch biopsy. We selected four patients with non-ulcerated, non-biopsied, suspected nodular BCC tumors. For each patient, we collected three samples: i) from baseline (first, diagnostic biopsy), and 1 week later ii) from the previously biopsied, wounded site, and iii) from a distant, unwounded site in the same tumor (Fig. 3A). All samples were subjected to RNA FISH and H&E staining, and two of them, to scRNA-seq. Notably, we found that one week after punch biopsy-induced wounding, the wounded tumor sites displayed a transition from a nodular to an infiltrative morphology, as reflected by increased morphological complexity and higher expression of KRT6A in tumor cells compared to baseline or unwounded tumor sites (Fig. 3B and Fig. S5A). Moreover, compared to the baseline or the unwounded sites, cancer cells in the site of induced wound significantly upregulated the MP7 gene program (Fig. 3C). Notably, the range of MP7 activation upon wounding was similar to the MP7 range observed between non-invasive and invasive BCC tumors (Fig. 3C, Fig. S5B). Consistently, individual MP7-specific marker genes, such as TIMP1, CHI3L1, and IFITM3 were significantly up-regulated upon wounding while several MP2-specific marker genes were down-regulated upon wounding in the two patients for which scRNA-seq data were generated (Fig. S5B-D). In fact, MP2 and MP7 marker genes were among the most differentially expressed genes in the wounded vs. baseline or unwounded conditions (Fig. 3D and Fig. S5D), indicating that these gene programs indeed captured the most prominent transcriptional changes that are induced in cancer cells upon wounding. In contrast, MP3 did not show a robust modulation upon wounding (Fig. S5B-C), consistent with our previous results showing that the spatial organization of this program, rather than its global activation, is associated with invasive progression. To investigate the spatial organization of wounded tumors, we stained the samples for TIMP1 and CLDN4. Remarkably, we found that tumor wounding locally induced the characteristic spatial organization of the WRI architecture, as reflected by the induced expression of both TIMP1 and CLDN4 at the basal and suprabasal layers, respectively (Fig. 3E-F). Altogether, our data show that injury locally triggers a cancer cell switch from a non-invasive to an invasive state. This observation supports the role of wounding as a driver of invasive progression in human BCC.

### Wounding induces a cancer-associated fibroblast state characteristic of invasive and advanced BCC

Our results revealed a transcriptional switch and spatial reorganization of cancer cells that is characteristic of both chronically invasive BCC tumors and (initially) non-invasive BCC tumors that were wounded a week before. To investigate whether the interconnection between these two conditions extends beyond cancer cells, we next examined the changes induced by wounding on the tumor microenvironment.

Compared to baseline, wounded samples displayed a ∼10 fold increase in the relative abundance of cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) and immune cells (including T cells, monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells), as well as a modest increase of endothelial cells and a decrease of pericytes (Fig. 4A). These results indicate wound-triggered inflammation and fibroblast expansion. In addition to proportional changes, tumor-infiltrating stromal and immune cells displayed altered transcriptional profiles in response to wounding, especially for CAFs (Fig. S6A). CAFs upregulated the expression of metalloproteases (MMP3 and MMP1), glycolysis-related genes (GAPDH, ENO1, BASP1, PKM, and TPI1), contractile protein genes (TPM2), and cellular stress genes (NFKBIA and IER3) (Fig. 4B and Table S3). These results indicate that BCC tumor wounding induces, within one week, a distinct CAF state characterized by profound changes in metabolism, contractility, and extracellular matrix remodeling.

The WRI switch in cancer cells, captured by MP7, appears in nodular (non-invasive) BCC tumors one week after wounding (Fig. 3) and is observed permanently in infiltrative (invasive) BCC tumors (Fig. 3C; Fig. S6B). We thus wondered whether the acute, wound-induced CAF reprogramming is also a shared feature of infiltrative BCC tumors. To test this hypothesis, we constructed a signature from the genes differentially expressed by CAFs upon wounding (“wound-responding” CAFs; Table S3), and used it to query transcriptional profiles of CAFs in our retrospective BCC patients cohort (see Methods). The “wound-responding” CAF score was significantly higher in invasive BCC compared to non-invasive BCC or normal adjacent skin samples (Fig. 4C, Fig. S6C, D). Consistently, it was also significantly higher in intratumoral stromal spots within infiltrative niches compared to stromal spots within nodular niches of non-previously biopsied tumors (Fig. 4D). Notably, human samples from advanced, hedgehog inhibitor-resistant BCC23, and from cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC) – a more aggressive, keratinocyte-derived cancer type24 – displayed the highest “wound-responding” CAF scores and cancer cell-intrinsic MP7 activation (Fig. 4C, Fig. S6B, see Methods). These data indicate that the CAF reprogramming induced acutely upon biopsy largely overlaps with the differences observed between non-invasive and invasive BCC tumors (further exacerbated in advanced BCC) and suggest that the invasive alterations triggered by induced wounding may persist over time and lead to poorer prognosis.

## Discussion

There are remarkable similarities between cancer hallmarks and wound healing3,27. While the effect of wound-induced inflammation on early stages of tumor formation has been well documented28–34, it is unclear how wounding can impact cancer cell progression in established tumors.

In this study, we showed that wounding induced by biopsy collection in BCC patients produces a transcriptional switch in cancer cells from a non-invasive to an invasive state. We found that invasive cancer cells are prevalent in ulcerated BCC tumors and that they are organized in a “wound-related invasive” (WRI) spatial architecture with characteristic expression of gene programs. In particular, MP7 and MP3 include genes implicated in epithelial cell migration (e.g. TNC, TIMP1, MMP1, ITGAV, ITGB6) and epidermal barrier restoration (e.g. CLDN4, DSG1, KRT1, KRT77, SPRR1B) respectively, and display a spatial coordination – at the basal and suprabasal layers - that reflects the specific architecture of the wound migrating edge35–38. In line with these findings, wound epithelial migrating cells and invasive cancer cells share the expression of ITGB6 (Integrin Subunit Beta 6) and ITGAV (Integrin alpha-V), coding for the fibronectin receptor αVβ6 implicated in adhesion to newly-formed, fibrotic stroma39,40. Consistently, BCC invasion is coupled with a CAF state that exhibits predominant extracellular matrix-remodeling features which, similarly to dermal fibroblasts upon tissue injury, may sustain invasive cancer cells by providing a provisional matrix41,42.

The identification of a wound response-related transcriptional program in cancer cells (MP7) that is active in unwounded invasive BCC tumors and switched on in non-invasive BCC tumors one week after wounding, raises the possibility of potential common regulators. The inflammatory response is triggered almost immediately upon tissue injury43–45, and emerges as a potential mechanism. We indeed observe early and broad immune infiltration upon tumor injury, coupled with the expression of inflammatory response genes in cancer cells (like the interferon response gene IFITM3). Wound healing, however, relies on redundant pathways for efficient wound closure46, and thus other mechanisms may be at play. The results raise the question of why the process is not self-limited in cancer, in contrast to normal tissue, and further insights may come from the investigation of discrepancies - rather than commonalities - between cancer and the late-stage of tissue repair.

Beyond the identification of tumor ulceration as a potential clinical indicator of an infiltrative BCC, our data raise questions about the benefit-risk balance of wounding procedures in the clinical practice. Biopsies, the mainstay for diagnosing and grading cancers, as well as physical (surgery, radiotherapy, cryotherapy) or chemical (chemotherapy, photodynamic therapy) therapies induce tissue injury. Our data support a potential deleterious effect of these procedures as they may trigger local progression of residual cancer cells. This observation might explain the noted disparity between histological evaluations of biopsy samples and subsequent excisions in various solid epithelial cancers, including BCC47–53. It could also elucidate why tumors that recur after wounding treatments often exhibit increased invasiveness compared to the initial primary tumors54,55. Notably, infiltrative BCC display increased resistance to standard-of-care therapies compared to nodular BCC4,5. In this context, an important consideration is whether therapies that induce wounds, in addition to promoting invasion, might trigger resistance of residual cancer cells, as also suggested by clinical studies56. Because of the conserved wound response in cancer, we anticipate that future research in wound healing will contribute to the identification of new therapeutic targets to prevent cancer progression.

### Study limitations

Due to ethical considerations preventing long delays between diagnosis and treatment, our study does not cover the full dynamics of alterations induced upon wounding, especially their long-term maintenance. Thus, the evidence supporting long-term consequences of the cancer cells transcriptional reprogramming induced by tumor wounding are indirect. Second, the deconvolution of cancer cell heterogeneity is based on a limited cohort of naïve BCC patients eligible for surgical treatment, and, as such, may not fully capture the heterogeneity of advanced BCC.
